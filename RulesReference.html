This chapter outlines the baseline mechanics that Bonfire uses for its systems.

When to Make Checks

If you’re an old hand at RPGs, this might seem like a silly section, but it is something to think about and it something that new GMs should learn.

When do you actually roll dice? And what do those dice do? Let’s take this one question at a time.

When to Roll Dice

Think about an RPG session like a conversation: there’s a back and forth between GM and players and a back and forth between players and other players.

But, unlike a normal conversation, there’s a point where dice are rolled, and the results read.

Very simply, it looks like the flowchart below.

99% of all RPG sessions will follow this flow, and those that don’t, only vary in minor ways, which we’ll talk about.

This is an extremely in-depth subject, but this section is primarily for new GMs so I’m only going to cover the basics.

Meaningful Failure

I wanted to take a small aside to talk about meaningful failure because, if there isn’t a chance of it it is the GM’s prerogative to simply wave the check, saying that the players succeed.

This is just a matter of streamlining the session: if failure doesn’t affect anything, there’s no point spending time on it.

This is usually meant in a mechanic sense (getting wounded) but it can also be in a story sense (it has no effect on the events going forward).

Session Critical

This is a common mistake for new GMs.

If you need a check to result in a certain outcome you should rewrite the session you had planned.

An example I see all the time is GMs putting the adventure hook behind a check. A man has a job for the players but will only give it to them if they can demonstrate that they have the skills.

But they fail all the rolls. The GM stops a second and then gives them the hook anyway.

If that’s the way the session is written; go back to the drawing board because it makes everything seem faked and contrived.

For me, personally, it also inflicts doubt in my actual agency as a player. Did I really beat the villain because of what I did or did the GM just fudge the outcome because that’s what they wanted?

What Do Those Dice Mean?

Something a lot of GMs don’t consider is the idea of structures within a game system.

There’s the resolution mechanic (how do I determine success or failure) but what that resolution mechanic means is based in the context of what is going on.

For instance, rolling an attack roll in a combat means you’re trying to physically hit someone or something with your weapon with the intent to kill or subdue while rolling to attack in an interrogation scene is more about striking to scare rather than wound.

That context is often nested in a structure. For instance, the combat is nested in the “Combat” structure which determines how often that attacks can be made while the interrogation scene doesn’t work when you run it like a combat.

But a lot of games don’t give you structures to run different types of scenes: they don’t give you the structure that helps you (and your players) play that interrogation scene out.

Bonfire isn’t going to make that mistake and so the next 3 chapters are going to be 3 different structures that you can use for 3 very different types of scenes.

As you get familiar with them, you should play around with them; tweak them, add to them, remove parts from them, and combine them, but only do so once you’ve mastered them.

Doing so will open up and depth and breathe to roleplaying that will astound you and will give your players the tools to play some truly unique characters.

Structure Overview

As mentioned, Bonfire has 3 that you can use. Here is a quick overview:

Confrontations

Confrontations represent battles of wills: either with another person in an argument or with yourself at a moment of crisis.

Confrontations are largely about emotional and subjective belief in the world.

Combat

Combat represent a physical struggle between two or more fighters. Most likely this struggle is to the death, with the main tools being weapons, shields, and armor.

Challenges

Challenges are for everything else that doesn't fit in the first two structures and are often called "Skill Challenges" due to their reliance on Skills.

Challenges are considered the default of the structures since they're highly flexible and can cover a lot of ground. In fact, if you're not comfortable with using one of the other systems (or you're running a campaign that doesn't focus on combat, parse), you could easily use Challenges as a workable substitute.

Making Checks

When you need to determine the outcome of a question or action, you’re going to make a Check.

You’ll do this by rolling a d20! and then adding the relevant Check Modifier and Rank.

The opposition will also roll a d20! and add any relevant modifiers on their side.

The side with the high total wins the Check. What that means is dependent on the framework.

Untrained Skills Suites

Untrained Skill Suites (marked with a U) represent Skill Suites that your character has very little or no knowledge about.

When making a Check using either that Skill Suite or an Advanced Skill in that Skill Suite that you have no Ranks in, you instead roll it on a dice size 1 size smaller (normally, a d12!).

Untrained Combat Skill Suites

If you’re Untrained in Melee Weapons, you shrink your Attack and Defense Die by 1 size.

If you’re Untrained in Ranged Weapons, you shrink your Attack by 1 size and your Recovery is twice as long.

If you’re Untrained in Shields, while using one, you shrink your Defense Die by 1 size.

If you’re Untrained in Armor, while wearing a set, you shrink your Defense Die by 1 size.

So, if you’re Untrained in Melee Weapons, Armor, and Shields, and you go into battle wearing Leather Armor and wielding a buckler and longsword, you’d be rolling a d12! to attack (d20! - 1 dice size) and rolling a d8 to defend (d20! - 3 dice sizes).

Check Modifiers

Check Modifiers come from the stat that most influences that Check.

So, for instance, if your character is trying to lift a heavy boulder, they’ll add their Strength Check Mod while trying to convince someone might use Presence (or Intellect, if they’re trying to be logical about it).

A lot of times, the Check Mod is already determined by what you’re rolling. So, for instance, Attack rolls already have a set Check Mod; same with History Checks, although GMs might alter that based on the circumstances.

Rank

Ranks represent knowledge. Ranks are used in several different places; Skills, and Characteristics. We’ll only go into generalities below about when to apply them to a Check but details will be covered in their specific chapters.

Skills

Skills represent broad areas of knowledge (represented as Skill Suites) or specific areas of knowledge (represented by Advanced Skills). Advanced Skills are ‘nested’ in Skill Suites.

When making a Skill Check, if you don’t have the specific Advanced Skill, you use the Rank of the mother Skill Suite instead.

For instance, if Randy has a Rank 3 in Streetwise and a Rank 5 in Current Affairs, while George has a Rank 2 in Streetwise but hasn’t purchased Current Affairs, when the GM calls for a Current Affairs Check, Randy adds a +5 to the roll (the Rank of his Current Affairs), while George adds a +2 to his roll (the Rank of his Streetwise since he doesn’t have Current Affairs).

More information can be found in Chapter 5:Skills.

Characteristics

Characteristics is the broad, category term for anything related to your character’s personality. This is broken down into Convictions, Devotions, Descriptions, and Reputation.

These will be added to Checks that specifically test your character’s willpower and drive as well as inner conflicts they might have.

More information can be found in Chapter 1: Step-by-Step Overview.

Exploding Dice

You’ll notice an exclamation mark (!) behind a lot of dice notations. This marks the dice as exploding, which means that should the maximum of that dice is rolled, you get to roll it again, this time with a - 1, and add what you rolled to the total.

This continues for as long as you roll the maximum dice, however, the - 1 always stays a -1 one.

So, if you roll a 3d8! for damage, you get a 5, 8, 8. Since you rolled the maximum value on two of your dice, you roll those dice again, and roll an 8, and 5, giving you a subtotal of 32 ( 5 + 8 + 8 + 8 - 1 + 5 - 1 ) but since you rolled another 8, you get to roll that dice again, and you get a 1, netting you a 0 ( 1 - 1 ).

There are two things you should be aware of: 1d100! explode to a 1d20!, while 1d20! explode to a d6!.

The second thing is that explosion dice don’t add additional dice to the number. So, in the example above, all that damage is only counted as having been done with 3 dice (instead of the 5 that were actually rolled).

Round Against the Player

Unless otherwise stated, you round against the players.

Stress

Working under pressure can get to you. When your crew mates are relying on you to pull out a win, it can mess with your head. Your character can become stressed under these circumstances and stress can later manifest in odd behaviors and penalties.

Characters can gain Stress in a lot of ways and those are discussed in their various sections - taking damage, making Skill Checks, etc. - the only one we’ll go over here is from acting against your character’s Characteristics, especially when not playing them would be a huge benefit.

Simply put, if the GM can inflict 1d4! Stress if your character goes against one of their Characteristics or a Flaw's Severity if it's a Flaw.

Most of the time, this will be the GM tempting the player: "I know your character is Greedy: I think he'd want to see if he couldn't take that golden statue". You can still choose to have your character turn away from the obvious trap but they take Stress because it goes against who they are.

Mental States

Your Stress Threshold is divided into 4 categories which are, in order, UNSURE (U), NERVOUS (N), SHAKEN (S), BREAKING (Br). Each of these make up a fourth of your Stress Threshold.

These are a way to easily communicate how your character is doing (both player-to-player and GM-to-player) but crossing certain categories have additional penalties.

As stated, the more Stress you take, the harder it is to function. When this happens is noted by your Panic Threshold

Panic Threshold is matched to a Stress Category: hitting that Stress Category means that you start rolling your Defense Roll and all Skill and Confrontation Checks on 1 lower die. This stacks with Fatigue.

Your Panic Threshold is set by your Honor Category at the beginning of the session.

Beyond Breaking & Mental Breakdowns

A character whose stress has gone beyond their Stress Threshold is suffering from a mental breakdown.

They seek to remove themselves from the stressful situation as quickly as possible and either gain a Flaw or increase the die size of an existing one. Determined by both the GM and player if the character survives.

This is specific to the situation, but the character has lost all rationality and so will take the most direct route in their mind, even if it exposes them.

For example, a character that’s having a mental breakdown in the middle of the fight drops their weapon, stands up, turns, and runs for the nearest exit at full speed: exposing themselves to the enemy.

Or they might simply drop their weapon and plead for mercy – refusing to defend themselves.

Healing Stress

Stress heals slowly over time: the more stress you have, the longer it takes to heal.

This is charted using Relaxation.

When a character’s Relaxation reaches their current stress, it resets to 0 and they lower their stress by 1, making high stress exponentially more difficult to heal.

But there are a lot of ways to gain Relaxation.

Taking a Breather

Taking a Breather heals both Stress and Damage and so it talked about in the Taking a Breather section below but it's a method you shouldn't forget to use.

Taking Time Off

A person is considered to spend 8 hours sleeping, with the rest of the day (16 hours), doing things.

However, for each hour your character takes to rest (read a good book, indulge in a hobby, take a bath), they get 1 Relaxation.

Additionally, by spending silver, you can increase that amount on a 10-silver-to-1-Relaxation basis.

So, going out to dinner for 2 hours would normally give you 2 Relaxation, but going to a fancy dinner and spending 50 sc on it, gives you 7 instead.

Hobbies

A character can also benefit from specific hobbies, which can be deduced from their Characteristics.

You can only gain the benefit of a Characteristic once per week for a number of hours equal to its Rank with each hour spent on the hobby giving an additional +1 Relaxation (since it combines with Taking Time Off), although you can stack multiple Characteristics so next time you go out drinking, bring a friend you have Devotion to along to hang out.

Flaws

Another option you have is to give yourself penalties due to your Flaws.

Having a Flaw increases the opponent’s Check by the Flaw’s Severity but heals you an amount of Stress equal to twice the roll of that die. Additionally, you don't gain the normal d4! Stress from the Check.

Creature Comforts

Finally, you can indulge in creature comforts.

This is similar to spending silver while taking time off, but these represent permanent purchases, like getting a really nice mattress for your bunk.

In this case, whenever a Description would apply, you get an extra point of Relaxation.

So, if you’re hiking but you have extra-padded, tailored boots, you would get 3 Relaxation.

Vitality

Vitality represents how close you are to passing out and dying. It is both your health and stamina. As you take damage, you will get closer to death and gain more penalties based on your Fatigue Threshold.

Physical States

Vitality is divided into 4 categories which are, in order, HURT (H), BLOODIED (B), WOUNDED (W), CRITICAL (C). Each of these make up a fourth of your Vitality.

These are a way to easily communicate a fighter’s health (both player-to-player and GM-to-player) but crossing certain categories have additional penalties.

Fatigue Threshold

As stated, the more damage you take, the harder the fight becomes. When this happens is noted by your Fatigue Threshold

Fatigue Threshold is matched to a wound category: hitting that wound category means that you start rolling your Defense Roll and all Skill and Confrontation Checks on 1 lower die. This stacks with Panic.

When completely unencumbered, your Fatigue Threshold is at the maximum level, Critical, meaning you only become fatigued once you are near death.

Wearing armor, shields, and carrying lots of stuff may lower your Fatigue Threshold to a less injured category (e.g. Bloodied or Hurt), meaning you become fatigued more quickly. In a pinch, it may be wise to doff your shield or drop some gear to increase your fatigue threshold a notch.

Note that if this would take you above Hurt, you become always Fatigued.

Changing Equipment While Wounded

Since Fatigue Threshold is partly determined by the shield you're using, a quick way to up your Fatigue Threshold is to simply drop your shield.

Now, the flip side of this is that if you become MORE encumbered while wounded, you could get Fatigued earlier.

Beyond Critical

A character whose total damage is beyond Critical is dead: roll up a new character.

The only exception is if your character is moving towards their goal.

In this case, they can continue to keep fighting until the end of the combat or until they reach double what they would need to reach Critical again.

However, this is a short lease: they’re still dead and no healing or medicine can bring them back.

Healing Damage

Whenever a character takes damage, they record another wound.

After a number of days equal to a wound’s current damage, you remove 1 damage from that wound.

This means that a 3-damage wound would take 3 days to become a 2-damage wound, which would take 2 days to become a 1-damage wound, and so on.

The upside is that all wounds heal simultaneously: if you have 10 3-damage wounds, it will only take you 6 days to completely heal.

There are other ways to speed up healing as well:

Taking a Breather

Taking a Breather heals both Stress and Damage and so it talked about in the Taking a Breather section below but it's a method you shouldn't forget to use.

First Aid

By administering first aid within the first 10 minutes of a wound being inflicted, you lower the damage by 1.

You can only do this once per wound, however.

Medicine

By administering the proper medication, it can greatly speed up healing, removing 1 day from each wound for each point of damage, although there’s a minimum of 1 day.

So, for that 3-damage wound, it would only take 2 days to get to 2 damage, and 1 day to get to 1 damage.

Resting & Nursing

You can also take time off and not put a lot of stress and pressure on yourself: allowing your body to focus on healing.

In this case, if you have no Stress, every point of Relaxation gained that day is taken off the days to heal instead, however, you can only heal 1 damage a day and it only applies to 1 wound.

Fallen Comrades

Watching your friends die around you or abandon you is extremely Stressful, even if you’re winning.

Each time a side loses a quarter of their original number (either through dying, going unconscious, or running away), those remaining gain d10! Stress.

Taking a Breather

Pausing and resting for 30 minutes heals you half the damage and Stress (rounded down) that you've take since the last time you've Taken a Breather.

If the rest is broken for any reason, you lose all benefit from it.

Honor

Honor is a measure how not only how a person measures their own integrity but also how other people see them. Honor isn’t about doing the commonsense thing; it’s about doing the heroic thing. People who run from a fight, even when outnumbered, lose Honor. People who let others talk bad about them and don’t get even lose Honor.

The question of Honor isn’t a question of good and evil; many evil men have been honorable. It’s more about living up to the ideals of your culture and being true to your inner self.

Categories & Bonuses

Honor has its benefits. Confidence allows you to push yourself a little bit more than you otherwise would. During the game, you can spend your Honor points to influence anything, rolls or static metrics, whenever you want on a 1 to 1 basis.

Doing so lowers your Honor but might save your character’s life. So, if you roll a 13 and your opponent rolls a 14, you could spend 3 Honor to beat their total. Likewise, if an enemy deals 8 damage to you and you have 6 Honor, you could spend all of it and only take 2 damage.

Additionally, each category has an Honor Dice, which will be talked about in the next session.

Honor cannot go above 25 or below 0. The size of the Honor Die is also determined at the beginning of session and doesn’t change during play even if the category does.

Dishonor

0 - 5 Honor, No Honor Die

The lowest category, this person is unsure that they have what it takes to be a hero and so every decision is laced with hesitation and uncertainty.

Low Honor

6 - 10 Honor, d4!

This character hasn’t really proved anything, one way or another. Others and herself are waiting for her to make her mark on the world.

Average Honor

11 - 15 Honor, d6!

Having reached this category, the character has shown that they have potential and some follow through.

High Honor

16 - 20 Honor, d8!

She has now separated herself from the common rabble and can stand a head above the crowd.

Great Honor

21 - 25 Honor, d10!

This rare sliver represents those who can perfectly balance being ahead of others but not so much so that they invoke the ire of those below them as well as being confident while not being cocky.

Honor Dice

Fundementally, Honor Dice can be used in the same way as Honor, where it can be spent like Honor, only, instead of spending it on a 1-to-1 modifier, when you spend an Honor Die, you roll the corresponding die and add or subtract it.

Unless otherwise stated, uses of your Honor Dice are cross session so there's a place on your character sheet to record how many you have banked. As a new character, you'll start with 1 but as you go up Honor Categories, you'll gain more.

GMs are also free to reward Honor Die for particularly heroic behavior.

Dice Size Hierarchy

Bonfire plays around a lot with dice sizes, so it’s important to know what dice to grab in those cases.

Dice sizes go as follows:

d2 → d3 → d4 → d6 → d8 → d10 → d12 → d20

If a d4 is exploding and drops to a d3 or lower, it stops exploding.

If the dice size increases past a d20, you’ll add an extra dice starting at a d4. Further dice sizes will increase this smaller dice. This can continue ad nauseum, adding more and more dice, but no matter how many dice there are, they’re always considered to be rolled on 1 dice.

So, increasing a d20 7 times to a 2d20! + d3! is still considered to be 1 dice.

Perfect Rolls on Shrunken Die

There are several places where rollings a natural 20 on a d20 offers additional bonuses, but your dice might be a smaller size for some reason (such as when you're defending while being flanked).

In those cases, you can still gain the benefits of natural 20 but only if the dice explode enough that the total value of those dice is equal to or over 20 (including the -1 from the explosion).

So, if you're rolling a d12! where you would normally roll a d20!, you'd need to get 1 explosion with the second roll getting higher than an 9.

Likewise, if you're rolling a d4! instead of a d20!, you'd need 6 explosions (with the last one being greater than a 2) to get you there.

Chapter 1Basic Mechanics

Introduction

What is a Confrontation?

A Confrontation is any extended battle of wills.

This is mostly of two people in disagreement, who’re trying to convince the other to bend but it can also be the battle in a man’s soul as he tries to decide what to do.

You won’t break out this framework every time someone wants to haggle but this should be a tool in your belt to diversify the types of stories you tell and where you can get conflict.

Flow of A Confrontation

A Confrontation is like a conversation: it tends to go back and forth but sometimes, it doesn’t.

It has a form but it is like a river; it flows, changes depths and widths; splits and rejoins.

But unlike a river, it can sometimes return to where it once was.

So, in that way, you can have a back-and-forth where people make arguments until one person concedes and moves onto their next set of arguments about a different concern they have, but, realistically, you might have a few back-and-forths that’s interrupted by a line of questions that are only there to get more information in preparation for another argument.

Or you might have people presenting arguments but what these arguments attempt at addressing swaps between the different concerns each party has and so what the last person said is only tangentially related to what they’re responding to.

Thus is the nature of conversations; thus is the nature of Confrontations.

The main goal of this framework is to allow the GM and players to arbitrate how well they’re doing when trying to convince someone without slowing down roleplaying. It will slow you down initially, as you learn the rules, but soon you’ll find that you can easily run small Confrontations all the time, without the players knowing and without you thinking twice.

Translating The Rules

Unless your players are very rules focused or have questions about how they’re doing, most of the time, this framework will ‘run’ in the background of a given scene where the players are talking.

Let’s talk about that.

You’re players want to convince a guild informer that they got their hands on to betray his brothers, acting in his own self interest.

Whenever a player says something, you take what they said and ask yourself “Are they appealing to this person’s emotions or are they presenting evidence?” That translates it into the rules.

And then ask yourself “how would this person respond? Why? Are they making an emotional appeal back or are they countering with something that they consider evidence?” Bam; you have their tact.

If the players Appeal and you Appeal back, that’s when you ask for a Check to resolve how well they did: framing the result within the narrative of what’s going on.

This does have a bit of a learning curve (as all new things do) but, theoretically, in these types of scenes, on some level, you’re already thinking about how the NPC is going to respond so this is just taking it one step further.

Trust me, soon it will become second nature.

When to Start a Confrontation

There are a lot of reasons why a Confrontation will take place, some better than others. Remember: When in doubt, the controling player or GM has the final say.

Can & Can’t Do

The first way to initiative a Confrontation is if both these criteria are met:

  1. The person (or people) can do what you want
  2. They’re on the fence

If the person can do what you want but they’re not on the fence about it, no Confrontation happens, so you can’t convince the holy knight to murder a baby: he can do it but he’s probably not on the fence about baby-killings.

If the person can’t do what you want but they’re not on the fence, in your favor, they’ll help where they can but, ultimately, it’s just not in their power.

So, a peasant you saved would be totally willing to help you take down the evil overlord but he’s unable to do so, so no Confrontation.

If a person can do what you want but isn’t on the fence but this time against you, no Confrontation. You’re not going to be able to convince your blood enemy to help you out by healing your wounds and giving you a hot meal.

Characteristics

All that being said, that’s not the end all be all; you can initiate a Confrontation if you can present it in such a way that it appeals to one of their Goals, Convictions, Devotions, Descriptions, Racial Temperament, or Flaws.

Goals

If a person can be convinced that doing what you want them to do would forward one of their Goals, that can put them on the fence, allowing a Confrontation.

So, if you know that your archenemy is looking to expand his power, you can approach them; if you team up, you can both take down the bigger threat.

They might still say ‘no’ but showing them that your Goals align might help pave over a rocky relationship.

Convictions

If a person has a Conviction that applies, it might put them on the fence or convince them to attempt the action anyway.

For instance, if the knight had the Conviction “Those above me know what is best” and is then tasked with killing a baby by his superior officer, that might initiate a Confrontation as he wrestles with his decision.

Devotions

Invoking Devotions area also a way to get people to consider things they normally wouldn’t.

So, if that peasant had a Devotion to the party, even though he can’t win against the evil overlord, he might still attempt it simply due to his love for the party.

Flaws

Finally, Flaws can cause people do things they normally wouldn’t.

For instance, a normally good knight who has an anger issue, driven mad by what the orc tribe has done to his hometown could enter a Confrontation to resist going on a murderous rampage and kill all the orcs, every single one of them. And not just the men, but the women and the children, too. They’re like animals, and he’ll slaughter them like animals.

Racial Temperament

Races in Bonfire have a natural temperament to which they lean which causes them to behave differently than other species. This is called the "Racial Temperament" and can cause them to act against their best judgment.

Learning Another's Characteristics

Every character who's going to use these rules should invest in the Intuition Skill.

Because you're not physically there and your GM is probably not the greatest actor of all time, things might get missed that your character would have picked up on: the Intuition Skill is meant to simulate that mastery of reading those subtle (and not so subtle) cues that people drop about who they are when interacting with others.

Playing it Out

Okay, so what does a Confrontation look like?

Essentially, both sides will go back and forth presenting arguments to the other, which either counters it with their own arguments or concedes the point.

This very much resembles a normal conversation punctuated with Checks.

The end goal is to get more points than the opposition.

Time Scale

Because they’re a battle of wills, Confrontations have an extremely elastic time scale.

Sometimes, an entire Confrontation can happen within a second (in the terms of making a snap-decision), while other times, it could take weeks (like a trial or diplomatic summit).

And sometimes, it’ll vary.

Say you’re trying to convince an expert to join your crew.

You meet with him in his office where you do your initial pitch, but you quickly realize that there’s a lot you don’t know.

So, you don’t push the point, tell him to have a good day and then do two weeks of research, after which, you approach him again.

Making A Check

Confrontation Checks like normal Checks with a base d20! + Stat Mod + the Applicable Rank, however, the different types of Characteristics have different interactions that are unique to Confrontations.

Both sides gain a base d4! of Stress unless the winner beats the loser’s total by more than 5, in which case the winner removes 1 Stress Dice.

Note that additional Stress dice might be added to a Check: in this case, you roll them together, taking the highest. These additional Stress Dice cannot be reduced for any reason.

Windle is making an impassioned plea for Lord Kilgore to spare the children. He has “Children are our future” as a Conviction at Rank 6 and the Oration Skills at Rank 3. He also has a Reputation as someone you don’t want to mess with (Rank 5).

He rolls a d20! + his Pre Mod (+2) + 6 for the Conviction + 3 for the Skill +5 for the Reputation.

Since he’s adding the Skill, he is going to roll 3d4!, taking the highest, for Stress but, since he beat Lord Kilgore’s roll by more than 5, he’s only going to roll 2.

He rolls a 2 for the first d4! and a 4 and then a 4 and then a 2 for his second for a total of 8 Stress.

Convictions

Convictions add their Rank to the Check.

You can add multiple Convictions but, for each one, you’re going to roll an additional d4! of Stress, taking the highest of all Stress dice rolled.

Devotions

Devotions are special and talked about later on so they can’t be used for Confrontation Checks if those rules for automatically adding them to your points come into play.

However, if you’re in a situation where points don’t matter (by only having a single Check or using the Morale Confrontation rules, for example), they behave like Convictions.

Descriptions

Descriptions can be added to a roll, however, if it is just Descriptions, you’re going to roll an additional d4! of Stress for each one added, including the first, taking the highest of all Stress dice rolled.

However, as long as you have at least 1 Conviction or 1 Devotion added to the same Check, you don’t have this penalty and can add any number of Descriptions freely.

Reputation

Reputations behave just like Descriptions.

Racial Temperament

Racial Temperaments behave just like Descriptions.

Flaws

Occasionally, Flaws may come into play.

If they give you a penalty, the normal penalties and bonuses apply.

If they give you a bonus, which they sometimes might, they act as a Description.

Skills

Skills can also help make an Appeal or additional Skills can help with a line of Reasoning.

In these cases, you can add their Rank to the roll, like a normal Check, but you’re going to add an additional d4! of Stress for each one added, taking the highest of all Stress dice rolled.

Penalties To A Check

Your Characteristics can also work against you: a Conviction might convince you to do something that isn’t in your best interest or your Description might make it hard to convince someone you have the best intentions.

In these cases, you’re going to apply your Rank as a penalty.

If the player was the one to suggest it, they can heal an amount of Stress equal to their Rank (or what they rolled on their Rank Dice) or gain 1 Honor.

If the GM is the one to suggest it, the player only gets the penalty.

What Stat Should I Use?

Pre is the default stat for Confrontation Checks, however, players can try Appeals based on more than just your charisma.

For instance, a big brute trying to intimidate someone might use Str instead.

Arguments

So, during a Confrontation, there are 3 things you can do: the first is to present Evidence, the second is to make an emotional Appeal, and the third is to Concede the Point.

Presenting Evidence

Evidence is facts: things that you or the opposition believe to be true, even if it isn’t true in actuality.

However, there is subjectivity in it. The only thing about Evidence that really matters is if the person presenting it actually believes it.

Countering With Reasoning

Patiently explaining why their Evidence is wrong can help but most people need a lot of Reasoning to move a little.

Countering Evidence with Reasoning results in the Reasoning person gaining 1 point and d4! Stress.

Countering with an Appeal

Countering Evidence with an Appeal also results in a stalemate but the defender gains d4! Stress.

Countering with Reasoning

Countering Evidence with Reasoning also results in a stalemate but the defender gains d4! Stress.

Countering with Multiple Pieces of Evidence

If a person has multiple pieces of Evidence that counter the presented Evidence or Appeal, it can be presented at the same time.

If not addressed and countered later on, each of these additional pieces of Evidence is considered to have been conceded to at the end of the Confrontation.

Making an Appeal

Appeals use a person’s emotions to try and convince them. It is using the power of your belief to sway the other person.

To make an Appeal, you need to have a Characteristic that correlates to the tact you’re taking. If you have none, then you’ll have to use Evidence or Concede the Argument.

Countering with Evidence

Countering an Appeal with Evidence is a stalemate and results in both sides getting no points.

Countering With An Appeal

Countering an Appeal with an Appeal results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic that relates to their Appeal.

The winner gets 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total. The loser gets nothing.

Countering With Reasoning

Countering an Appeal with Reasoning results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic or Skill that relates to their Appeal or line of Reasoning.

The winner gets 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total if the winner was using a Characteristic. If using a Skill instead, they get 1 point + 1 for every 10 full points of difference. The loser gets nothing.

Showing Your Reasoning

Reasoning with someone means trying to convince someone with knowledge that you have and so is based off of Skills.

Unlike Evidence it relies on 1) your Confidence, 2) your actual knowledge of the subject, and 3) your ability to communicate that knowledge effectively to the person you're talking to.

Generally, this is used for knowledge that the player doesn't have but the character might based on their Skills.

Countering Reasoning with Evidence is a stalemate and results in both sides getting no points.

Countering Reasoning with an Appeal results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Characteristic or Skill that relates to their Appeal or line of Reasoning.

The winner gets 2 points + 1 point for every 5 full points above the loser's total if the winner was using a Characteristic. If using a Skill instead, they get 1 point + 1 for every 10 full points of difference. The loser gets nothing.

Countering With Reasoning

Countering Reasoning with Reasoning results in the person with the greater force of personality prevailing.

A Check is made with each side choosing a Skill that relates to their line of Reasoning.

The winner gets 1 point + 1 for every 10 full points of difference. The loser gets nothing.

Insults & Taunts

Insults are used to inflict Stress or, if there are others viewing (or deciding the Confrontation), a way to discredit someone

If discrediting the person and when appropriate, you gain points and inflict stress, otherwise, you just inflict additional stress but, either way, you lose honor equal to a roll of your Honor Dice

Conceding the Argument

If a person can’t counter an argument with their own, either through an emotional Appeal or through Evidence, they have to concede the point. This is most often signaled by them changing the subject.

In this case, the other side gets 3 points.

Just Inflicting Stress

You can also use these rules to just inflict Stress on your opponent in psychological warfare, in which case you won't be running a traditional Confrontation.

In this case, you'll need to have done something that would warrant taking Stress (such as killing any enemy, causing them to make a Trauma Check, going down a Wound or Stress Category, mutilating their friends' bodies; any war crime done in sight of them or where they'll come across later will do, really) and then make a Check like it's a normal Appeal.

However, because you're not trying to convince them of anything, you won't be tracking points: instead, the number of points a person would have gotten is instead converted to Stress (including the normal Stress you would get). This does mean that, if you fail, you might be getting the Stress instead.

Tracking Points

The easiest way to track points is to create a quick grid of 2 horizontal lines and 1 vertical. Label 1 column “Players” (or “P” for short) and the other column “Opposition” (or “O”) and then label 1 row “Concessions” (“C”) and the other “Appeals” (“A”).

In the end, you’ll have something that looks like the first image.

Whenever one side has to Concede an Argument, put a slash in the C row under their column and, whenever they win an Appeal, note the number of points they got under the A row.

It should look something like the second image by the end of it.

Messy but the quick notes allow you to easily track points and keep things moving.

At the end of the Confrontation, add up all the numbers in the A row and add them to the number of slashes in the C row, multiplied by 3 (because each slash is worth 3 points).

Other Things to Note

Devotions

Devotions are powerful things and can create irresponsible biases in people.

At the start of the Confrontation, Devotions are given directly as points where appropriate.

Positive Devotions to a person, for example, give that person points while trying to persuade you, while negative Devotions will give you points to resist them.

Starting Bias

It will be extremely rare that the two sides will be coming into the Confrontation with a clean slate. Preconceived bias can come from a number of places with the most common being cultural or racial tensions but they can also be the result of people sharing the same faith (at least, outwardly) or being from the same hometown.

There are 5 levels of starting bias. Preferred, Like, Tolerate, Dislike, Hate. These are based on how the person 'defending' in the Confrontation views the person who's trying to convince them.

Equipment

Equipment can also be useful in a Confrontation. If used as Evidence, the normal rules apply but what if you use it as part of an Appeal or a line of Reasoning? You draw your sword to help let the person you're interrogating know you mean business.

If that's the case, you add an extra d6! + the Rank of any applicable Descriptions the equipment might have. If there are multiple pieces of equipment, you increase the dice size by 1 per piece and add their Descriptions as well.

If the equipment doesn't really suit the purpose you're using it for, like threatening someone with a broken chair leg instead of a mace, you apply a d4! instead, although the same rules apply for stacking pieces of equipment.

Getting Points

Okay, so a Confrontation will look very much like a normal conversation and there will be a lot more going on than just people presenting Evidence, making Appeals, or Conceding the Argument.

Sometimes a side will just ask a series of questions to learn more about the other side or the whole Confrontation will just look like a tea party.

If there’s any confusion: ask what the intent is. If the intent is to try and convince the person, it might be time to add points.

If the point is anything else (information gathering, for example), then just keep things rolling.

Ending a Confrontation

In the end, one side will need to decide either way, which will end the Confrontation.

This can either be one side dropping it and walking away or it could be them pushing the point to get the other to make a decision.

Pushing the Point

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to push the point, or end the Confrontation. He simply tries to push the other side to make a decision.

A couple of things can happen here.

If the other person has favorable disposition to the person pushing and they still have more to say, the Confrontation continues, with that person gaining 1 point.

If the other person doesn't disposition to the person pushing and they still have more to say, the otherside is considered to have Conceded against every remaining, unaddressed argument that the person has. The Confrontation ends and points are totalled.

If the argument is based off of a Characteristic, they're only considered to have Conceded once per Characteristic and only if that Characteristic was never used in that Confrontation. Additionally, things that would have used as Evidence is also only counted as being Conceded to once as well and, again, only if it wasn't addressed during that Confrontation.

If, however, it turns out that the pushed person does, in fact, have no more to say, regardless of their dispoition towards the other person, than the Confrontation ends and it’s time to compare point totals.

Victory or Defeat

At the end of the Confrontation, both sides reveal their points. The one with the most points gets their way.

Introduction

What is combat?

Combat is what happens when one side wants to kill the other and are trying to do it right now, on a second-by-second basis.

I don’t think I really need to define combat because, as a naturally violence species, combat often comes second nature to us but I thought it would be an interesting exercise.

Starting a Combat

The Clock

The Clock begins at 1, and then progresses to 2, 3, 4 etc, ad infinitum. When the Clock matches a character’s Initiative, they go. Each of these numbers represent 1 second of time passing. What second a character's next action is referred to as their initiative (or "where they are on the Clock")

This can be a difficult when you first start so we recommend that you start with only running two or three enemies until you get the hang of it.

Each side rolls an initiative die. The lower the die, the more prepared they are for the attack, with the instigator going on second 1. The initiative’s die is based on the general combat preparedness of the company, which is generally based on how likely they think danger is.

The table provided gives you the extreme ends of the spectrum as well as the default but the initiative die can be anywhere in between.

Surprise

Those whose initiative hasn't come up are surprised and, on second 1 of the combat can choose to do one of three things: contine what they’re doing, stand still (if they’re not going faster than a Jog), or flee away from the danger (if there is a clear escape route, otherwise, this isn't an option).

Whatever they choose, they have to keep doing that action until their Initiative comes up: They can take no other actions, not even to defend themselves, thus they roll their defense die on a dice size 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!).

You can lower your Initiative (thus lowering the amount of time you're surpised) by either making a lot of noise or by being hit.

If someone is making a lot of noise, for whatever reason, you lower everyone's (enemy and ally alike) initiative by 2. Multiple sources of noise doesn't stack.

If the surprised person is hit, the pain quickly brings them into the reality of the situation, and they may act 2 seconds later (or before that if their initiative was the next second).

This can be done by either an enemy hitting you with their weapon or by a friend, running up and slapping you across the face.

Ambushes

Surprise happens regardless of whether or not the danger was visible and known to the characters but in cases of ambush, where the danger is unknown until suddenly its not, the GM can call for a Listening Check, which represents characters cluing into subtle clues that the manure is about to hit the windmill.

Each Boon gives a -2 to the Initiative roll while each Complication gives a +2 to the roll.

For example; Kelso, Bran Redhand, and Dalsini the Odd are traveling through the wilderness. Kelso, being clever, asked about the route they were traveling before and got rumors that there’s been an uptick in bandit attacks in the area so the company is wary while traveling.

Their second day out, they cross a bridge; unknowingly, the ambush point of the single bandit that’s been causing so much trouble.

They’re not taking care to be particularly quiet or stealthy as they’re traveling by road so the bandit, in his hiding place, sees them coming from awhile away. As they start to cross the bridge (Redhand in front, Kelso in back, and Dalsini in the middle), the bandit sneaks out and attacks Kelso from behind in an ambush.

The bandit goes on count 1 with their attack since he's completely ready for them. The company would normally roll d8s since they're traveling in a generally dangerous place, however, because they're on the lookout for bandit attacks, they instead roll on d6s since, you know, they're getting attacked by a bandit.

Redhand has a +2 Initiative Modifier but got 2 Boons on his Perception Check so, when he rolls a 1, his total is -1, so he goes on second 1. Dalsini has a +0 Initiative Modifier and got 1 Boon on his Check and his roll was 6, so he goes on second 4.

Finally, Kelso got 2 Complications on his Check and with an -2 Initiative Modifier and roll of 6, he goes on second 8, although, if the bandit hits, he’ll be able to act on second 3.

On second 1, Kelso stops to figure out what’s going on until his initiative, while Dalsini, being a weak weird-adept, decides he’s going to run for it, so starts running towards the other end of the bridge - away from the present danger.

Redhand, however, leaps into action on second 1, drawing his sword as he rushes to his friend’s aid.

Combat Basics

Taking Actions

Every action takes a certain amount of time. On a character’s Initiative, they declare their action and are told how long that action will take. After that time, the action is complete.

So, if a character wants to walk across a room, which will take 8 seconds, and his Initiative is 5, once the Clock reaches 5, he starts walking and once it reaches 13, he’s gotten the other side of the room.

At any point, you may choose to forfeit your action and change your course. If, when halfway across the room (at second 9), the character sees that his buddy is in trouble, he can immediately decide that, no, he’d rather be there, his character stops walking across the room and instead heads over to help his friend.

For every 10 seconds of Running, you take damage. This starts at a d4! but increases by 1 dice size every 10 seconds, capping at a d20!.

Zones of Engagement

There are 3 positions that you can have relative to your enemy. These positions aren’t reciprocal by nature: you can be threatened by someone and not have them be threatened by you.

Unengaged

This is basically the default. You are too far away for them to reasonable do anything.

Threatened

This means that you’re within 5 feet + your opponent’s weapon’s measure. From here, if you move faster than a Jog and/or if they’re within half their weapon Recovery, they can attack you.

If you go from Engaged to Threatened, your weapon initiative doesn't reset, so if you become Engaged again for any reason before your weapon's Recovery, you have to wait the rest of your Recovery to attack again.

If you go from Threatened to Unengaged, you need to wait half your weapon's Recovery - your Initiative modifier to reorient yourself before you attack again (minimum 1 second). If someone Threatens you in that time, you go back to having to wait your full Recovery before attacking (minus the time you already waited).

Engaged

You’ve closed the distance and can actually make an attack. If both combatants aren't surprised, the person with the highest weapon measure attacks first. If both weapons are exactly the same measure, the attacks happen simultaneously.

Note that this is only for the first attack: if subsequent attacks happen on the same second, they happen simultaneously, meaning that two people with different measure, attacking on the same second, can kill each other at the same time.

You're Surprised when you become Engaged, you're unable to attack back and, once you leave surprise, you must wait your weapon's Recovery before attacking.

If you move from being Engaged to being Threatened, for any reason, and the other person is within half their weapon's Recovery, they get a free attack on you.

Melee Combat

Melee Attack & Defense

Basic melee attacks always roll a base d20! + any attack modifiers, while defenders roll a d20! + any defense modifiers

Perfect Attacks & Defenses

A Perfect Attack or Defense is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll, while a Perfect Defense is an unmodified 20 on your Defense roll, but only if your final totals are still greater than your opponent’s. If not, the attack still hits for Perfect Attacks and you still dodge the blow for Perfect Defenses but you don’t gain the auxiliary benefits.

If both those qualifiers are met, you gain the following benefits on top of the normal ones:

Perfect Attack

You gain a free automatically successful, well-executed, Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Ranks in the Unarmed Skill Suite or any of its Advanced Skills, you may roll for the additional benefit, although, even on fumble, this doesn't affect the normal bonus for delievering a perfect attack.

Perfect Defense

You gain a chance to riposte, gaining a free full attack the next second if you’re wielding a melee weapon. This attack doesn’t affect your current count.

Near Perfect Attacks & Defenses

An unmodified roll of 19 on either Attack or Defense results in a Near Perfect Attack or Defense, where you may Shift for no penalty, change your Tactic, or, for attacks, gain an extra d4! to damage (which doesn't count as additional dice for /d DR), or, for defenses gained a free riposte with your off-hand weapon if it’s smaller than your size or a sidearm or an unarmed attack if you have at least 1 hand free.

Fumbles

On the flip side, rolling an unmodified 1 on your Attack of Defense roll results in a 'fumble', with your opponent getting a free attack on the next second that doesn't reset their next attack.

Fumbling when your opponent gets a Perfect Attack or Defense results in the normal bonuses and the free attack while both sides rolling a fumble results in the person with the highest total getting their way (so, if the attacker has the highest total, they hit) but no free attack for either.

Dealing Damage

Once both sides have rolled and added all modifiers, compare the totals against each other.

If the defender’s is equal to or greater than the attacker, the attack misses.

Parrying

If a melee attack roll is within the Parry Threshold of the defender’s total and they're not surprised, the defender may choose to deflect some of the incoming attack.

The attacker rolls their damage and subtracts the shield or weapon’s DR (2/d DR for weapons), which subjects them to a chance of breakage, and then their armor’s DR. Any leftover is still done to the defender. For piercing weapons, the /d DR of the shield or weapon is tripled.

So, a defender with a Parry Threshold of 4, and a total of 10 for their defense roll vs an attack roll of 14, could choose to parry the blow, taking less damage.

However, if it’s not, the attack lands and the attacker rolls his weapon’s damage die and adds any appropriate damage modifiers. The defender than subtracts their Damage Reduction and the remaining total is subtracted from their Vitality and added to their list of wounds.

Damage Reduction

Armor allows you to reduce the amount of damage taken. This is often times shorted to DR. There are two types: Static, noted by just a number, and Per Die, noted by a number /D.

Static simply subtracts that number from the damage and is done.

/D subtracts that number multiplied by the number of dice the attack is using. So, if someone is attacking you with a slashing weapon (2d6!), and you have armor that gives you 4/D, you’d subtract 8 from the damage.

Either way, if the attacker chooses to remain in engaged, he then adds his weapon’s Recovery to the current second on the Clock. Once the Clock reaches that new number, he may attack again.

Ranged Combat

Ranged Weapon Firing Break Down

If you’re wondering what you’re doing at any given second when firing a ranged weapon: here’s a breakdown for you.

So, if you already loading and cranked (drawing) your crossbow, it would only take 5 seconds to fire it.

This is drawing the missile from its quiver or pouch. For firearms, that means drawing out the powder and bullets.

This is the process of lighting the fuse. This can be dropped to 1 second but increases the chance of backfiring from 1 to 5.

This is knocking the arrow or loading the bullet and powder in the barrel.

Ranged Attack & Defense

Ranged attacks and defenses function identical to melee attacks and defenses (including Flanking), only get a few additional bonuses and penalties.

The first is that range (of course) and the second is movement.

When defending against a ranged attack, you get a +2 for each Movement Category above standing still. So, if you're getting shot at, get the lead out.

Perfect Attacks

A Perfect Attack is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll.

If you’re firing a ranged weapon, you managed to hit a sensitive area, lowering their max movement speed by one category, minimum Crawl speed. This continues until that wound is completely healed.

Near-Perfect Attacks

An unmodified roll of 19 on your Attack results in a Near Perfect Attack, giving you an extra d4! to damage.

Cover

Cover is anything that gets between you and the person trying to shoot at you.

For solid objects, such as walls, where it is easy to calculate how much of the person is covered, a flat bonus is appropriate. For every 10% covered, you get a +2, so 25% would be a +5, 50% would be a +10 and so on.

For people, tree, a moving object, or really anything that’s harder to calculate, you’ll use a dice backed on the difference in size, starting at a d20. Each difference in size shrinks or increases that cover die by 1 size.

Cover dice for most objects works the same as shields - if you’re behind cover, you add a roll of that dice to your defense. If you’re actively crouching behind it, you add the max it would give you to your defense plus another roll of the dice.

So, two medium size people standing one in front of the other would give the guy in the back d20 cover, same as two small sized people. If the person in the front is small sized and the guy in the back is still medium sized, the guy in the back only gains a d12 cover.

If the person is actively moving or trying not to be your cover, you can’t gain the bonus for actively crouching behind them.

If you don't move, however, cover becomes more and more useless as your shooter sights in. For each shot against you where you haven't moved, you either lower your effective cover percentage by 10 or shrink your cover dice by 1 size, depending on whether the cover is static or not.

Note this also applies to shields as well, so if you don't move while using a shield, it becomes less and less useful.

Firing Into Melee

Whenever you would hit with a ranged attack, if there are any creatures within 5 ft of the line of fire, treat each interposing creature as providing Cover and check each in order to see if they were hit instead.

Starting at the person closest to the archer or gunner, roll their Cover value as normal; if adding that value to the original target’s defense roll would make the attack miss, the new target makes a Defense roll against the original attack to determine if they hit the new target instead.

If they were not hit, don't resolve using the normal Range Miss rules; move to the next person giving Cover and resolve in the same manner

If there are any objects between the archer and their original target, resolve them according to their relative position to the intervening combatants. So, if a tree is between two fighters, resolve the closest fighter, than the tree, than the other fighter.

If none of the interposing combatants were hit, resolve any Cover the original target has (such as a shield). If that didn't help, the missile landed true: roll damage against the original target.

If the ranged attack was a miss, see the Ranged Misses section below.

Dealing Damage

Dealing damage with a ranged weapon works almost exactly like dealing damage with a melee weapon, however, you’re unable to Parry a ranged attack (that’s what Cover is for)

Ranged Misses

On a miss with a ranged weapon, the missile doesn’t magically disappear into oblivion; there’s a good chance it might hit someone else.

In the case of a miss, roll a d4. An odd result means it goes left, an even result means it goes right.

The difference between the attack and defense roll determines how many feet away the missile strayed.

Draw an imaginary line between the archer and that point. Everyone within 5ft of that line needs to roll a defense vs the original attack roll, starting with the person closest to the archer and ending with the furthest possible target, ignoring the original target.

Inanimate objects may provide enough cover to be considered targets in both Firing into Melee and Ranged Misses, such as a large boulder or tree. Resolve using the normal rules.

Misc. Combat Rules

Facing & Fighting Multiple People

The likelihood is that at some point you’ll want to be flanking or someone with want to flank you, to try and gain an advantage.

Defending right in front of you is pretty easy and so brooks no penalties, however, every 45 degrees off that the defender lowers their Defense dice by 1 size, with those being attacked from directly behind defend on a dice size 3 sizes smaller (normally, a d8!) since they take advantage of a huge blind spot.

This penalty can be mitigated through the use of shields. Small shields allow you to defend against an additional assailant, depriving them of that bonus, while Medium and Large ones allow you to defend against an extra 2 and 3, respectively. This only mitigates attacks coming from the side the shield is on and can never mitigate an attack directly from behind but otherwise, the player can decide who they're blocking.

A maximum of 8 people at a time can effectively attack person at once, assuming that they’re out in the open. After that, every assailant’s attack die is lowered by 1 size per extra person.

Trauma

If someone does more damage after DR than your Trauma Threshold, this triggers a Trauma Check: that person rolls a d20 and tries to get under half their Con stat.

Failure means that your character has been overcome with pain to the point that he’s unable to act, leaving him completely defenseless for a number of seconds equal to how much he failed by times 3.

Double Trauma

A character that gets dealt their full Vitality in damage in a single blow has suffered a grievous wound - the scar of which, if he survives, will be with him until the day he dies.

When this happens, the trauma is too much, and they simply die where they stand if they fail their check. Even if they succeed, they’re still suffer Trauma for the next 30 seconds but gain a wicked looking scar.

Battle scars are marks of pride and honor. Characters who become scared gain a Rank 1 Description “Battle Scars”. If they would get this Description again, increase that Rank by 1.

Coup De Grace

A helpless enemy is one completely at your mercy.

If a character is completely helpless, either from failing a Trauma check, being bound, or deep asleep, a character can kill the target instantly.

This move takes 10 seconds to complete as it takes a bit of positioning to pull off. The character’s whose throat is being slit gives out a cry of pain as they expire.

Equipment

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch and these are catchall rules for the use of equipment that doesn’t already come with auxiliary benefits, such as weapons (damage) and armor (DR).

On an applicable Check or roll, you add an extra d6! + the Rank of any applicable Descriptions the equipment might have. If there are multiple pieces of equipment, you increase the dice size by 1 per piece and add their Descriptions as well. More on equipment Descriptions is found in Chapter 6: Equipment.

If the equipment doesn't really suite the purpose you're using it for, like trying to pry open a door with a weapon, you apply a d4! instead, although the same rules apply for stacking pieces of equipment.

Finally, if there is a specific use for the piece of Equipment (like weapons and armor), you can redeem an applicable Description to gain a temporary bonus to a roll or static modifier.

So, if you have Fine-Steel (+2) armor, if you get hit, you can increase the armor's DR by 2 for that damage roll specifically.

Redeeming this bonus causes the tool degrades, giving it (and any other equipment you use) 1 Wear, whether you succeed or not. More on Wear can be found in Chapter 6: Equipment.

Tactics

These rules add another layer of strategy to combat and should be implemented as soon as you have the basics down.

Each Tactic, at the top, when you can employ them and which other Tactics they can be combined with which. For “On Defense” and “On Attack”, the Tactic has to be declared before you roll.

Once committed to, Tactics can only be switched out on your next Attack or when you are no longer Engaged. So, if you’re Active Dodging, you have to commit to that Tactic on your defense but cannot stop Active Dodging until your next attack.

Active Dodging

On Defense

Compatible Attacks Hold Back

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself, and Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

This move relies on actively focusing on dodging against incoming attacks.

A defender commits to how much he wants to focus on dodging incoming attacks, both melee and ranged, choosing from a bonus to his defense of either +3 or +5, but netting an equal penalty to his attacks (-3 or -5).

Additionally, this increases the range for Near Perfect Defenses by 1, so you score them on a base natural 18 - 19.

But focusing on defense does slow you down, and you are limited to 5 ft / second of movement.

Choking Up On Your Weapon

On Defense

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, Charge, and In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses All except Fend Off, and Long Guard

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

When you’ve choked up on your weapon, your weapon’s Measure is halved, and you add that number, rounded down, to its Recovery. Your halved Measure is considered your new Measure for determining who attacks first and what Tactics can be employed on you as well.

However, if an opponent is In Your Guard and you choke up on your weapon, you defend with a standard d20! and deal full weapon damage.

Feint

On Attack

Compatible Attacks Called Shot, Charge, In Their Guard

Compatible Defenses Choking Up, Hold Back, Long Guard

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

When performing this move, you (hopefully) get your enemy to commit his guard to a different location than you plan on hitting. You receive a +5 to that Attack but receive a -3 to Defense.

Fend Off

On Attack

Compatible Attacks Hold Back, Jab, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Hit the Deck, Long Guard, Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

If your weapon's measure is at least 1 foot greater than your opponent's, you can try and fend them off. This means that you may attack them freely, while they can't do the same to you.

If you attack first when first closing with someone, you may forfeit that attack to automatically be Fending Off. After that, while Fending Off someone, you can attack as normal.

To become fully engaged with you, they'll have to knock your weapon aside with a regular attack that deals no damage. Should they hit, they become fully engaged and can attack as normal, although they need to wait their full weapon Recovery.

Until they do so, they’re only considered to be Threatening you so they’re unable to attack you directly.

You can fend off multiple people, however, they have to all be in front of you (up to 90 deg off center).

Flee

Any Time While Engaged

Compatible Attacks All except Charge

Compatible Defenses All

While engaged, you can turn around and move at a jogging speed or higher to attempt to disengage. This also applies to running by an opponent who Threatens you.

However, you open yourself up to an attack. If you have any foes who are within half of their weapon recovery they may make a free attack against you.

Note: because of the inherent risks involved in Fleeing, people will typically not choose to do so unless forced to by a mental break or some other means.

In Their Guard

On Attack

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, Charge, and Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Step Back

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

If you're engaged with a person and have a weapon that is at least 1 foot shorter than your opponent, you may attempt to get in their guard.

On a successful attack, the shorter weapon combatant has successfully knocked his foe's weapon aside and is now fighting at extreme close range, causing them huge penalties.

The attacker deals no damage but the longer weapon defends on a dice 1 size smaller against all attacks made against them and all damage dice is also reduced by one size (3d4! → 3d3!) on all attacks they make.

Power Attack

On Attack

Compatible Attacks Charge, In Their Guard, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses Brace Yourself

Other Compatible Flee, Shift

This is the fighting equivalent of baseball batting your enemy, swinging with all your might.

With this attack, you receive a -3 to your Attack and next Defense but your damage increases.

Piercing weapons increase 1 die by 1 size. Slashing weapons increase all dice by 1 size and crushing weapons increase its highest die by 2 sizes.

This only affects the base damage dice the weapon has: not damage added via Skills.

Shift

Any Time While Engaged

Compatible Attacks All except Careful Aiming, and Charge

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself, and Step Back

While Engaged, you can move at your walking speed to either side of your opponent or change 1 facing per second but suffer a -3 to your next Attack and Defense. This penalty doesn’t stack.

However, if you Shift the same second you use another, compatible Tactic, you don’t receive this penalty and gain a +3 that can be either used to increase a bonus or decrease a penalty from the other Tactic.

Step Back

On Defense

Compatible Attacks In Their Guard, Phalanx Fighting

Compatible Defenses All except Brace Yourself

This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back from your opponent’s blows at your walking speed.

Before your defense roll, if you declare a Step Back, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll and can decide to move directly back or back and to the side.

If you move directly back you move your Jogging speed backwards for 1 second.

If you move back and to the side, you move at a Walking speed for 1 second.

Either way, you gain a -3 to Attack. If you perform this Tactic multiple times between attacks, you gain an additional -3 to Attack for each time you do so.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Feint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage.

Ending Combat

Combat ends when one side is incapacitated, reached a truce, or have fled the field. At that point the Clock stops, and play moves back to narrative time.

If you engaged in a Morale Confrontation, everyone on the side that won the combat gains the difference in points to their Honor, regardless of whether or not their side has less. So, if the players have 3 points and the goblins had 7, every player still gains 4 Honor.

The losing side subtracts that with the same caveats.

Introduction

Challenges cover a lot of ground: in fact, it’s easier to say that Challenges are anything that’s not covered by Confrontations or Combats.

I know, that doesn’t help, so here’s a more practical definition:

A Challenge is when the characters have a thing they want to accomplish (an Objective) but one or more things that would result in meaningful failure stand in their way (Obstacles).

Challenges are a framework that you can use as a catch-all for all the other types of situations that might come up in play.

Because these are new terms, here is a high level overview: Challenges > Obstacles > Checks, where Challenges are a conceptual framework made up of 1 or more Obstacles and Obstacles are tools that interact with 1 or more Checks.

Objectives

What do the players want? What thing, material or immaterial do they want? Where do the players want to be?

This is the Objective and is often determined by the Goals of the characters involved.

Setting up the endpoint allows you to get the scale of a Challenge down, which can help you get a scale and number of the Obstacles the characters are going to face.

Obstacles

Obstacles are places along the path to the Objective where meaningful failure could occur.

And we’re defining “meaningful” here as “things that will either effect the likelihood and shape of the Objective or the next Obstacle”.

So, for instance, if you’re breaking into house, climbing the wall could result in meaningful failure as a fall could damage the character, making it more difficult for them to perform the next task.

Or, if you’re researching a spell, an Obstacle could be taking notes: poor notes could mean that either you take longer to get the spell or you miss an important part of the shaping, and your spell now has a side effect.

The Obstacles should make sense: don’t feel like you need to force them or hit an arbitrary number, although you might find that how characters deal with Obstacles could result in new Obstacles or another Challenge.

Additionally, Obstacles can be made obsolete by player planning or choice; they can be optional, granting an extra bonus but not actually being required to reach the Objective of a Challenge; they can be replaced based on how the players are tackling things; and they can be player-initiated with the players decided to take on extra Obstacle to perhaps gain a boon or solve a problem.

For instance, fixing the serious side effects from shaping that new spell could result in another Challenge where the characters now have to figure out a way to undo a negative enchantment.

Or it could just result in another Obstacle. It all depends on the focus of the game.

At the end of the day, it's important to remember that Obstacles are tool for the GM to use to help arbitrate things and to provide a consistent and common-sense way of the players interacting with the world.

Difficulty

Difficulty is a dice that is added to the Skill Check based on things that might be going on at the time to either help or hinder your success. This difficulty is noted just as a single dice (+d6!, for example) with you’ll add to the d20!.

Yes, No, and Maybe Skills

Players will use Skills to try and overcome the Obstacle and, when arbitrating, just be aware that there are Yes, No, and Maybe Skills.

Yes Skills are Skills that 100% apply to the Obstacle. For instance, Mathematics when trying to cheat a person with some complicated formulas or Move Silently when trying to sneak behind a guard.

No Skills are Skills that don't apply and will never apply. As much as you might want it, History isn't going to get you up that wall and Monster Craft won't help you understand woman.

Finally, Maybe Skills are Skills that could apply depending on how the player approaches it. This is often a player not having the perfect Skill for it but might have something that could apply, like using Acrobatics to climb a wall instead of Climbing.

In these cases, they're just using the Repurpose Skill Tactic.

It is on the GM to give the final say on where a Skill falls on this spectrum.

Complications & Failures

Failing a Check doesn’t automatically mean that you’re instantly screwed: Failing a single Move Silently Check doesn’t result in the guards catching you. In Bonfire, there are Complications and hard failures.

Every Obstacle will have a number of Complications it can gain before it hard fails. This is called its Failure Threshold. You gain Complications by failing Skill Checks: every 5 points below what you needed to get gives the Obstacle 1 Complication, rounded up (so a failure by 1 - 5 is still considered a Complication).

Complications represent narrative challenges that change the circumstances or something bad that happens that doesn't directly stop the players from getting over the Obstacle or resetting to the start, such as the guard hearing something, or you leaving a clue behind that might give someone a heads up that you're there.

When an Obstacle gets more Complications than its Failure Threshold, it hard fails. Hard failures are when something majorly bad actually happens. This can either be something that prevents you from getting what you want entirely forcing you to try a different tact (you're discovered by the guards), something that resets you to the beginning of an Obstacle (you fall off the wall and landing on the ground) or something that puts you in a much worse position (your lock picks getting worn down). Even after a hard failure, you can sometimes try again, like in the case of falling off the wall.

Failure doesn’t diminish during the Skill Challenge, but it does reset for a new one.

Complications in the Narrative

Complications don’t mean that the Skill Challenge grinds to a halt while the players keep reattempting that Skill: instead, it should represent the players coming closer to danger.

For a quick example, let’s say that there’s an adventurer, we’ll call him New York Smith, York for short, who is attempting to jump a chasm, which has a Failure Threshold of 1.

He rolls his Check but fails by 2, giving the Obstacle 1 Complication. In this case, he obviously jumped but also didn’t make it. What does that mean? Well, York didn’t quite make it but managed to grab the ledge as he fell, so he didn’t fall to his death, but he isn’t safe either.

York is grabbing hold of the ledge so he can't make another Jumping Check, but he does reason that he can use Climbing. Makes sense to the GM so they roll again. If York fails this time, he falls, but if he succeeds, he manages to scramble up the cliff, safe on the other side, so he better hope he put Ranks in Climbing.

Success & Boons

All that is required to overcome an Obstacle is 1 successful Check, although clever players who can think their way around a problem can, of course, bypass an Obstacle in a different way.

GMs might find it useful to break Obstacles up into Skill Challenges based on the level of granularity they're looking for in their game.

For instance, if players are trying to scale a cliff, the GM can handle it in two ways:

The first is if the cliff isn't a major part of story: she wants to represent the difficulty of climbing the cliff but doesn't want to spend a lot of time on it. In this case, she'll just have a single Obstacle, "The Cliff", that she assigns a Difficulty to and has the players make Checks against.

The second is if the cliff is a big part of the game (perhaps it's a metaphor for one of the players). In this case, she's break it into Obstacles based on the different sections the players will have to climb: a long stretch (not hard so it's more a test of endurance), an overhang (a particularly difficult bit), a part where the players need to jump from one handhold to another (not difficult but very little margin for error).

She can then, from there, figure out difficulty and maybe some unique Complications and Fail states based on the Skills and equipment the players use as well as things about that particular Obstacle. For instance, Complications and Failure on the long stretch might result in damage from fatigue or scares, resulting in Stress rather than falling, while failing on the overhang results in another Obstacle, "Catching the Following Character", that can be attempted by only players below that character. Additionally, the jump might cause a Complication where the player falls but the rope catches, leaving the player dangling away from the cliff face: they're unable to grab ahold of a hold on the cliff but could possible swing themselves back or climb up their rope, a harder but doable alternative.

Boons

These are the opposite of Complications: they give you a small benefit or boost for accomplishing a test extra well. You get 1 Boon for every 5 full points above what you needed.

Possible Boons might be:

  • Removing Stress
  • Accomplishing the task faster
  • Gaining Relaxation (1 per Boon)
  • Getting free Prep on the next, similar Check
  • Giving a small bonus to the next person (+2 per Boon)
  • Gaining additional insight
  • Finding a secret

Fallout

It is assumed that, during a Challenge, a character is working their hardest to win, therefore, every Check, whether successful or not, causes d4! Stress unless it was the GM who specifically called for it.

However, a Boon can be spent to not gain this Stress.

Time Scale

Like Confrontations, Challenges don’t have a set time scale they work off of, however, unlike Confrontations, once their time scale is set, there’s very little variation.

So, for instance, breaking into a house could have a time scale of a couple of hours, while tracking a bandit could have a scale of days, weeks, or months.

Sometimes an Obstacle will be on a longer or shorter time scale but that should be handled on a case-by-case basis.

For instance, while tracking Corwin, the characters find themselves lost in the old mines. This is an Obstacle that will take a few days to overcome.

Later, they come across a chasm that used to have a utility bridge, but it was destroyed after Corwin crossed it, so they need to find another way to get across.

Another Obstacle within the same Challenge but this time it only takes hours to resolve.

Tactics

Assets & Contacts

These represent static pools of knowledge or information that you can pull from if you have time. So, for instance, a book on the subject, or a library are examples of an Asset but also an old friend who used to be a ranger and who could give you a few pointers could also be considered one.

Anything you can think of that might apply might be an Asset you can draw on.

Assets have two stats: their Knowledge and their Organization. Basically, how much they know and how easy that knowledge is to get.

Each of these is given a qualifier of either Poor, Average, or Good.

For Knowledge, Poor represents a +3, Average +6, and Good +9 while for Organization Poor is d4! Days, Average is d4! Hours, and Good is d4! Minutes.

Spending sufficient time with an Asset (as determined by their Organization) allows you to employ their Knowledge as a bonus on a relevant Check.

That bonus can then be redeemed on a Skill Check later on. This can also be applied to Weapon, Armor, and Shield Skills through manuals and sparring.

You don’t automatically know the Knowledge or the Time Investment of an Asset.

Another benefit of Assets is that it is often multifaceted and can be used to get different bonuses. That old ranger friend can help you with navigation but also probably knows a thing or two about botany.

Assets can also be increased and expanded upon as well, increasing its qualifiers. A good relationship with a contact might give you information quicker, donating to the local library may increase its selection, or hiring excavators to dig up a ruin may unearth more artifacts that help you decipher that ancient script.

There may be some special cases where an Asset will take care of itself, but you may have to check back on it, such as Snaring in the forest. You set your trap and then a day or two later, you can go back and check it to see if you caught anything - in the meantime, you can get other work done.

Redeeming Contacts

Characters get a number of starting contacts based on their Presence, with the expectation they'll gain more as they adventure, but these starting contacts can prove useful as they can be redeemed at any time and defined on the spot.

So, if the players need, for example, access to a monastery’s library, one of the players can remember that one of his buddies is actually a monk there and might be able to help, redeeming one of his contacts.

After that, of course, contacts can be used time and time again: they're part of the world.

How a contact helps is largely determined by the circumstances of why they were redeemed but the GM should, for the most part, consider them Assets starting with Poor Knowledge and Organization. A player can increase that when they redeem a contact by sacrificing additional contacts, raising one of their stats on a one-for-one basis.

So, a character with 3 unredeemed contacts can bring one in as a smuggler buddy who knows a lot about the local underworld, which would normally only cost him 1 of his contacts, but, instead, he sacrifices his 2 remaining ones to increase his smuggler buddy's Knowledge to Good. He can't redeem any more contacts but at least his smuggler buddy knows what he's talking about.

Roleplaying Out a Scene vs Using Assets

Assets are not meant to replace specific roleplaying areas where you need specific pieces of information: they’re meant to reward players for remembering to do otherwise boring and mundane things that might still help their character. You can also combine the two.

For instance, if the players are hunting a griffon, they might consider talking to a local monster hunter, who, though a scene, tells them specifics of how the griffon hunts and fights. During this time, the players learn that griffons tend to nest in mountains, so, after the talk is finished, one of the players hits up an old friend who knows a lot about climbing and spends a few hours training and getting points with him.

The first scene is roleplayed in detail while the second is handwaved because there’s nothing specific that the player will learn that will help them. Additionally, the first scene could also give the players a bonus to their Monstercraft roll for remembering additional things about the beast.

Using Other Players as Assets

I think it's completely reasonable that players would want to use each other's characters as Assets and why shouldn't they? So, in this case the player’s bonus is the Rank they have in the applicable Skill with their Organization being based on their Characteristics.

Total the Ranks in Characteristics that relate to teaching, communication, and organization; if less than 3 they’re considered Poor; 4 - 6 to makes them Average; greater than 6, Good.

GMs should also feel free to reward players who sit there and talk things out and plan by awarding the Asset bonus as well, even if they don't spend a full hour+ planning.

Equipment

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch and these are catchall rules for the use of equipment that doesn’t already come with auxiliary benefits, such as weapons (damage) and armor (DR).

Players are assumed to be using all equipment that would help them with a Check if they have time so they shouldn’t feel the need to go through their list of climbing gear every time they scale a wall, although, if time is short and they need to rush, that may be appropriate.

On a Skill Check, you add an extra d6! + the Rank of any applicable Descriptions the equipment might have. If there are multiple pieces of equipment, you increase the dice size by 1 per piece and add their Descriptions as well. More on equipment Descriptions is found in Chapter 6:Equipment.

If the equipment doesn't really suite the purpose you're using it for, like trying to pry open a door with a weapon, you apply a d4! instead, although the same rules apply for stacking pieces of equipment.

Redeeming this bonus causes the tool degrades, giving it (and any other equipment you use) 1 Wear, whether you succeed or not. More on Wear can be found in Chapter 6:Equipment.

Asset- & Equipment-Based Skilled

Some skills are only going to be useful if you have the proper equipment or the right Asset. For instance, if you’re not out in the wilderness, you can’t really use Snaring or if you don’t have the proper tools or a woodshop, Maintenance won’t do you any good. In cases, being able to make the Check in the first place is considered an auxiliary benefit and so this bonus doesn't apply. This will need to be handled on a case-to-case basis.

Equipment as an Obstacle

There is a difference between using equipment to help you take on an Obstacle and using equipment to get around an Obstacle and nowhere is that better demonstrated than with the rope.

A rope can be used to scale a cliff but it can also be scaled instead of the cliff. In the former case, it acts just like normal equipment, giving you a reroll, and in the latter case, you replace the cliff Obstacle with the rope Obstacle, which may or may not be better and may or may not come with its own problems.

Helper Skills

Let’s say that you’re tracking someone in the wilderness. You have Hunting at Rank 4 and Endurance at Rank 3. Seems like being able to move quickly over a longer period of time, should help you catch the guys, and it does.

If you have another Advanced Skill that you think might help you, you can add it as a bonus, however the GM is going to roll an additional d4! for Stress for each Skill you add but they're only going to take the highest. This can't be reduced by Not Sweating It.

Others can also apply their Advanced Skills to your roll with the same penalty, assuming they're in contact with you and can give you a helping hand.

This may seem like it is nearly identical to Preparation, and they are very close. The difference is that Preparation is a general thing that applies to a Skill Check you’re going to make in the future, while using a helper Skill is in the moment and is specific to the scenario. You can use both at the same time, since the help Skill gives a bonus and Preparation gives a reroll.

Skill Mobbing

For GMs, note that Helper Skills are how you handle Skill Mobbing, where everyone just overpowers a roll by all rolling against it. Instead, only one person rolls and only those with an applicable Advanced Skill and willing to take that Stress add their Rank to help out.

Multi-Tasking

Let’s be realistic here: you’re probably going to end up trying to do two things at once, like climbing a wall and doing so quietly.

If that’s the case, you’re going to have to “split” your d20! into smaller dice and assign those to each of the Checks you’re trying to make, because your attention is divided. The total max value of the dice can never exceed 20 but it can be lower than it. The other side will still be rolling full d20!s against you.

So, if you’re climbing a wall and trying to do so quietly, you’d have to make a Climbing and a Move Silently Check. You could split it like so: d12! for Climbing and d8! for Move Silently, or d10! for Climbing and d10! for Move Silently, or d12!+d4! for Move Silently and d4! for Climbing.

Or you could just say “screw being quiet” and roll a d20! for Climbing and take a 0 for Move Silently. In this case, the GM would just roll against your Skill Rank. So, if you choose to ignore Move Silently but have a Rank 4 in it, the GM would roll their dice, and you can hope they don’t roll higher than 4.

Either way, besides this, it works like a normal Check, except that, for the Stress gain, you're going to roll 1d4! for each Skill they're attempting but only use the highest if applicable. They need to succeed all Checks by 5 to completely remove the Stress gain.

Not Paying Attention

Multi-Tasking is also used when someone isn't paying attention, such as a guard who's on watch but he's been there for several hours at this point so he's not doing it so closely. In this case, start at a d10!, lowering it further as needs be.

Helping Others

It feels good to help people and Bonfire is all about group cooperation. The fighter helps the skill monkey survive combat and the skill monkey helps the fighter not look like an idiot while doing detective work.

And how you help other characters will really depend on the situation. Perhaps your Preparation can be used to help another person hide or perhaps you have a bit of knowledge that they don’t.

Really, this will be done to what feels right in the moment, but this note is here to draw attention to someone helping you as an Asset and someone adding their Rank to yours with a Helper Skill, and how they’re different.

And the difference is time. If you have plenty of time to coach someone while they’re climbing a cliff face, you’d be an Asset and you wouldn't take Stress.

But if you’re being chased by the guards and it’s a matter of seconds or minutes, you’d add a Helper Skill, and you would need to take the Stress.

Note that if the skill monkey is also trying to climb the cliff face and coach as they go, that would be considered a d10! worth of effort for Multi-Tasking, though you don’t have to actually roll to see if you help.

Not Sweating It

While the default is to assume that characters actually care about the outcome of the Check, that's not always the case since not everything is equally important. In the cases of lesser importance, the character can simply not sweat it.

In this case, they take no Stress from the Check but take a -4 penalty.

Preparation

Preparation is anything your character does to help a specific Skill Check that will be made in the future (even if that future is 2 seconds from now). Note that this doesn't have to be a Check you make: it can also apply to an ally's Check (if it makes sense).

Usually, these only take a couple of seconds but can vary widely depending on the circumstances. Throwing a rock is a matter of moments while constructing a gilly suit will take a lot longer.

As long as there is time, there is no chance of failure or, if the players have plenty of time, Preparation just happens. Mark what you did for later in the Skill Challenge.

However, if there is a time crunch or a chance of failure, Preparation works like a normal Skill Check with success means the preparation is actually useful.

Either way, you only get one Prep Check per Advanced Skill that applies for each upcoming Check, unless you have Assets but remember that they do take time.

That means that if you know you're going to have to make a jump, you can use Acrobatics to stretch, Endurance to do a few practice sprints to the edge, and Sports (long jumping) to remember techniques all to prep for the same Jump check.

When you redeem Preparation that you’ve readied, you can reroll the Obstacle's d20, taking the worse of the two results.

Planning & Action Phases

While it isn’t entirely black and white, it’s helpful to think of Challenges as composing two phases: planning and action; the laying out the plan and then the heist.

Planning is gathering intel, getting your team and equipment together, and planning your route of attack. This is where you’ll use Preparation and Assets mostly.

Action phase is when you’ll be doing a lot of the Checks and Stress, as well as equipment and companions - using what you learned in the Prep Phase to help you get through the sticky parts.

But don’t get too hung up on separating these ideas though. Even during the heist, you might find it worth your time to sit on your heels and take your time while doing some Preparation as there will almost certainly be problems that crop up that you need to adjust to.

Repurpose Skill

Sometimes you have a Skill that kind of works but it's the perfect Skill for that situation: in that case, you can repurpose the kind-of Skill, applying it's Rank to the roll, as normal, but getting an extra d4! Stress. This Stress can't be reduced by Not Sweating It.

Rushing It

If you’re in a hurry to get the job down, you can just rush it. This lowers the time a Check actually makes but gives you a -5.

The bonus for this is that Complications you would get from the Check don't get added to Universal or Linked Obstacles in the Challenge, though Hard Failures do add 2 Complications to them.

Chapter 4Challenges

Favor

Favor is a measure of your standing with a given god. It’s not an absolute measure, however, but more a measure of the chance that a Divine will answer your prayer. How far your Favor goes ranges widely based on how devoted you are to a god and how Honor you are.

Invoking Miracles

Now that you’ve got a nice stash of Favor, you probably want to spend it. That’s pretty simple: pray out loud for the Miracle in question. Silent prayers don’t work because no Divine is going to answer a prayer, they’re not going to get credit for.

For every Favor you spend, you get another use of your Honor Dice but comes with a few differences: the first, and most major, is that you can spend it on ANYTHING - you want to run faster or jump higher? You want to heal your wounds? You want to attack slightly faster? You can with Favor, however, the effects are only temporary, of course.

The second is that you can spend to change the world around you. Every Favor you spend can change a 5 ft square area with the following restriction: it can’t directly contradict things that have already been said.

For instance, you cannot wish to have an avalanche suddenly on the steppe or an inn to appear before the party that wasn’t there before, but you could wish for a flash flood or for an inn to be over the next rise, which was just out of view when you prayed.

Restrictions

Miracles do come with several restrictions.

Covenant

Miracles are bound by Covenant: you can’t pray for something your Divine doesn’t have power over, so a Divine of the city can’t really help you in the mountains, for example.

But this is where being in a pantheon does help out: you can pray to another Divine in your pantheon for their blessing instead, but it requires twice as much Favor, and you don’t gain the benefit of your Cosmic Link.

Room for Doubt

Another restriction is that the Miracles need to be subtle: no angels coming down from the skies to keep you from falling to your death but perhaps there’s water or a passing hay cart that catches you just in time to soften your landing. There needs to be room for doubt.

Banking Honor Dice

The final restriction is that the Honor Dice must be spent on what was prayed for and they must all be spent before the end of the session (the Divines being fickle with those who keep them waiting). Otherwise they’re lost.

This can mean that they’re temporarily banked but it doesn’t mean that you can spend them freely.

Miraculous Healing

Servants can invoke miracles to heal their companions if their Covenant is specifically healing related.

However, the number that it rolled is subtracted from the days a wound has left to heal, not from the damage of the wound.

Acquiring Favor

Servants turn their eyes from the earth below to higher worlds, both literally and metaphorically. In this way, they’re the keeper of the times and seasons, as well as the calendar to note the occurrence of Holy Days.

As such, players who play Servants will need to keep track of the passage of time. Doing so gives them special bonuses according to the schedule laid out below.

You’ll note that it follows the Gregorian Calendar layout with holy days happening according to the Judeo-Christian traditions. The assumption that the characters will be following an in-game calendar with random Holy Days specific to their cult but we’re “translating” into the Gregorian calendar, in the same way that we assume the characters are speaking in their native fantasy tongues and not in English.

Doing this makes it easier for players to keep track because the calendar is a familiar shape. They won’t have to track out a whole year and remember that Beltane is on the 5th of Seed’s Row.

Rules for using your own calendar and keeping all Servants relatively balanced against each other is going to be the Deluxe Rules.

Favor is unique to the Divine you’re petitioning. Gaining Favor with another Divine, means that you lose an equal amount of Favor with all others because gods are bad at sharing.

Daily Sacrifices

Every Day

These are small, daily sacrifices. These tend to cost about 1 or 2 sc, 5 to 6 points of Stress, or 1 or 2 Vitality, depending on your Divine's preferences. They can be the sacrifice of a small, sacred animal, the burning of a portion of your meal, or the lighting of incense during morning prayers.

Servants

These sacrifices net 1 Favor for Servants of that Divine.

Anointed

The anointed gain 1 Honor for observing these sacrifices.

Unanointed

Nothing initially. An unanointed character that strictly observes their daily sacrifice can gain a free Devotion to the faith; the first step to becoming anointed.

Sabbatic Holy Days

Every 7 Days

This is a day of rest and contemplation, where the faithful are instructed to put aside the things of the world and focus on the Divine with activities related to their Covenant.

There is an emphasis on ritual over practicality to emphasize this divide, so while a Divine of Battle encourages his followers to fight every day, on his sabbath, he only wants them to focus on ritualistic combat and controlled bloodshed, as opposed to actual combat.

Again, this is to remind his followers of the holiness of the action and by forcing his followers to stop and think about their actions, he forces them to contemplate the deeper realities of combat.

The sabbath takes up 1 to 3 hours and the rest of the day can be spent at the faithful wants.

Servants

Servants who observe the sabbath of their Divine gain all Favor back.

Anointed

Anointed worshippers make a d20! + Pres Check Mod + applicable Religion Rank vs d20! + 6.

Success gives them all their Favor back.

Unanointed

Unanointed worshippers gain nothing.

Lunar Holy Days

4 Sabbatic holy days

Where normal Sabbaths are days of rest, Lunar Holy Days are about sacrifices and consecration, which are thematically appropriate to the cult’s Covenant. Those observing the holy day need to stop and spend the whole day or they gain no benefit.

Servants

Servants gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their Pres Check Mod (although this can never penalize them).

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Lunar Holy Day.

Anointed

The anointed gain all Favor back.

Unanointed

Unanointed who celebrate the Lunar Holy Day gain 1 Favor.

Solar Holy Days

Every 3 Lunar Holy Days

These holy days celebrate the turning of the seasons and where the other festivals can be severe, with fasting and reading and nerd stuff like that, Solar Holy Days are about feasting and letting loose a bit. The Divines demand their faithful to party all day.

The anointed faithful gain their full Favor back and the unanointed gain their Min Favor from Presence if they roll a 4 or better on their Honor die adding one Devotion related to that Divine

Servants

Servants gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their applicable Devotion.

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Solar Holy Day.

Anointed

The anointed gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their Pres Check Mod (although this can never penalize them).

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Lunar Holy Day.

Unanointed

Unanointed worshippers make a d20! + Pres Check Mod + applicable Religion Rank vs d20! + 6.

Success gives them all their Favor back.

Annual Holy Days

Every 4 Solar Holy Days

The Annual Holy Day either celebrates the new year or an important event in the cult, such as the birth of their messiah or the founding of their faith. It is a 2 day affair and is often occupied by feasting, gift giving, public rituals, and other such nonsense.

Servants and Anointed followers gain all Favor back with Servants also gaining extra Favor equal to their Devotion and the Anointed followers gaining extra Favor equal to a roll of their Honor die. For Servants, this extra Favor is reset on the next Annual Holy Day (which means you can’t stockpile it). The unanointed gain 1 Favor.

Servants

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Annual Holy Day.

Anointed

The anointed gain all Favor back plus extra Favor equal to their applicable Devotion.

This extra Favor can take them over their normal max and lasts until the next Annual Holy Day.

Unanointed

Unanointed worshippers gain all their Favor back.

Alm Scripts

Special

An alm script is a small prayer, inscribed on a piece of vellum. When burnt, the burner receives an amount of temporary Favor equal to what is invested in the alm script

This Favor can bring a person over their Max Favor but it has to be used immediately in with a prayer that is uttered when the script is burnt.

Creating Alm Scripts

Any Servant can create an alm script, however, few outside of chantries and monasteries do.

It requires 20 sc of special materials (consecrated inks of various colors, silver-inlaid quills, and vellum made from an animal sacred to the Divine) and a day’s worth of work where the Servant only works on transcribing the script.

For every 10 Favor the Servant spends while making the script, 1 Favor is invested in the script at the end of the process.

Chantries and monasteries will produce alm scripts as a way of supplementing their income (see the Alm Script entry in the Goods, Adventuring table of Chapter 6: Equipment).

Taking an Oath

Special

Not a sacrifice or holy day parse, Taking an Oath is essentially getting Favor on loan from the Divines for the promise that, if you get out the bad situation you're in, you'll make a huge sacrifice to them soon after.

By Taking an Oath, you gain a die worth of Favor for every 20% of treasure you gain from the adventure you promise to the Divine, should you survive.

This die is equal to your Honor dice if you’re anointed to the Divine you’re petitioning.

The Unanointed must also promise to become Anointed in addition to the cash and goods, in which case they gain a d6! worth of Favor. This Favor can put you over your max but must be spent on a Miracle immediately.

The Divines are extremely litigious. If you don't fulfill your oath in 1 month + 1 for every Honor Category higher than Average Honor you're in, they will seek vengeance.

You may no longer gain any Favor with any Divine that shares a pantheon with the one you're in and what you do have will decay, causing you to lose 1 Favor per day.

Priests of the Divine will also condemn you as an oath breaker, a very serious accusation, causing you to drop 1 Honor Category per month and preventing you from increasing your Honor.

Should you fall into Dishonor, you gain the Excommunicated Flaw.

Chapter 5The Divine

What is Weird?

Seems like a reasonable place to start. It is called a lot of things by different cultures: mana, magic, juju, but essentially, it’s the metaphysical power that allows people to reshape reality, which is why working with it is often called weirdshaping, or simply shaping, if you don’t want to sound pretentious.

Weird lies beneath the surface of all things, a hidden power that imbues life with those indescribable feelings. It is the reason you feel attracted to someone at first sight, are speechless when you see a beautiful view, or the reason why planets have healing properties. It is not that some things are inherently ‘magical’ or ‘non-magical’ - all things have weird that is waiting to be shaped.

Weird Traditions

Ways of shaping the Weird are split up into different “Traditions”, which with their own bent on ways the Weird can manifest. These are:

Animism

This Tradition focuses on the manipulation of the body, forcing it to endure beyond its normal limits and making it into a weapon.

Calling

This Tradition focuses on the Realms beyond the Middle one we’re so fond of. It involves the summoning of demons, elementals, and spirits.

Charm

This focuses on the manifestation of the Weird in its raw and primal form. This allows the Weird to interact with our world in a more physical way.

Glamour

This Tradition focuses on the manipulation of the senses, tricking the brain into seeing what the weird-adept wants you to see.

Investiture

This focuses on inanimate objects and either imbuing them with power or manifesting the latent potential that is already in them.

Occultism

This Tradition focuses on the hidden world and opening of the third mind: the divination of knowledge and the unseen.

Sortilege

This Tradition focuses on fate, chance, and probability: more specifically, it focuses on messing with it so that things go in your favor.

Rudiments & Spells

When you shape the weird, it manifests in the Middle Realm as a spell, which is broken up into different parts or “Rudiments”, which are split into types. More details on the specifics are found under the Acquiring Rudiments section below but here’s a brief overview:

Shape

This is the shape that the spell shows up in. This can be a circle, cone, or wall, for example.

Range

This depends on the Shape of the spell but generally it’s the reach of the spell: how long is the cone or how far can you launch a salvo shaped spell.

Origin

This is where the spell comes into being: this can start as the weird-adept and emanate out, it can imminent from his touch, or it can manifest yards away from him.

Effect

This is where the spell usually gets its name and it’s what it actually does. A spell can cause Stress, remove friction, change appearances, and so on. Effects will also list the Interval, which will talk about next, and the base Range for that spell.

Effects are also special because you can add multiple Effects (or even the same effect multiple times, thus making it more powerful) to the same spell but increasing the Drain in the same amount. If it isn’t clear how an Effect would work if Stacked with itself, there’s a Stacking section for each Effect that should clear things up.

Shaping a Spell

Each of a spell’s Rudiments will have a Drain value, which added together, is how much that spell costs to cast. A spell must have at least 1 of each Rudiment type.

When you first shape a spell (on the second you receive your SP - although, if you don't have the spell Rudiments set, the GM is free to move the count along), you subtract its Drain from the number of SPs you have at that moment and the spell lasts for an amount of time equal to its Interval.

Interval

However, if you have SP left over at the end of that Interval, you can keep the spell up. Again subtracting the Drain from the number of SP you have.

If you have at least 1 SP when the Interval comes up but don't have enough to cover the entire Drain cost, you can continue to keep the spell up for 1 more Interval if you take the amount of SP you're missing in Stress. This is called "Weirdburn". You can only do it once per spell.

If you choose to stop the spell at the Interval, any left over SP are lost.

You cannot stop a spell that has been shaped until it reaches its Interval. Additionally, if you've gained SP since shaping a spell, that SP has to be put into a new spell; it cannot be used to renew a spell at its Interval that's already been shaped.

Stacking Different Effects & Interval If you're stacking multiple, different Effects, you use the shortest Interval to determine what the Interval for the spell is.

Shape Rudiment Descrptions

Circle

Drain 2

The Circle is the most basic Shape. It extends from a point within the Origin with a Radius equal to the modified Range of the Effect. Anyone in the Circle is affected by the spell only as long as they stay within it. If it deals damage or inflicts Stress, this occurs on the Interval or if a person enters/re-enters the shape.

Chain

Drain 0

This is the most primal Shape a spell can come in. The weird-adept casts the spell and it chooses 1 viable, random target within the Range of the Spell. After half the spell’s Interval, it then leaps to the next. Anyone hit by the spell suffers its Effects for the entire time the spell is cast.

People may be hit multiple times, which simply counts as the spell Stacking on them. The weird-adept may try and redirect the spell, causing it to skip over a person. This means that the target is randomly deteremined again, although everyone in the Shape of the spell is still an option. They take Stress equal to the number of times they’ve redirected the Chain each time they do so.

Cone

Drain 3

The Cone starts with its tip at a point within the Origin and then extends in a 45-degree arch from it up to the spell’s Range. Anyone within the Cone is affected by the spell for as long they're in its Shape. If it deals damage or inflicts Stress, this occurs on the Interval or if a person enters/re-enters the shape.

The Cone can be rotated around its Origin, moving a facing every 3 seconds. This takes no effort or concentration from the Weird-Adept.

Personal

Drain 2

This makes the Shape of a spell just a single individual. Starting at a point within the Origin, it effects the first viable target (deteremined by the Effect Rudiment) and then stops. The spell continues to affect that person after that point, wherever they move.

Salvo

Drain 5

The Salvo is the spell chucked up to 3 * its unmodified Range from a point within the spell’s Origin. It requires a ranged attack to hit (though ignoring Cover) a single viable target. The shaper gains a bonus based on the Range Rudiment they're using: +0 for Personal, +1 for Half, +3 for Full, +5 for Double. Anyone that is hit, suffers the full effects of the spell for the duration of the spell. If it deals damage or inflicts Stress, this occurs on the Interval.

If a shield wouldn't be a viable target for the spell, the defender can use its cover value.

On a miss, it follows the normal ranged miss rules.

This Shape can be stacked with itself to fire additional Salvos that can target different targets. If targeting the same target, they target needs to make multiple defense rolls.

Sphere

Drain 5

The spell takes on the shape of a 2 ft diameter sphere that flies around, coming into being at a point within the spell’s Origin. While the weird-adept has complete control over the direction with only minimal effort (so it doesn’t affect what else he’s doing), it still has some limitations. The first is that it only goes 10 ft / second, no faster, no slower, and the weird-adept may only turn it 45 degrees every 3 seconds. It also travels alone the ground.

The second is that the sphere needs to stay in the shaper's eyeline. If the eyeline is broken for most than 1 second, the sphere stops. Regaining view of the sphere will give the shaper control again. Otherwise, the distance at which the sphere is controls is unlimited.

Ray

Drain 3

The ray is a thin, straight line of magic that extends from a point within the spell’s Origin up to the spell’s Range. This is modified by Range Rudiments. It manifests in a single instant, requiring a roll to hit. This cannot be Parried and equipment is also part of the target. Those that it hits, suffer the full Effect of the spell for the duration of the spell.

If no one was hit, the weird-adept can manifest another ray on each following second until either a target is hit or the spell ends. Note that the Interval is always ticking; a target struck on a follow-up attempt will be affected for a shorter duration than one struck on the initial attempt.

The shaper can hit multiple viable targets with one Ray if they're in line with each other but he has to do so simultaneously. Non-viable targets, besides equipment that the viable targets are wearing, block the Ray or provide Cover: otherwise the Ray passes right through everything else.

Wall

Drain 2

The wall is a 1-ft-thick line that starts at a point within the spell’s Origin and extends up to its Range, but also into the air and below the ground up to 50 ft. It also doesn’t have to be a straight line, weaving, twisting and turning, however, no part of it can get within 5 ft of another part. It must also be anchored to the ground. The wall cannot be moved once placed unless it has an Aura Origin. There is no way to increase that height or thickness.

Anyone passing through the wall is affected by the Spell for the rest of the duration of the spell. Those that are in the wall’s way when it is shaped, get a defense roll vs a ranged attack to get out of the way.

Illumination

For sake of simplicity, there’s five categorizations of how much light there is in an area:

Blinding

This is like staring into the sun. It's very painful and not very pleasant.

Assuming that you cover your eyes, you'll be acting as if you're blind. Even then, a moment's exposure even temporarily results in a d10! second delay as you try to recover. If you continue to expose yourself to this level of illumination, you'll be unable to act at all and will do blind in a number of seconds equal to your Con.

Bright

This is the level of light of starting into a fire or just having your eyes near it. It sucks but it probably won't do permanent damage.

This doesn't really affect your aim or anything like that, but it does make it difficult to focus on things, delaying any action by 1d4! seconds as you try and squint and see what you're doing.

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a dice size 1 size smaller and take longer.

Normal

This is normal daylight and occurs no operating penalties.

Dim

This the equivalent of a dark room or nighttime. There’s some visibility; the character can make out shapes and shadows but no fine details.

In melee combat, those in dim-lighting defend and attack with a dice size 1 size smaller and using ranged weapons is even more of challenge. However, if you insist, you’ll be attacking with a dice size 4 sizes smaller (normally, a d6!).

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a dice size 1 size smaller.

Additionally, Sprinting is impossible without acquiring a Stress per second moved.

Dark

This is pitch black with the character not even being able to see their hands in front of their faces. This is a dungeon after the torch goes out.

Combat becomes difficult as anyone in darkness defends and attacks with a dice size 4 sizes smaller (normally, a d6!) And ranged combat is right out, but, if you insist, you’ll be attacking with a dice size 5 sizes smaller (normally, a d4!).

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a dice size 4 sizes smaller (normally, a d6!).

And moving above Strolling speed is extremely nerve racking, netting a Stress per Speed Category per second. So, moving at a Run for 3 seconds gives you 9 Stress (3 for the Run for 3 seconds).

Your character isn’t immortal, or at least, they didn’t start out that way, and so still needs to fulfil their basic needs of food, drink, and sleep. Without those basic requirements, he may find himself a bit more lethargic or, worse case scenario, dead.

There are three things your character needs to take care of, as mentioned above: Food, Water, and Sleep, and three levels associated with each, Normal, Rationing, and Going Without, which related to how much you have of each.

If a person sleeps the normal amount (assumed to be 8 hours), eats a healthy amount of food, and drinks a good amount of water, they take no damage or Stress.

However, if they cut back on any of three (called rationing) or don't get any sleep, food, or water (called going without), they take the associate damage and/or Stress as noted by the table.

This is on 1 Wound per need but does stack: having no food and water gives you 2d6! damage and d6! Stress.

Additionally, a Wound doesn't heal until the associated need is returned to normal.

Stat Score Damage

Stat Score damage occurs in several places and it’s fairly straightforward. Then why have this section, you ask? As a quick aside to keep the game moving.

The sudden change in ability is incredibly unnerving, meaning that until the person can stop, take a breath and re-adjust to their new reality (meaning the player can adjust the stats on his character sheet without breaking off Combat, a Confrontation, a Challenge or any narrative tension the GM has built up), he gets a penalty of 1 for each point lost to anything that Stat Score influences.

So, a -3 to Dexterity would net a temporary -3 to Attack, and Defense, +3 to Initiative, all while reducing the Confrontation Die by 3 sizes, if applicable.

If the amount drained is more than the score, the person becomes appropriately incapacitated, but not dead, until the Stat Score damage is healed.
