Welcome to Bonfire Character Creation! You'll notice that there's a lot of steps and, if you're familiar with other TTRPGs, that it's a bit longer and a bit more complicated than other games. Why is that?

The first is that it gives you, the player, more choice. Do you want to build a cunning fighter than actually using their brains instead of their brawns to defeat others? Do you want to play a sly diplomat that crushes her opponents' morale without even lifting a finger? Or perhaps you want to play a power wizard that concocts his spells on the fly, building the perfect one for each scenario?

More importantly, do you want those different characters to actually feel different when you play them? So that convincing some actually feels different than just beating them to death?

This is the first thing that Bonfire offers you.

The second thing is that it allows you to be surprised during creation: through your choices and rolls, you'll get a more fully formed character than in other systems. You'll get what you want with extra twists that help add interest and depth to your character that you wouldn't get in other systems.

And not only that, but you'll also have those choices, twists, and depth mean something in play. Is your character brash? You'll be rewarded for playing that way in the game.

So, what are you waiting for? Let's get started!

Step 1: Roll Stats

Your character has 6 attributes that define your character physically and mentally. These are Strength (Str), Dexterity (Dex), Constitution (Con), Intellect (Int), Willpower (Will), and Pre (Presence).

Strength

Strength (Str) represents your character’s pure brawn. How much can she lift and carry? How strong are her punches?

It governs the base damage you do, how much you can carry, and strength-based skills.

Dexterity

Dexterity (Dex) is a measure of how agile your character is. Can she dodge arrows, or can she execute a graceful pirouette? Dexterity is crucial for avoiding blows in combat or traps in a hallway.

It covers how accurate you are, how well you can dodge, how quickly you get up in moving in situations, and dexterity-based skills.

Constitution

Constitution (Con) represents your character’s overall health and toughness. Can he shrug off the Dancing Death or does he succumb to a head cold? Can she take a solid blow in combat and not even blink? These are the things Constitution oversees.

It controls your base Vitality, the amount of minimum Vitality at every other level up, and how much damage you can suck down without going comatose, and constitution-based skills.

Intellect

Intellect (Int) measures your character’s raw knowledge: how much data can he remember? Can she do mathematical calculations or remember who the Elector of Stanitz is? Intellect doesn’t help in applying that knowledge, but it does give you knowledge to draw on.

It governs how accurate you are, how many CrP you get on level up, and intellect-based skills (which are a lot of them).

Willpower

Willpower (Will) is applied knowledge. Does she process information quicker and learn from other’s mistakes? Does he understand life is more than just a set of numbers of probabilities? Can she see other’s viewpoints? How well can she overcome adversity or keep her ground?

It controls your starting Stress Threshold, how likely you are to get hit, how quickly you get up and moving in situations, and willpower-based skills.

Presence

Presence (Pre) represents force of personality. Does he bend others to his will? Is she the focus of any room she enters? Do people look up to them or what to be him? Presence is not looks or even likeability: it represents this animal magnetism that people follow.

Presence covers how much bonus Favor you get when worshipping the Divines, your starting Honor, how strong your Traits are, how many contacts you start the game with, how many followers you can have, and presence-based skills.

A Note About Low Stats

Bonfire is specifically balanced for you to roll 3d6 in order, with 10 being the average result but don't worry about feeling like you have low scores or they're not in the order you want: you'll have a chance to rearrange and modify them later.

But even if you have objectively lower stats than the rest of the company, you'll still be able to contribute in a meaningful way in your own area of expertise because Bonfire is built as a team game, so nobody is going to be good at everything.

In Bonfire, you'll almost always be weaker than your opponent: what makes a hero is not supernatural abilities, but the simple fact that he fights longer and harder for what he believes in than other men.

Step 2: Receive CrP

Alright, you have your scores. Now it’s time to determine how many Creation Points (or CrP, for short) you’ll start with. These CrP will be spent on modifying your character, buying them their skills and increasing their Scores. They may also be spent on rerolls at certain steps.

Even still, you may want to switch your stats around. This may be because you have almost the right stats, but you have a specific character concept in mind. For instance, using the example stats in Step 1, your party may need a diplomatic character more than a strong character. In this case, it would really help to swap Strength and Presence.

You can freely switch around your stats as need but, in that case, you only receive 25 CrPs.

If you switch only 4 of your stats, you receive 50 CrPs.

If you switch 2, you receive 75 CrPs.

And if you keep your stats the way they are, with no changes, you gain 100 CrPs to work with.

Step 3: Select Race

Your character’s race will change how they interact with the world and what they’ll be good and bad at so pick carefully.

Details are found in Chapter 2:: Races so go there and pick one and come back here when you’re done.

Step 4: Select Archetypes

Your character has an area of expertise represented by two Archetypes.

You choose a Primary and a Secondary Archetype and pay the price in CrP for both based on your race’s Archetype Cost section. Descriptions of the different Archetypes are found in Chapter 3:: Archetypes along with more details on how to combine them.

If you’ve chosen to reroll your stats and have no CrPs to work with, congratulations, you’re now in CrP debt and need to make it up by the end of Character Creation.

You must select two Archetypes before going further.

Step 5: Roll Flaws

All heroes have flaws. That’s what makes them relatable and human (even if they’re an orc). Character Flaws earn you additional CrP, which is great. Flaws can also be used in Confrontations to further the story.

For a list of Flaws flip to Chapter 4:: Flaws.

If you’ve gone into CrP debt and you’re not out of it by the end of this stage, you’ll need to go back and reselect your Archetypes so that you are or go back to you starting set of stats and begin Character Creation all over again with them.

Step 6: Adjust Stats

Now, it is time to do the final adjustments to your stats. This isn't your final adjustments: you'll have a chance to increase them again level up.

The price to increase a Stat is the Stat + 10. So, to get a Stat to 9 from 8, it would cost 18 CrP.

This can't bring a stat above 20.

Levels of Induction & Max Favor

There are 3 levels of commitment to a cult, that determines how often you get Favor and how much Favor you can have:

Unanointed

These are the lowest level: those outside the cult and its mysteries. They gain no benefits of any kind.

Anointed

These are those that are the lay members of the faith. They gain small bonuses to their max Favor for the deity they’re devoted to. You can only be anointed to one Covenant at a time.

If you have a positive Devotion to a deity, one of their cults, or one of their Covenants, you can choose to begin the game as an anointed to that deity. Otherwise, you can become Anointed during gameplay once you have that Devotion.

You can only be anointed to one deity at a time.

Servants

These are the highest rung of the commitment ladder and serve as the officers of the cult of the god. They’re priests, abbots, hierophants, pontiffs, nazarites, hermits, etc. These are men and women that have taken vows to devote their life to the cult.

In game terms, this means that these are those who have a Servant Archetype as either their Primary or Secondary Archetype.

Step 8: Training & Skills

Trainings and Skills are additional areas of expertise that your character has obtained above his or her race and class.

Both cost CrP and are found in Chapter 5:: Skills & Trainings but, before you rush over there, you need to determine the starting Ranks for your Skill Suites.

Calculating Starting Ranks

Each character starts with 5 Skill Suites. They are:

  • Athletics
  • Lore
  • Streetwise
  • Survival
  • Tactics
  • Trades
  • Weirdcraft

To determine your starting Rank in each, follow the guide below:

  • Your Primary Generalist's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 3.
    • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 3, the other starts at Rank 2, otherwise pick one to start at 3.
    • When you Specialize, you gain Rank 2 in its Main Skill Suite
      • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 2, the other starts at Rank 1, otherwise pick one to start at 2.
  • Your Secondary Generalist's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 2.
    • If you would normally get two, you only get one of them.
    • When you Specialize, you gain Rank 1 in its Main Skill Suite.
      • If you would normally get two, you only get one of them.
  • Finally, you may pick 1 Skill Suite to start at Rank 2.
  • If, for any reason, a Skill Suite doubles up, you only get a +1 to its Rank.
  • All other Skill Suites start at Rank 0.

Note that you get the Advanced Language Skill for free in your native language with its Rank starting at your Int.

Step 9: Flesh Out Personality

Your character also has a personality (shocker) that sets them apart.

What do they want? What do they believe? Who do they like? What do people think of them?

These questions are answered using Goals, Traits, Racial Temperament, Devotions, and Reputation, which we’ll fill out now.

Goals

Goals serve two purposes.

First, they help your GM figure out what you want to do with the character: where you want to take them and what they’re trying to accomplish.

Second, they help you get CrPs that you can then turn around and use to make your character stronger.

A character can have 3 Goals at any one time, although it’s fine if you only start with 1.

You should discuss with your GM what Goals are going to work for the campaign to get on the same page.

Have a Goal that you can continue to work for but also have a Goal(s) that are actionable as you get bonus CrP for completing them.

Traits

Traits are a lot of things.

They can be things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (“I have blonde hair”) or something general (“I have an intimidating demeanor”) or somewhere in between (“I’m an orc”).

They don’t have to be physical in nature. It could be how your character views himself (“I’m confident” or “I’m good at math”). They can also be something about your character that he just can’t explain (“I’ve always been good with dogs” or “I can read people easily”).

You get a number of Traits based on your Int.

Devotions

What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god? How does he feel about that thing?

Relationships between individuals don’t need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Devotion.

Everyone receives two free Devotions: one to their character’s clan/family and another of their choice. Each Devotion comes with a "relationship verb" and the thing itself. So, his clan Devotion could be "Hate Clan" or "Have mixed feelings about Clan" or "Loyal to Clan"

These Devotions start a 2.

Purchasing Devotions

You can also increase your Devotions or purchase new ones as well.

All new Devotions start at 1 and cost 4 CrP to purchase. From there, you can increase their size which costs 6 + (2 * your current Devotion). So, to go from a 2 to a 3 Devotion, it'll cost you 10 CrP ( 6 + ( 2 * 2 ) ).

Reputation

Finally, Reputation, which acts like a Trait but is controlled by your GM.

Reputation, in a nutshell, is what your character is known for and how other people see them. So, if you want to convince someone that you’ll keep your word, having a Reputation for doing so will help you out.

Reputation takes the form of 3 incomplete sentences starting with “I’m known for...”. Each time you finish a period of training, your GM will finish or change out one or more of those sentences and assign points to them that represent their strength. She’ll choose how to finish them based on how your character has been acting or the results of their actions.

That’s right: just like in real life, you don’t really have control over your Reputation, though there is a way to influence it as you go.

All your Reputations start at 0.

Chapter 1: Step-by-Step Overview

Which Race Should I Choose?

Your race effects character creation in 3 ways: Stat Score Modifiers, Gameplay Rewards, and Archetype Costs. If you're new to RPGs in generally, go for a race that interests you based on the flavor.

Stat Score Modifiers

These modify the base stats that you roll. You can use it to balance out your character's weak spots or you can use it to maximize what your character is already good it. We recommend the latter as Bonfire is a game built around teamwork: you'll have friends who will be good at things your character isn't.

Note that these adjustments can't bring score above 20 or below 1.

Gameplay Rewards

Each race is meant to encourage a certain type of play. For instance, orcs gain bonuses for starting things, like combats or Confrontations, so they reward players who are aggressive, while elves get rewarded for being careful with how they engage with others. These are noted under the Racial Temperament section in their summary.

Archetype Costs

Some races can spend less time mastering things than others, freeing up time (CrP) to focus on other pursuits or becoming true masters of their vocation. This is reflected in the Archetype Costs.

Appearance

Elves are slender beings that are widely considered classically beautiful, though many find their features alien and off putting. They uniformly have long ears that taper to a point at the end.

Personality

To describe an Elf as “careful” would be a huge understatement. They see other races as brash and impetuous while they're in turn seen as slow and cowardly.

It's not they're incapable of great emotion or quick action but elves seem to understand that with every action comes a consequence, whether that action was well meaning or not, and that this karmic force is more powerful than any other law or power.

However, once set on a course, it is hard to dissuade an elf, for they do nothing lightly.

Personality

An orc’s life is defined by struggle: from birth to death, they conceptualize the universe as having a vendetta against them. This can often give them a martyr complex and make them morose traveling companions at times because they feel the weight of the world.

They can also come across as aggressive, which some have pointed out is a defense mechanism, but orcs would simply scoff at that.

There’s nothing an orc values more than their honor and the chance to prove themselves against a worthy foe. This is often seen in their deities across orcish cultures, which are almost exclusively pantheons of their greatest foes. It is taken for granted that you are only as good as your enemies.

Chapter 2: Races

Introduction

Archetypes represent your character’s background training and starting skills and there are two types of Archetypes: Generalist and Specialist.

Generalist Archetypes

These Archetypes represent the overall theme.

For instance, Fighter Archetypes tend to be good at things related to combat. So, if you’re looking to make a combat focused character, you can look there. They are Expert, Weird-Adept, Servant, Socialite, and Fighter. You must pick two starting out.

Generalists will have a wide variety of discounts within their theme that are good for just getting straight bonuses.

Purchasing Archetypes

First choose a Generalist Primary and a Generalist Secondary Archetype and pay the price in CrP for both based on your race.

You use the Vitality Die, the Skills & Trainings, and the Primary Drawback sections from the Primary, and combine both Archetype Abilities sections, though you remove 1 leveling ability from your Secondary Archetype, unless you Double Specialize.

All Generalists, except for the Servant, can be selected as both your Primary and Secondary Archetype, called Double Specializing. So, you can be a Fighter/Fighter but not a Servant/Servant.

The benefits for double specializing will be noted at the end of the Archetype.

If two characters have the same ability, unless it says otherwise, those abilities stack.

Specialist Archetypes

These represent a more focused version of the Generalist Archetypes.

They’re more expensive but come with additional powers. When you purchase a Specialist Archetype, it replaces the Generalist Archetype of the same type. So, for instance, if you’re an Expert, you can replace it with the Ranger or the Thief.

Purchasing Specialist Archetypes

At 1st level and at every level up, you can purchase a Specialist Archetype which replaces your Generalist Archetypes. If at 1st level, see the Specialist Archetypes at 1st Level sidebar below.

You can replace one or both at each level, in either order, barring double-down Weird-Adepts who have to specialize both at the same time into the same Specialist Archetype.

You can also choose to replace neither if it doesn’t fit your character because once you purchase a Specialist Archetype, you are stuck with it.

Specialist Archetypes completely replaces everything from the Generalist Archetype they’re replacing though it doesn’t retroactively remove things you’ve already rolled for or free purchases.

So, a Fighter, with a d10 Vitality Die decides, at level 4 to become a Soldier with a Vitality Die of a d8. He doesn’t then reroll all his Vitality Die - from that point forward, he simply uses the d8 instead.

You’ll pay an additional 20 CrP for each one you’re replacing regardless.

Your GM may choose to reject an Archetype combination based on the fact that the two Archetypes don’t fit together thematically. So, while a Thief/Priest (Beast Covenant) might be really powerful because of the Beast Covenant’s Shape Changing ability, if there’s not a Patron of Thieves and Animals in the campaign world, you’re out of luck.

Remember that if you’re replacing your Secondary Archetype, you’ll still need to select a leveling ability from your new Archetype to remove.

Specialist Archetypes at 1st Level

If you’re more familiar with the game, your playstyle, and which Archetypes suit you, you might consider purchasing your Specialization(s) at 1st level. That’s perfectly fine, but you’ll still need to pay the CrP for both the Generalist and the Specialist.

You’re considered to have purchased your Generalist Archetype first AND THEN purchased your Specialist, which means that you still get any free stuff from abilities that say "at first level" from your Generalist, so Experts get some free skills and Weird-Adepts get their starting Rudiments.

Your Primary Specialist’s Vitality Die and Primary Drawback also replaces your Primary Generalist’s.

If you’ve already bought a Basic Training that a Specialist comes with, you receive its worth in CrP as a bonus. This doesn’t extend to Skills since their benefits stack.

Anatomy of the Divine

Covenants

Divines have spheres of power that represent their aspects. These are codified into Covenants, or promises they make with their servants in exchange for a portion of the Divine’s power.

Cults of that Divine typically pick one, maybe two Covenants, of their chosen Divine to focus on.

For instance, Wülder, a Gothic god, watches over Raiding, Industry and Trade. There could be 3 different priests to Wülder, one focusing on his role as the Bridge Between Men, one seeing him as primarily a watcher over people going on cattle raids, and one worshiping him as the greater crafter and source of inspiration.

While they all worship the same Divine, the Miracles they can invoke in his name are different.

This means that priests of different cults may not get along with each other and there can even be intra-faith wars to purge heretics (those that focus on different Covenant from them).

The main bonus of Covenants is that they make Miracles within them easier to invoke. The specifics are elaborated on in the Invoking Miracles in Chapter 5:: The Divine.

Cosmic Links

The Higher Planes are those that exist above the crude and primitive world and so, naturally, the Divines are linked to the events and behaviors of the celestial bodies that make their homes in the heavens.

A Cosmic Link is the specific celestial body that the Divine is associated with, and it usually gives an insight to the personality of the god. The Cosmic Link doesn’t necessarily mean your Patron is primarily the Divine of that Cosmic thing. So, a Divine associated with the moon might not be primarily a moon god, in the same way that while Venus was associated with the Morning Star, she was primarily viewed as a love goddess by the Romans. Still, your holy symbols may incorporate the moon motif commonly to represent her.

For clarification’s sake: when something says “decrease” in this section’s explanations, you go down the chart by that many steps. If it says “increase” go up, so increase will always be a bonus, and decrease will always be bad.

Comet

This is associated with fervent but fleeting powers. Whenever you invoke a Miracle, you gain two Honor Dice with a +3 but lose an additional Favor.

Evening Star

The evening star is a source of strength and beauty: it's the first star to rise in the evening. When you invoke a Miracle, if you roll below the average, you gain the average instead. So, if you roll d8 and get a 3 or less, you get 4 instead.

Moon

The moon is a steady force, its effects are subtle but constant. When you invoke a Miracle, if you roll two Honor Dice, keeping the highest.

Morning Star

The morning star is the last star in the heavens before day and is a lone watcher. When you invoke a Miracle and roll the minimum amount, you don’t lose any Favor.

Sun

The sun is the lord of heaven, strong and unbending, its power unquestioned. When you invoke a Miracle and roll the max amount, you don’t lose any Favor.

Cyclical

Often hailing from older, more primitive times, the Cyclical deity represents earthly inevitabilities in a dichotomy, sometimes with themselves and sometimes with an opposing deity. This can be Spring and Summer, life and death, creation and destruction. They may be weak now, but they will wax unstoppable. When you invoke a Miracle, and roll under its average, your next Miracle gains a +1. This is cumulative.

If you roll above its average, this bonus is then removed, and you get a -1 to your next Miracle. Again, this is cumulative and resets when you roll below.

So, if you have a d6! Honor Dice, and you roll a 2, the next Miracle you invoke gets a +1. That next Miracle you roll, you get a 3 for a total of 4 (because of the +1), giving you a +2 the next roll. On that roll, you roll a 5 for a total of 7 ( 5 + 2 ). The next Miracle you then invoke gets a -1

Honor Of The Divines If you or someone you've spent Favor on rolls the maximum value on your Honor Die while invoking a Miracle, you gain an extra use of your Honor Die.

Runegalder

Choose this Archetype if you want to play a weird-adept and are familiar with other games' spellcasters.

Vitality Die

d6

Skills & Trainings

Main Skill Suite Lore and Weirdcraft

Archetype Abilities

Vancian Shaping The Runegalder chains the power of spells to various runes that he then invests, sealing them with his blood until he’s ready to release them.

Every 24 hours, the Runegalder can seal 3 spells. He can also bind an additional spell for every 3 levels he’s obtained. This means that he may cast those spells at any time by simply speaking the rune they’re linked to, once prepared.

Speaking the rune releases it from its binding so speaking the same rune again before he’s resealed it will not recast the spell - the spell’s power is gone.

He may cast a spell multiple times per day, but he’ll need to bind each instance of the spell to a different rune.

He may also unseal a rune without actually shaping it, but he is not able to immediately reseal a new rune right after.

The Runegalder's magic is 'dead' and so is less potent than other types of magic, thus allowing some to shrug off their spells.

When shaping a spell, the Runegalder rolls a d20! + 10 + their level. All those that would be affected by the spell roll a d20! + their level. If their total is above the Runegalder's, they're unaffected by his magic. This roll is made regardless of whether or not the person wants to be affected by the spell.

Arcane Components The Runegalder gains 3 additional random Rudiments and gains an additional 2 Rudiments every level.

Grimoire This is the Runegalder’s spell book and keeps the spells that he binds to runes. Unlike a normal spell book, however, the spells are scribed in meteorite ink and blood that seals their magic power in the text itself. This means that the Runegalder’s Grimoire does the channeling latently for him.

At first level he constructs 3 spells using his known Rudiments and an additional 1 at level up. These spells can’t have a Drain greater than 7 + their level (so at 1st level, his spells can only have a Drain of 8) when it is scribed into the Grimoire. A spell’s Rudiments cannot be redistributed and changed: spells in the Grimoire are immutable.

So, a 2nd level Runegalder scribes a new spell. He can max out at 7 Drain. He chooses to make it a Wall Shape (3 Drain), at Full Range (1 Drain), Near Origin (1 Drain) and for its Effect, he chooses Disorient (2 Drain), for a total of 7. He could have also made it Chain Shape instead for 1 Drain, and Stacked the Disorient Effect, if he wanted.

Note that spells cast by the Runegalder only last their Interval unless their Effect is stacked, in which case, it either increases that Interval by a like amount or gains the Stacking bonus but not both. This is decided when the spell is cast.

Spells inscribed at different levels count as completely different spells and must be sealed to different runes. So, if the Runegalder has a 8 SP Flash Grenade and a 11 SP Flash Grenade, he would need two runes to cast both. He couldn’t simply bind “Flash Grenade” to one rune and then choose which he wanted to use in the moment.

Skill Focus (Weirdcraft, 1) Through the course of her career, a Runegalder seeks to further their knowledge of the occult. They receive 1 free purchase of the Weirdcraft Skill Suite at every other level up.

Primary Drawback

The Runegalder uses his body as a prison for latent magical energies which wears on a person over time. He ages at twice the normal rate.

They also find other types of Weirdshaping strange and so are unable to take another Weird-Adept Archetype as their Secondary, however, they are able to double specialize in their Primary.

Skills Vs Trainings

There are two different places you can gain bonuses from: Skills and Trainings.

You don’t really need to know the difference because they work the same when you’re actually running the game, but Skills represent silos areas of knowledge (being good at Athletics doesn’t make you good at Tactics) while Training represents knowledge that is interrelated (you can’t reasonable learn how to strike more accurately with a blade without also learning to guard yourself).

Basic Training

Armor Basic Training

This basic training allows a character to more effectively utilize armor of different types based on their level of familiarity with them. It’s split into 3 categories:

Light (15 CrP) This covers armor that tends to not to restrict the wearer so is often favored by skill centric characters.

Medium (10 CrP) This armor is a bit heavier than Light armor and offers a tradeoff between protection and mobility.

Heavy (10 CrP) The armor in this category is durable, thick, and heavy, able to withstand blows but at the cost of being hard to move in.

You have to buy armor training in sequence, starting at Light and moving all the way to Heavy.

You can always try and use armor without the baseline knowledge of how to properly wear and don it, but the Defense penalty is doubled and you're always considered Fatigued if that’s the case.

You can further your training in armor to increase the benefits. See the Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Training Sidebar for more details.

Shield Basic Training

For 10 CrP, this teaches a character the basic understanding of how to properly use and employ shields in combat. This training covers all types of shields.

You can always try to use a shield without the training; however, you gain a -4 to your defense when doing so.

You can further your training in shields to increase the benefits. See the Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Training Sidebar for more details.

Weapon Basic Training

By gaining the basic understanding of how a weapon is wielded, used and taken care of, you not only expand its longevity, but also your longevity on the battlefield.

Weapon training costs 10 CrP and is taken for individual weapons, however its benefits extend to weapons of that share similar features.

A person completely untrained in weapons both Attacks and Defends with that weapon using a d12!.

However, one trained uses a d20! Attack and Defense (depending on whether or not they use a shield).

Additionally, this is extended to any weapon that shares similar qualities.

For example, a character with training only in the Dusack extends that training to all melee weapons.

A character with training in both a Melee and Ranged weapons, gains that training to all weapons.

So, why would you take training in multiple weapons once you have one melee and one ranged? Because this training allows you to further master that weapon, increasing its usefulness.

You can further your training in weapons to increase the benefits. See the Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Training Sidebar for more details.

Advanced Training

Weapon, Shield, & Armor Advanced Trainings are further training in those particular fields, like the name implies. They can only be taken for things that you already have Basic Training in.

Each of these types of Training has two things: Tiers, which represent how broad your knowledge is (sort of like Suite Suites and Advanced Skills), and Ranks.

For instance, the Tiers of Armor training are All Armor, Armor Type, and Specific Armor. So, if you could gain a bonus for using all armors, or medium armors, or a single type of armor, like brigandine. Obviously, the broader the area of knowledge the more applications but the most expensive it’s going to be to increase your bonus with them.

Every type will also have areas you can specialize in. These are the same across the Tiers of that type of Advanced Training, but you train in specific Tiers. So, Armor Advanced Training has Defense, Recovery Modifier, Fatigue, and Initiative as its areas. Each purchase in that area gives them a Rank in one of the Tiers, which is a bonus that’s applied when applicable. This bonus can't bring a negative bonus above 0, however.

You can’t buy up a Rank again for a given Tier until you’ve either filled out all the rest of the areas for that Tier or you have two areas in that Tier at the current highest Rank. This is true all the way down so your Advanced Training forms a weird looking triangle (as shown to the left). This is true even if you have reached the maximum benefit from an area.

For example, let’s say that you want to increase your training in Scale Armor, which, as we all know, has the stats of Def 0, Init 3, Recovery 1, and Fatigue of Bloodied. If you want to get a Rank 5 in Fatigue, you would have to buy up to a Rank 4 in a different category, Rank 3 in another, and Rank 2 in a final thing.

Ideally, that means that you bought Rank 4 in Init, Rank 3 in Recovery, and Rank 2 in Def. Notice that that additional 2 Ranks in Recovery and that Rank 1 in Def doesn’t give you anything besides allowing you to reach Rank 4 in Fatigue.

Tiers don't stack with each other: you gain all the bonuses from a single Tier of your choice. If you have a +1 Attack and Damage in all melee weapons and a +1 to Defense and -1 to Recovery in polearms, while wielding a pike, you either gain the +1 Attack and Damage with it or +1 Defense and -1 Recovery, but not +1 Attack, Damage, Defense, and -1 Recovery.

Bonuses cascade down through tiers but only if they’re applicable.

Ranged Weapon Firing Break Down

If you’re wondering what you’re doing at any given second when firing a ranged weapon: here’s a breakdown for you.

So, if you already loading and cranked (drawing) your crossbow, it would only take 5 seconds to fire it.

This is drawing the missile from its quiver or pouch. For firearms, that means drawing out the powder and bullets.

Fuse

This is the process of lighting the fuse. This can be dropped to 1 second but increases the chance of backfiring from 1 to 5.

Loading

This is knocking the arrow or loading the bullet and powder in the barrel.

Draw

This is drawing back the bow, cranking the crossbow, or cocking your arm for the throw. This can be prepped ahead of time for the belly- and crossbows but not the composite, long, or war bows

This is actually sighting your target and lining up the shot. This can be skipped, making it so you fire faster, but you roll attack on a d12! instead of the standard d20!. This can't bring a weapon below its minimum weapon Recovery.

Firing

This is pulling the trigger or loosing the arrow.

Advanced Training that lowers Recovery take from Missile Retrieval, Fuse, Loading, Drawing, and Aiming evenly but they can never reduce a single category to below 1 second. So, the minimum firing Recovery for a Composite Bow is 5 seconds.

Skills

Skill Suites Vs Advanced Skills

You’ll notice that there are two types of Skills in Bonfire: Skill Suites and Advanced Skills.

So, what’s the difference?

Skill Suites

These skills represent broad swathes of knowledge that cover a variety of subjects. Suites are hard to increase because there’s so much ground to cover but can be used if you don’t have the specific Advanced Skill.

An example would of this would be using the Survival Suite if you didn’t have the Signaling Advanced Skill.

Advanced Skills

These skills are more tightly focused, covering one intellectual discipline. They’re easier to master but may not be applicable in every case.

An example of this would be First Aid, Fire Building, and Scavenging are all Skills in the Survival Skill Suite, while Law, History, and Medicine are all Skills in the Lore Skill Suite.

Acquiring Skills

Throughout your character’s career, she’ll acquire and improve her skills. Both types of skills are purchased with CrP, that is spent either at Character Creation or when leveling up.

All skills have a unique cost that represents how easy or difficult they are to master. You pay the price as noted in the section below, you improve/purchase the skill.

Cost

Skill Suites cost their Base Cost + (Current Rank * 10).

If you’re Untrained in that Skill Suite, “Current Rank”, the cost is just Base Cost.

When you buy a Skill Suite, if you've already bought Ranks in its children Advanced Skill, you increase those Ranks by 1 as well.

Advanced Skills cost their Base Cost + (Current Rank * 2).

If you haven’t purchased an Advanced Skill before, its current Rank is equal to the Rank of the mother Skill Suite and increased by 1 from there.

Skill Specializations

It often pays to specialize. After all, many dissertations cover extremely minute subjects with incredible depth and drawing on that well of knowledge can be a huge benefit.

Skill Specializations represent an area of knowledge within an Advanced Skill that your character spends extra time versing themselves in. As such, they gain a bonus.

Skill specializations come in layers, modifiers that narrow down the field. Each layer gives a +1 to your effective Rank when it comes into play.

For example, a monster hunter studying predators, would gain a+1 Rank, while another that studies flying predators would gain a +2 Rank bonus to their checks, and a monster hunter that studies the flying predators of the Uchka Mountains would gain a bonus of +3 to their Rank.

There may come a time when a specialization only kind of applies. In this case, it may be kind of helpful, as the max bonus that can be gained from it is +1.

So, when encountering a land predator in the Uchka Mountains, all three monster hunters would only gain +1 to their Rank.

That sort of super-focused study takes a lot of time and effort, and man only has so much time and energy to give. The total number of specializations per Advanced Skill that a character can have is equal to half his Intelligence score.

A character can gain a specialization like any other skill; having his character go out of the way to learn more about his craft. The price is the base cost of the Advanced Skill times the number of layers in the specialization plus one.

A character can go back and increase the number of layers in his Skill Specialization after his initial purchase of it, but it costs additional amount of CrPs equal to twice the base cost of the Advanced Skill.

Lore Skill Suite

This suite covers general knowledge, which is mostly academic in nature. Most of it is pretty heavy stuff but the basics are second nature or stuff that’s been picked up here and there.

Administration

While they’ll never inspire the awe or respect that other leaders do, people who oversee the day to day functions of an organization keep the lights on so that others can make the speeches.

Administrators can make a Check at +0 to gain a clue about an item the party might need on their adventure, or one to retroactively remember that they did buy an item back at the market for just such an occasion.

Each of these Checks they make increases the Difficulty of the next one by 1 step.

History

Everyone knows certain large events, but few can tie those events into an overarching narrative and understand their effects on later events.

The History Skill allows a character to not just recall events but also to link them to the present day, to certain traditions and rituals of a culture, or the behavior of certain members of a culture, giving insight on how to proceed.

Language (specific)

A character receives this Skill for free in their native tongue with a Rank equal to their Int and it allows them to speak and be understood by others with Skill in that language.

Purchasing other languages can be done, however, if it a language completely unrelated to a language you already known, the Language's Rank starts at 0: otherwise use your Lore Rank as normal.

Checks are only made when timing is critical, such as communication in a battle, or when understanding is key, such as when debating foreign policy.

If one of the characters is in a Confrontation with another in a tongue that they're not Expert in, that person can only gain a bonus to their roll less than or equal to their Rank in that language per round.

Additionally, when two speakers are in a Confrontation, the one with the higher Language Rank gains a +d4! to their roll (see Applicable Skills in Chapter 2:: Confrontations). In case of tie, nothing happens.

The Lore Skill Suite can’t be used in place of a Language Check for tongues you don’t know unless you have the Language Skill in a related Language. Using a real-world example, someone with the Language (Swedish) Advanced Skill couldn’t use the Lore Skill Suite to understand a person with Language (French) but could use it when talking to someone with Language (Norwegian) or Language (Danish).

Law (culture)

This Skill covers the intricacies of the legal system, including landmark rulings and common punishments for common crimes. A character can make their case or find loopholes to exploit the legal system.

A character’s Lore Skill can only be used to substitute for their native culture but not others’. If the character does Willh to branch out, his mastery of Law for that new culture starts at 0, as he must learn everything from the ground up. Diving more into his own culture’s laws (buying this Advanced Skill for his culture) uses the Lore Skill as base, as per usual.

Literacy (alphabet)

This Skill allows a character to read and write in a language’s alphabet, giving them the power to pen books and poems, or passive aggressive notes. A character who's taken the Lore Skill Suite is considered to be literate in their native language's alphabet.

This Skill can be taken multiple times, each instance representing mastery of a new writing system. The scribe can then use that alphabet to pen any language they know, in the same way that we could use the Latin Alphabet to pen Arabic or the Korean Alphabet to write English.

To decipher a text, the person needs to know both the alphabet it's written in and the language it's using, which most of the time won't matter but can be used as a cheap way to do a cypher in a pinch.

Mathematics

While this may seem like just a Skill that allows you to be able to juggle figures and add totals and calculate the area of a circle like a huge nerd, mathematics has a more practical use: correctly guessing distances.

While you can try to eyeball how far away something is, a Mathematics Check vs +0 allows you to do rough calculations, giving you the ability to know exactly what penalty you’re going to get for using a ranged weapon or who's going to be affected by the Area of Effect of a spell you’re going to cast.

Medicine

With proper treatment and nursing, a wound that would lay you out for weeks, may only keep you down for a couple of days. This Skill involves the knowledge of long-term care, when bandages should be changed, which poultices will have the greatest effect, and what minor surgeries need to be performed.

A character with this Skill can make a Check for a wounded person, adding the Severity of his largest Wound to the opposing roll.

So, if a person has a 5, 3, and 8 Severity Wound, he can make a Medicine Check vs d20+8.

Success means that the person can take 1 day off the healing time for every Wound every time it drops a Severity.

So, a success against a 5 Severity Wound means that it was treated properly and takes 10 days to completely heal ( 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 0 ) instead of the normal 15.

You may also increase the Difficulty modifier by half again as much - if you succeed, you take 2 days off per Severity. So, if you're against a d20!+9, you can make it a d20!+14 ( 9 + ( 9 / 2 ) ) for that bonus.

Additionally, when resisting Poison, Toxin, or Diseases, you can add your Medicine Rank, rounded up, to each round of the Confrontation.

Monster Craft

This Advanced Skill is under both the Lore and Survival Skill Suite but still counts as 1 Skill, so you don’t have to buy it twice - they share their Rank. It simply represents the different ways that each Skill Suite approaches the craft.

Monster Craft gives you insight into the creatures that roam the Middle Realm. Simply put, each Boon gives you a (truthful) answer to a question.

Difficulty is based on how common the monster is. Common is +0, uncommon +d6!, rare is d12!, and legendary is +d20!.

Religion (cult)

This Skill acquaints the character with a knowledge of cults, their common Covenants and Miracles, as well as their hierarchies, means of advancement, and important members.

It also allows a character to gain a knowledge of their rituals and sacrifices, as well as the taboos of their clergy and how to appease their heavenly messengers and avenging angels.

The Religion Skill is taken for a single Covenant, but can be purchased independently for different Covenants.

A character adds the tens digit of this Skill to their chance of gaining Favor when making sacrifices to the god of that cult.

World Craft

This area of study covers the topography of the Middle Realm. It’s forests and rock formations and biomes. It helps predict whether or not it’s going to rain or whether the next summer is going to be a hot one.

Predicting what’s coming in the next hour is a Check vs +0, with the Difficulty increasing by 1 dice size for each time you double the prediction window. So, detecting the weather in the next 2 hours is vs +d4!, while doing so in the next 16 hours is vs +d10!.

Streetwise Skill Suite

This suite covers knowledge that a character might pick on the street or in the school of hard knocks. While Lore is book smart, Streetwise is street smart.

Current Affairs

A character with this Skill is well informed about the day to day affairs of regional powers and men and women of importance, their dealings and alliances, as well as their hatreds. He can pick out celebrities and know how and why someone got rich and famous.

They can also use this Skill to guess the Honor category of someone by watching how people react to them and letting them know about certain NPCs' Reputations.

A lazy guard probably won’t Check your passport too closely right before lunch, but a particularly astute one who's on the lookout for a promotion will.

Gambling/gaming

This Skill doesn’t give the character a gift for winning games, just that he knows the rules and knows the odds, which could in turn lead to him winning the game.

Intuition

The ability to understand people and get where they're coming from can help you figure out what will and won't work with them better.

A Check vs +0 allows the character to get a feel for how open a person would be to a Confrontation over something or what would be definite deal breakers for them. It doesn't reveal any of their Characteristics or their strengths as it's more of gut feeling than anything.

Each Check on the same person increases the Difficulty by 1 dice size.

They can also use it to confirm their theories about what Characteristics a character has. The player needs to make a reasonable guess about the Characteristic first - so players who’re simply throwing out guesses automatically fail.

This is done at a Check vs +0 to start with but increases by 1 for every dice size per guess, whether the character succeeded at the last ones or not.

Listening

While Perception handles the other senses, Listening has to deal with your character’s auditory senses. This goes beyond simply hearing something: listening allows the character to know when an otherWille innocuous noise is actually something to pay attention to though it might not always be apparent why at first.

Perception

This covers four of the five senses that a character has: sight, touch, taste, and smell. Technically, your character is always receiving inputs from these senses, but Perception helps filter out the unimportant ones. Can he detect if there are orcs in the nearby bush? Can she taste the hidden poison in her drink before she swallows? These are all Perception Checks

Like Listening, sometimes Perception will help the character notice something that may be important, even if it’s not obvious why it’s grabbing your attention.

Sleight Of Hand

This Skill covers all things that require you to do quick, delicate movements with your fingers. This may be slipping a card up your sleeve, picking a pocket, or doing cat’s cradle while bored.

Survival Skill Suite

This suite covers everything that a character might need to survive in the wild; Knowledge of what plants to eat and the Skills to hunt and breed animals.

Botany

This gives a knowledge of what the herbs used in cooking, alchemy, and medicine look like in their natural environment and it allows you to identify their common uses. It also helps find food in the wilderness.

If halving their overland travel speed, a person with Botany can make a Check every travel period: if they succeed, they can consider themselves 1 step up on the Long-Term Exhaustion table. So, if they had nothing, they could consider themselves Rationing.

If they choose to stay put, a success nets them 2 steps up.

4 or more Boons results in doubling their output, meaning they possibly found themselves enough food for the next day.

Mostly, this is used for detecting poisonous plants, which is Check vs +d4! but is a common Helper Skill for both Medicine and Cooking/Baking.

Fire Building

Against the cold, lonely nights out in the wild, there is only the fertility symbols you carve for yourself and the roar of the fire. This Skill allows you to build one of those things: the fire.

Under normal conditions, it takes about 30 minutes and a Check vs +0 to set up and light a fire, assuming you don’t have to go far out of your way to find the wood.

First Aid

This gives the character a crude understanding of battlefield medicine: making splints and staunching wounds.

A character with this Skill can make a Check for a person wounded in that last 30 minutes, adding the Severity of any Wounds that he's treating to the opposing roll.

So, if a person has a 5, 3, and 8 Severity Wound, he can make a First Aid Check vs d20+16 or he can choose not to treat the 8 Severity Wound and make a vs d20!+8. Success means that the person can take 1 level of Severity off those Wounds. So, a 5 Severity Wound drops immediately to a 4 Severity Wound. You cannot go back and do a Check for a Wound that you skipped.

You have 30 minutes to perform First Aid Checks for a person, with each Wound taking 3 minutes, if applicable.

You may also increase the Difficulty modifier by half again as much - if you succeed, you hit 2 points of damage. So, if you're against a d20!+9, you can make it a d20!+14 ( 9 + ( 9 / 2 ) ) to heal 2 points of damage.

Handle Animal (specific)

This Skill deals with the handling and care of a specific animal, from birth to the slaughterhouse. It also deals with training and common illnesses to make sure the stock is healthy and virile.

While most Checks are handled like normal knowledge Checks or on a case-by-case basis (such as calming down your animal during a storm), using a question-and-answer style, this Skill is also used for training, with the base number of tricks you can teach an animal equal to their Int (note that carrying things and handling a bridle are two different tricks).

The animal needs to have both the basic temperament and physical ability to learn a trick (no sheep becoming pack animals and no dogs learning to pick locks). The GM may increase or decrease this for particularly smart/dumb and obedient/obstinate animals.

After a month of training, a roll is made vs +0. If successfully, the animal learned the trick. If not, another Check can be made in a month, although every failure increases the Difficulty to learn that trick by 1 dice size.

The base price of this Skill is calculated towards domesticated animals. If the player Willhes to learn how to deal with wild animals, this Skill costs thrice as much to purchase.

Hiding

This allows a character to make the most of a situation or environment to cloak themselves against prying eyes. This is always opposed by Perception, with the Difficulty determined by the area. Characters can also use this to instantly get an idea of how well they can hide in the given space.

Monster Craft

This Skill is the same as the Monster Craft under the Lore Skill Suite. Look there for details on it.

Navigation

Either using the stars or the position of the moons and sun, this character can track their way even through the most untamed wilderness without getting lost. It also allows them to tell the time.

Without proper equipment, a character can only give rough estimations of where they are, anchored on larger geographic features (like mountains or rivers but not towns or roads) to the nearest 10 miles.

Scavenging

There’s a gift in high-speed appraisal. This is often used by those picking over battlefields for anything they could sell, but it’s also found a niche with adventurers who use it to find food and sustenance while traveling away from civilization.

A Check vs +0 tells a character which of two things considered is worth more. This can’t be used for coinage, but can be any other type of good. It also gives the character an idea of whether broken equipment can be reasonably salvaged.

This doesn’t give even a ballpark of what anything is worth, but it only takes a few seconds of consideration.

If using this Skill to scavenge for food, use the rules presented in the Botany section, however, two or more Complications results in you eating something poisonous.

Snaring

This teaches the character how to properly create and place snares to trap smaller wildlife. It also gives some cursory knowledge on the best places to put them.

Snares have a chance of giving you food once a week, although you’ll only actually roll when you Check them. Every Boon you get gives you half a day’s worth of food.

Hunting

Most of the time, you’re not going to down your query in one go, and so the hunt is on as your watch for subtle signs of their passing. While this Skill was originally used to hunt wildlife, it has been repurposed by wardens and bounty hunters trying to capture the most dangerous game.

The Hunting Skill can be used to track your prey, get a sense of their movements, and predict where they’re going. It can also give insight into common and uncommon natural creatures.

If you’re using it for food, hunting is a boom or bust strategy: at the end of each full day you spend hunting, roll a Check.

For each Boon you gain 2 days’ worth of food, but either way, you gain 1 damage from exhaustion and possibly more if you suffer a Complication.

Use Rope

Covering all kinds of knots and bindings, the Use Rope Skill is pretty self-explanatory, though it should be noted that you can also use this Skill to get a sense of the tensile strength of a rope or how much you’re going to need for a certain job.

To keep your players on their toes, I suggest you make the Check, not when they tie the knot, but when it’s tested.

Tactics Skill Suite

Combat isn’t just about raw physical power, but more about how it’s directed and used. This Skill Suite deals with logistics and making the most out of what you have.

Combat Style (style)

By studying the best tactics and how to fight in certain circumstances, a warrior can take advantage of his surroundings and combat styles emerge. Combat Styles can be anything but involved in parts: a situation and a metric.

This situation is when the Combat Style is most effective. For instance, it can be “in the forest”, “fighting on rooftops”, “being outnumbered”, “being in a duel”, “fighting while prone”, etc. Really anything (as long as your GM approves).

The second part of the Combat Style is the metric applied to. This can be anything you could normally specialize in.

Both the situation and metric are selected at the time of purchase and cannot be changed.

If the situation is applicable, the character receives a +1 to that metric for every 2 Ranks of that Skill he’s obtained.

So, a character with the Combat Skill (unarmed fighting, attack) at 5 who gets into a tavern brawl gets a +2 to attack as long as he doesn’t draw his weapon and simply uses fisticuffs.

Having a high Rank in Tactics doesn't automatically give you Combat Style bonuses under all circumstances and buying this Skill for the first time starts it off at Rank 0, instead of the Tactic Suite's Rank.

More than simply yelling “hey, look” at someone, the art of distraction is the art of holding people’s attention.

The amount of time that someone is delayed is based on the Difficulty of the Check and how bent on their current activity they are.

On a success, the person (or persons) is distracted for the amount of time according to the Difficulty * half the number of Boons, rounded up.

This isn’t magical: an obvious threat to their person will cause the person to focus in.

This Skill is less effective when the person knows they’re in immediate danger. In this case, as long as they’re not actively engaged in melee with someone (in which case it’s an instant failure under most circumstances), the person is distracted for seconds, instead of minutes.

Each successive Check’s Difficulty gets increased by 2 dice sizes in these cases.

Leadership

The difference between a leader and an administrator is that the latter gets stuff done and the former gets others to get stuff done but hooking into what they believe and how they view themselves.

Assuming you have a willing audience, you may make a Leadership Skill Check vs +0 pointed at a Characteristic you think the person or group might have.

If you succeed the Check and they have the Characteristic you’re looking for (or close enough - you don’t have to be exact), you increase their Panic threshold by 1 step for the next d4! hours. Gaining 4 or more Boon increases it by 2 steps.

You can increase the number of Characteristics you’re aiming for by increasing the Difficulty dice size of the Check by a similar number.

Quarter Mastering

There’s an art to packing and making sure that everyone is well fed and prepared. If the quartermaster is using a personal container of any kind, he adds 2S for each Rank he has in this Skill. OtherWille, he adds 1S per Rank. This also applies to any person he packs for.

Rally

When manure hits the windmill, no speeches or grand narratives will keep a person from bolting. Only the cold will of a competent commander can keep a battle from becoming a route.

A captain who successfully makes a Check vs +) for someone who is currently over their Stress Threshold, heals that person a number of Stress equal to 3 * his number of Boons. This is opposed by the enemy captain, if applicable, and each Check against the same target increases the Difficulty by 1 dice size, regardless of whether it was successful or not.

Riding

This Skill teaches a character how to maintain their stability while on horseback and makes them capable of feats of equine prowess.

The bonuses for this Skill are varied and many with the specifics spelled out in Chapter 3:: Combat under the Mounted Combat section so they won't be listed here. Seriously, go Check it out.

Signalling

This is the knowledge of how to communicate over long distances, either using semaphore, smoke signals, signal lamps, or horn blowing.

When using this means of communication, each side rolls a Check based on how complicated the message is and how quickly they need to send it.

Signaling is an ad hoc language with no grammatical rules or complicated tenses and so even at the best of times, only simple messages can be sent.

Warfare

The Warfare Skill hands directing troops on the battlefield, weighing options, and devising plans on the fly.

Those with this Skill can roll it to temporarily pause combat to ask one question and receive one answer from a specific member of the party, or to simply tell them something. On top of this, you can also use this, out of combat, to confer with players who’re characters are not present in the scene.

Checks start at +0 Difficulty but increase by 1 dice size each time they’ve been rolled that combat.

Warfare also has additional benefits and uses that will be outlined in Crowns of Fire, the kingdom management and mass combat supplement for Bonfire.

Trades Skill Suite

This suite covers the crafting and creating Skills. While there are a wide array and breadth of professions, this list is pared down to those areas of knowledge where the common man and the adventurer overlap in their needs.

Appraisal (specific)

It’s Wille to know what something is worth before selling and buying it. This Skill handles that and is taken for a specific type of good, such as books & scrolls, livestock, jewelry, arms & armor, and so on.

If a character has the appropriate Skill covering the item he’s looking to sell/purchase, a Check will tell them what kind of deal they're getting on the item - how much they’re being ripped off or how much of a steal it actually is.

Additionally, the character should get a bonus if he has any Skill that’s applicable to the thing he’s purchasing.

Articulation

You may think that the truth is powerful enough to carry your argument, but you can be beat out by a master of deception. Articulation is the Skill of rhetoric and formulating the truth in such a way that makes it the most convincing.

The level of Difficulty to convince someone is how much evidence you can supply to back up your arguments. This is opposed by Deception or Articulation but only if someone is actively trying to deceive or convince the same person as you.

Note that the Difficulty of their Deception Check may add bonus dice to your Articulation Check.

Even if you succeed, this isn’t a permanent way of changing a person’s mind. Others may lie more effectively than you can tell the truth and that may cause them to rethink their opinion on what you tried to sell them.

Artistry (specific)

While some may not find the Fine Arts a worthy way to spend time, others may disagree. Artistry covers all artistic endeavors, including painting, sculpture, embroidery and anything else that is done for art’s sake.

Cooking/baking

On the road, a hot meal can count for a lot. A character who has a hot meal gains 1 Relaxation for every soft success the cook had on their Check.

On top of this, a character can prepare otherWille unpalatable or poisonous food in such a way as to make the most of it, though it is a Check vs d12! to do so.

Unless the character is trying to prepare a feast worthy of His Imperial Majesty, basic daily cooking only requires a Check vs +0.

Craft (specific)

This Skill covers any Skill that makes something that isn’t noted here, such as carpentry, tailoring, basket weaving, pottery, etc. The GM may adjust the price for a particularly difficult to master study.

Leatherworking

From the moment it leaves the beast to the moment the final touches are finished, leatherworking handles the process of making and crafting works from the hides of animals, including skinning and tanning.

Maintenance

The Skill of tinkers and adventurers, this gives the person a general knowledge of easy and ad hoc fixes that can be done on the fly.

A successful Check lowers the Wear on the item by 1. The level of Difficulty in repairing something is equal to the Wear on the item, rounded up to the nearest dice size.

So repairing a spear with 1 Wear is a d4!, while repairing one with a 5 Wear is a d6!.

Another Check can't be made until the item gains more Wear.

Metalworking

This covers the process of changing crude ore into a beautiful weapon, armor, or decorative piece. It also covers the construction and repair of more mundane things, like plows, but if you’re picking this Skill up, I doubt you’re doing it to corner the farmer’s market.

Musician (specific)

This covers the mastery of specific instruments, including singing. At the end of the day, the dulcet tones of a neatly played mandolin can ease the ache of a long day.

A character who hears someone play an instrument gains 1 Relaxation for every Boon the player had on his Check. This doesn’t stack with others playing; however, you will take the highest roll and use that to determine Relaxation. It also does no good if you’re in a stressful situation, like being hunted.

Unless the character is trying to serenade a queen, basic daily ditties only require a Check vs +0.

Performance

This Skill covers everything from making your case before a court or a stirring speech before charging into battle.

On a success, anyone listening gains a temporary Devotion to what the performer wants: whether that’s themselves or an organization or a case. If the Devotion already exists, the size is temporarily increased.

The Devotions strength is equal to the number of Boons the performer had.

Weirdcraft Skill Suite

The science of the Weird is an oft studied but little understood branch of knowledge that requires a deep knowledge of the nature of the Painted World.

With this Skill Suite, someone can identify a spell, point out needed regents and processes for ritual shapings, detect if something is touched by the Weird and what its properties are, or even detect if a spell has been cast in an area, and have a chance of finding out what it was.

All Advanced Weirdcraft Skills

Unlike other Advanced Skilled, the Weirdcraft Advanced Skills pretty much work the same and so don't need their own section.

The first bonus that having an Advanced Weirdcraft Skill gives you is that it gives you further insight into a particular Weird Tradition, allowing you to detect it better. For more information on what each Tradition specializes in, head over to Chapter 6:: The Weird.

The other thing it does is allow you to shape cantrip versions of spells from that Tradition, regardless of Archetype. The ability to shape is inherit in all living things though it takes decades of practice, a strong family bloodline, and/or a natural gift to do what Weird-Adept Archetypes do, a form of practical "folkweird" is often passed down as part of the tools of the trade.

This doesn't, and will never, allow someone to shape the full version of a spell but it can give them a boost or an edge. The specific details should be left up to the GM and player, but they should always be appropriate to the Tradition and never be more powerful than a spell that does the same thing. Most commonly this will mean it will get used as a Helper Skill.

Shaping a cantrip is still shaping and is stressful and so that anytime any of these Skills are used to shape, the shaper gets 2d4! Stress.
