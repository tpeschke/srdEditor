Welcome to Bonfire Character Creation! You'll notice that there's a lot of steps and, if you're familiar with other TTRPGs, that it's a bit longer and a bit more complicated than other games. Why is that?

The first is that it gives you, the player, more choice. Do you want to build a cunning fighter than actually using their brains instead of their brawns to defeat others? Do you want to play a sly diplomat that crushes her opponents' morale without even lifting a finger? Or perhaps you want to play a power wizard that concocts his spells on the fly, building the perfect one for each scenario?

More importantly, do you want those different characters to actually feel different when you play them? So that convincing some actually feels different than just beating them to death?

This is the first thing that Bonfire offers you.

The second thing is that it allows you to be surprised during creation: through your choices and rolls, you'll get a more fully formed character than in other systems. You'll get what you want with extra twists that help add interest and depth to your character that you wouldn't get in other systems.

And not only that, but you'll also have those choices, twists, and depth mean something in play. Is your character brash? You'll be rewarded for playing that way in the game.

So, what are you waiting for? Let's get started!

Step 1: Roll Stats

Your character has 6 attributes that define your character physically and mentally. These are Strength (Str), Dexterity (Dex), Constitution (Con), Intellect (Int), Willpower (Will), and Pre (Presence).

Roll 3d6 for each of these Abilities to generate that score.

Strength

Strength (Str) represents your character’s pure brawn. How much can she lift and carry? How strong are her punches?

It governs the base damage you do, how much you can carry, and strength-based skills.

Dexterity

Dexterity (Dex) is a measure of how agile your character is. Can she dodge arrows, or can she execute a graceful pirouette? Dexterity is crucial for avoiding blows in combat or traps in a hallway.

It covers how accurate you are, how well you can dodge, how quickly you get up in moving in situations, and dexterity-based skills.

Constitution

Constitution (Con) represents your character’s overall health and toughness. Can he shrug off the Dancing Death or does he succumb to a head cold? Can she take a solid blow in combat and not even blink? These are the things Constitution oversees.

It controls your base Vitality, the amount of minimum Vitality at every other level up, and how much damage you can suck down without going comatose, and constitution-based skills.

Intellect

Intellect (Int) measures your character’s raw knowledge: how much data can he remember? Can she do mathematical calculations or remember who the Elector of Stanitz is? Intellect doesn’t help in applying that knowledge, but it does give you knowledge to draw on.

It governs how accurate you are, how many CrP you get on level up, and intellect-based skills (which are a lot of them).

Willpower

Willpower (Will) is applied knowledge. Does she process information quicker and learn from other’s mistakes? Does he understand life is more than just a set of numbers of probabilities? Can she see other’s viewpoints? How well can she overcome adversity or keep her ground?

It controls your starting Stress Threshold, how likely you are to get hit, how quickly you get up and moving in situations, and willpower-based skills.

Presence

Presence (Pre) represents force of personality. Does he bend others to his will? Is she the focus of any room she enters? Do people look up to them or what to be him? Presence is not looks or even likeability: it represents this animal magnetism that people follow.

Presence covers how much bonus Favor you get when worshipping the Divines, your starting Honor, how strong your Traits are, how many contacts you start the game with, how many followers you can have, and presence-based skills.

A Note About Low Stats

Bonfire is specifically balanced for you to roll 3d6 in order, with 10 being the average result but don't worry about feeling like you have low scores or they're not in the order you want: you'll have a chance to rearrange and modify them later.

But even if you have objectively lower stats than the rest of the company, you'll still be able to contribute in a meaningful way in your own area of expertise because Bonfire is built as a team game, so nobody is going to be good at everything.

In Bonfire, you'll almost always be weaker than your opponent: what makes a hero is not supernatural abilities, but the simple fact that he fights longer and harder for what he believes in than other men.

Step 2: Receive CrP

Alright, you have your scores. Now it’s time to determine how many Creation Points (or CrP, for short) you’ll start with. These CrP will be spent on modifying your character, buying them their skills and increasing their Scores. They may also be spent on rerolls at certain steps.

Even still, you may want to switch your stats around. This may be because you have almost the right stats, but you have a specific character concept in mind. For instance, using the example stats in Step 1, your party may need a diplomatic character more than a strong character. In this case, it would really help to swap Strength and Presence.

You can freely switch around your stats as need but, in that case, you only receive 25 CrPs.

If you switch only 4 of your stats, you receive 50 CrPs.

If you switch 2, you receive 75 CrPs.

And if you keep your stats the way they are, with no changes, you gain 100 CrPs to work with.

Step 3: Select Race

Your character’s race will change how they interact with the world and what they’ll be good and bad at so pick carefully.

Details are found in Chapter 2:: Races so go there and pick one and come back here when you’re done.

Step 4: Select Archetypes

Your character has an area of expertise represented by two Archetypes.

You choose a Primary and a Secondary Archetype and pay the price in CrP for both based on your race’s Archetype Cost section. Descriptions of the different Archetypes are found in Chapter 3:: Archetypes along with more details on how to combine them.

If you’ve chosen to reroll your stats and have no CrPs to work with, congratulations, you’re now in CrP debt and need to make it up by the end of Character Creation.

You must select two Archetypes before going further.

Step 5: Roll Flaws

All heroes have flaws. That’s what makes them relatable and human (even if they’re an orc). Character Flaws earn you additional CrP, which is great. Flaws can also be used in Confrontations to further the story.

For a list of Flaws flip to Chapter 4:: Flaws.

If you’ve gone into CrP debt and you’re not out of it by the end of this stage, you’ll need to go back and reselect your Archetypes so that you are or go back to you starting set of stats and begin Character Creation all over again with them.

Step 6: Adjust Stats

Now, it is time to do the final adjustments to your stats. This isn't your final adjustments: you'll have a chance to increase them again level up.

The price to increase a Stat is the Stat + 10. So, to get a Stat to 9 from 8, it would cost 18 CrP.

This can't bring a stat above 20.

Starting Stress Threshold

Your starting Threshold is your Will times 3.

Starting Vitality

To calculate your base Vitality, add a roll of your Primary Archetype’s Vitality Die to your Constitution + their Vitality Size Modifier.

Divide that by 4: that’s the amount of the Vitality in each Category.

Your Trauma Threshold is twice that (rounded up). Trauma is talked about more in Chapter 3:: Combat.

For fractions on Wound Categories, round up on Hurt but down on the rest.

So, if you’re a Human with and your Vitality Die is a d10 and you roll a 7 with a Con of 12, you have 34 Vitality. Your Hurt is 1 - 9, Bloodied between 10 - 17, Wounded between 18 - 25, and Critical between 26 - 34.

Record your Vitality Die and your roll on your character sheet.

Levels of Induction & Max Favor

There are 3 levels of commitment to a cult, that determines how often you get Favor and how much Favor you can have:

Unanointed

These are the lowest level: those outside the cult and its mysteries. They gain no benefits of any kind.

Anointed

These are those that are the lay members of the faith. They gain small bonuses to their max Favor for the deity they’re devoted to. You can only be anointed to one Covenant at a time.

If you have a positive Devotion to a deity, one of their cults, or one of their Covenants, you can choose to begin the game as an anointed to that deity. Otherwise, you can become Anointed during gameplay once you have that Devotion.

You can only be anointed to one deity at a time.

Servants

These are the highest rung of the commitment ladder and serve as the officers of the cult of the god. They’re priests, abbots, hierophants, pontiffs, nazarites, hermits, etc. These are men and women that have taken vows to devote their life to the cult.

In game terms, this means that these are those who have a Servant Archetype as either their Primary or Secondary Archetype.

Out of Order Character Creation

If you’re a little bit more familiar with Bonfire Character creation, the 3 last steps (step 8, 9, and 10) can be taken in any order you’d like, even skipping back and forth between them.

Step 8: Training & Skills

Trainings and Skills are additional areas of expertise that your character has obtained above his or her race and class.

Both cost CrP and are found in Chapter 5:: Skills & Trainings but, before you rush over there, you need to determine the starting Ranks for your Skill Suites.

Calculating Starting Ranks

Each character starts with 5 Skill Suites. They are:

  • Athletics
  • Lore
  • Streetwise
  • Survival
  • Tactics
  • Trades
  • Weirdcraft

To determine your starting Rank in each, follow the guide below:

  • Your Primary Generalist's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 3.
    • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 3, the other starts at Rank 2, otherwise pick one to start at 3.
    • When you Specialize, you gain Rank 2 in its Main Skill Suite
      • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 2, the other starts at Rank 1, otherwise pick one to start at 2.
  • Your Secondary Generalist's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 2.
    • If you would normally get two, you only get one of them.
    • When you Specialize, you gain Rank 1 in its Main Skill Suite.
      • If you would normally get two, you only get one of them.
  • Finally, you may pick 1 Skill Suite to start at Rank 2.
  • If, for any reason, a Skill Suite doubles up, you only get a +1 to its Rank.
  • All other Skill Suites start at Rank 0.

Note that you get the Advanced Language Skill for free in your native language with its Rank starting at your Int.

Step 9: Flesh Out Personality

Your character also has a personality (shocker) that sets them apart.

What do they want? What do they believe? Who do they like? What do people think of them?

These questions are answered using Goals, Traits, Racial Temperament, Devotions, and Reputation, which we’ll fill out now.

Goals

Goals serve two purposes.

First, they help your GM figure out what you want to do with the character: where you want to take them and what they’re trying to accomplish.

Second, they help you get CrPs that you can then turn around and use to make your character stronger.

A character can have 3 Goals at any one time, although it’s fine if you only start with 1.

You should discuss with your GM what Goals are going to work for the campaign to get on the same page.

Have a Goal that you can continue to work for but also have a Goal(s) that are actionable as you get bonus CrP for completing them.

Traits

Traits are a lot of things.

They can be things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (“I have blonde hair”) or something general (“I have an intimidating demeanor”) or somewhere in between (“I’m an orc”).

They don’t have to be physical in nature. It could be how your character views himself (“I’m confident” or “I’m good at math”). They can also be something about your character that he just can’t explain (“I’ve always been good with dogs” or “I can read people easily”).

You get a number of Traits based on your Int.

Like Skills, Traits have Ranks, although all Traits start at Rank 0.

You can buy them up using CrP, but you also get a number of “Trait Points”, equal to your Pre that you can divide among them on a 1-point-to-+1-Rank basis.

Unspent Trait Points are lost after character creation, and you must spend as many as you can before you use CrP.

If you want to by up a Trait with CrP, cost to increase a Trait is its 1 + (Current Rank * 4).

Thinking of Traits

Thinking of Traits on the fly is hard. For new players, there's two ways of handling this: either picture a character from fiction you like or find interesting and try to describe them. These are their Traits, and you can crib off them to start with.

Or if you have no idea where to start or are just a risk taker, you can use the Trait Generator, which will give you a random Trait. These Traits may not always make sense, but they can often serve as a jumping off point for your imagination.

Racial Temperament

Your Racial Temperament comes from your race.

With GM permission, you can change this based on the setting and how your character personally feels.

Devotions

What relationships does he have? What organizations does your character associate with? Is he religious and, if so, is his loyalty more to his cult or to his god? How does he feel about that thing?

Relationships between individuals don’t need to be reciprocal or even contemporary. A young fighter may be inspired by a girl who doesn't love him back or by a long dead king he greatly admires. These are still viable candidates for his Devotion.

Everyone receives two free Devotions: one to their character’s clan/family and another of their choice. Each Devotion comes with a "relationship verb" and the thing itself. So, his clan Devotion could be "Hate Clan" or "Have mixed feelings about Clan" or "Loyal to Clan"

These Devotions start a 2.

Purchasing Devotions

You can also increase your Devotions or purchase new ones as well.

All new Devotions start at 1 and cost 4 CrP to purchase. From there, you can increase their size which costs 6 + (2 * your current Devotion). So, to go from a 2 to a 3 Devotion, it'll cost you 10 CrP ( 6 + ( 2 * 2 ) ).

Reputation

Finally, Reputation, which acts like a Trait but is controlled by your GM.

Reputation, in a nutshell, is what your character is known for and how other people see them. So, if you want to convince someone that you’ll keep your word, having a Reputation for doing so will help you out.

Reputation takes the form of 3 incomplete sentences starting with “I’m known for...”. Each time you finish a period of training, your GM will finish or change out one or more of those sentences and assign points to them that represent their strength. She’ll choose how to finish them based on how your character has been acting or the results of their actions.

That’s right: just like in real life, you don’t really have control over your Reputation, though there is a way to influence it as you go.

All your Reputations start at 0.

Chapter 1: Step-by-Step Overview

Which Race Should I Choose?

Your race effects character creation in 3 ways: Stat Score Modifiers, Gameplay Rewards, and Archetype Costs. If you're new to RPGs in generally, go for a race that interests you based on the flavor.

Stat Score Modifiers

These modify the base stats that you roll. You can use it to balance out your character's weak spots or you can use it to maximize what your character is already good it. We recommend the latter as Bonfire is a game built around teamwork: you'll have friends who will be good at things your character isn't.

Note that these adjustments can't bring score above 20 or below 1.

Gameplay Rewards

Each race is meant to encourage a certain type of play. For instance, orcs gain bonuses for starting things, like combats or Confrontations, so they reward players who are aggressive, while elves get rewarded for being careful with how they engage with others. These are noted under the Racial Temperament section in their summary.

Archetype Costs

Some races can spend less time mastering things than others, freeing up time (CrP) to focus on other pursuits or becoming true masters of their vocation. This is reflected in the Archetype Costs.

We who were trapped in prisons of flesh look to our forebears. For as the Alwealda ascended to return to the Divines, we, their children, will also ascend when we too have perfected ourselves in their sight.

The Elves are the first born of the Divines. Originally, the inhabitants of the Painted World, the source of all magic, they now inhabit the Middle Realm as mentally ethereal beings.

Appearance

Elves are slender beings that are widely considered classically beautiful, though many find their features alien and off putting. They uniformly have long ears that taper to a point at the end.

Their eyes are pure white, with no iris or pupil visible, this matched with their sharp teeth, can give them a feline turn to their face.

Elves naturally survive off the ebb and flow of magic that drifts through the air of the Painted World. While still present in the Middle Realm, these winds are a sliver of their natural magnitude and so Elves need to find other sources; the main one being through the magic inherent in all living creatures. While vegetables do have magic in them, an Elf would have to consume 100s of pounds of lettuce each day to not die of starvation. The only realistic way of getting enough magic to survive is through the consumption of raw meat.

Personality

To describe an Elf as “careful” would be a huge understatement. They tend to live longer than other races, not because some supernaturally long lifespan, but because they're always careful about their actions. They see other races as brash and impetuous while they're in turn seen as slow and cowardly.

It's not they're incapable of great emotion or quick action but elves seem to understand that with every action comes a consequence, whether that action was well meaning or not, and that this karmic force is more powerful than any other law or power.

However, once set on a course, it is hard to dissuade an elf, for they do nothing lightly.

Days of ashes and nights of fire; we were great warriors then. But our swords and shields were stolen from us and now we, the nomads, search for things that no hand can hold. Where must we go? We who search the wasteland for our better selves?

Heirs to a great empire long since lost to the ages, the orcs cling to tradition in the wake of uncertain and dark times. Some say that they value only strength but to truly known an orc is to know the soul of honor.

Appearance

Orcs have dark green or grey skin with porcine nose and tusks that jut up from their lower jaw. They tend to be stocky, with thick muscles and hunched shoulders.

Their hair is wiry, closer to steel wool than anything else and their eyes are completely black, with no pupils or irises, giving them an indescribable inhumanness to it.

Naturally, orcs are scavengers, able to survive on practically anything, no matter how rotten or foul.

Personality

An orc’s life is defined by struggle: from birth to death, they conceptualize the universe as having a vendetta against them. This can often give them a martyr complex and make them morose traveling companions at times because they feel the weight of the world.

They can also come across as aggressive, which some have pointed out is a defense mechanism, but orcs would simply scoff at that.

There’s nothing an orc values more than their honor and the chance to prove themselves against a worthy foe. This is often seen in their deities across orcish cultures, which are almost exclusively pantheons of their greatest foes. It is taken for granted that you are only as good as your enemies.
