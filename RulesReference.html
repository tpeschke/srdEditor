This chapter outlines the baseline mechanics that Bonfire uses for its systems.

When to Make Checks

If you’re an old hand at RPGs, this might seem like a silly section, but it is something to think about and it something that new GMs should learn.

When do you actually roll dice? And what do those dice do? Let’s take this one question at a time.

When to Roll Dice

Think about an RPG session like a conversation: there’s a back and forth between GM and players and a back and forth between players and other players.

But, unlike a normal conversation, there’s a point where dice are rolled, and the results read.

Very simply, it looks like the flowchart below.

99% of all RPG sessions will follow this flow, and those that don’t, only vary in minor ways, which we’ll talk about.

This is an extremely in-depth subject, but this section is primarily for new GMs so I’m only going to cover the basics.

Meaningful Failure

I wanted to take a small aside to talk about meaningful failure because, if there isn’t a chance of it it is the GM’s prerogative to simply wave the check, saying that the players succeed.

This is just a matter of streamlining the session: if failure doesn’t affect anything, there’s no point spending time on it.

This is usually meant in a mechanic sense (getting wounded) but it can also be in a story sense (it has no effect on the events going forward).

Session Critical

This is a common mistake for new GMs.

If you need a check to result in a certain outcome you should rewrite the session you had planned.

An example I see all the time is GMs putting the adventure hook behind a check. A man has a job for the players but will only give it to them if they can demonstrate that they have the skills.

But they fail all the rolls. The GM stops a second and then gives them the hook anyway.

If that’s the way the session is written; go back to the drawing board because it makes everything seem faked and contrived.

For me, personally, it also inflicts doubt in my actual agency as a player. Did I really beat the villain because of what I did or did the GM just fudge the outcome because that’s what they wanted?

What Do Those Dice Mean?

Something a lot of GMs don’t consider is the idea of structures within a game system.

There’s the resolution mechanic (how do I determine success or failure) but what that resolution mechanic means is based in the context of what is going on.

For instance, rolling an attack roll in a combat means you’re trying to physically hit someone or something with your weapon with the intent to kill or subdue while rolling to attack in an interrogation scene is more about striking to scare rather than wound.

That context is often nested in a structure. For instance, the combat is nested in the “Combat” structure which determines how often that attack can be made while the interrogation scene doesn’t work when you run it like a combat.

But a lot of games don’t give you structures to run different types of scenes: they don’t give you the structure that helps you (and your players) play that interrogation scene out.

Bonfire isn’t going to make that mistake and so the next 3 chapters are going to be 3 different structures that you can use for 3 very different types of scenes.

As you get familiar with them, you should play around with them; tweak them, add to them, remove parts from them, and combine them, but only do so once you’ve mastered them.

Doing so will open up and depth and breathe to roleplaying that will astound you and will give your players the tools to play some truly unique characters.

Making Checks

When you need to determine the outcome of a question or action, you’re going to make a Check.

You’ll do this by rolling a d20! and then adding any relevant Check Modifiers. A lot of times, these modifiers will be determined by the Skill or Training you’re using, but they can also come from your Traits or Flaws.

The opposition will also roll a d20! and add any relevant modifiers on their side.

The side with the high total wins the Check. What that means is dependent on the framework.

Exploding Dice

You’ll notice an exclamation mark (!) behind a lot of dice notations. This marks the dice as exploding, which means that should the maximum of that dice is rolled, you get to roll it again, this time with a - 1, and add what you rolled to the total.

This continues for as long as you roll the maximum dice, however, the - 1 always stays a -1 one.

So, if you roll a 3d8! for damage, you get a 5, 8, 8. Since you rolled the maximum value on two of your dice, you roll those dice again, and roll an 8, and 5, giving you a subtotal of 32 ( 5 + 8 + 8 + 8 - 1 + 5 - 1 ) but since you rolled another 8, you get to roll that dice again, and you get a 1, netting you a 0 ( 1 - 1 ).

There are two things you should be aware of: 1d100! explode to a 1d20!, while 1d20! explode to a d6!.

The second thing is that explosion dice don’t add additional dice to the number. So, in the example above, all that damage is only counted as having been done with 3 dice (instead of the 5 that were actually rolled).

Stress

Working under pressure can get to you. When your crew mates are relying on you to pull out a win, it can mess with your head. Your character can become stressed under these circumstances and stress can later manifest in odd behaviors.

Characters can gain Stress in a lot of ways and those are discussed in their various sections - taking damage, making Skill Checks, etc. - the only one we’ll go over here is from acting against your character’s Characteristics, especially when not playing them would be a huge benefit.

Stress Penalties

Stress affects almost everything you do, making your life harder and your character becomes emotionally pent up.

Every 10 points of Stress gives you a penalty of 1 to Defense and Skill Checks.

Healing Stress

Stress heals slowly over time: the more stress you have, the longer it takes to heal.

This is charted using Relaxation.

When a character’s Relaxation reaches their current stress, it resets to 0 and they lower their stress by 1, making high stress exponentially more difficult to heal.

But there are a lot of ways to gain Relaxation.

Taking Time Off

A person is considered to spend 8 hours sleeping, with the rest of the day (16 hours), doing things.

However, for each hour your character takes to rest (read a good book, indulge in a hobby, take a bath), they get 1 Relaxation.

Additionally, by spending silver, you can increase that amount on a 10-silver-to-1-Relaxation basis.

So, going out to dinner for 2 hours would normally give you 2 Relaxation, but going to a fancy dinner and spending 50 Credits on it, gives you 7 instead.

Flaws

Another option you have is to give yourself penalties due to your Flaws.

Having a Flaw increases the opponent’s Check by the Flaw’s Severity but heals you an amount of Stress equal to the roll of that die.

Creature Comforts

Finally, you can indulge in creature comforts.

This is similar to spending silver while taking time off, but these represent permanent purchases, like getting a really nice mattress for your bunk.

In this case, whenever a Trait would apply, you get an extra point of Relaxation.

So, if you’re hiking but you have extra-padded, tailored boots, you would get 3 Relaxation.

Mental Breakdowns

A character whose stress has gone beyond their Stress Threshold is suffering from a mental breakdown.

They seek to remove themselves from the stressful situation as quickly as possible and advance or gain a Flaw.

This is specific to the situation, but the character has lost all rationality and so will take the more direct route, even if it exposes them.

For example, a character that’s having a mental breakdown in the middle of the fight drops their weapon, stands up, turns, and runs for the nearest exit at full speed: exposing themselves to the enemy.

Or they might simply drop their weapon and plead for mercy – refusing to defend themselves.

Vitality

Vitality represents how close you are to passing out and dying. It is both your health and stamina. As you take damage, you will get closer to death and gain more penalties based on your Fatigue Threshold.

Physical States

Vitality is divided into 4 categories which are, in order, HURT (H), BLOODIED (B), WOUNDED (W), CRITICAL (C). Each of these make up a fourth of your Vitality.

These are a way to easily communicate a fighter’s health (both player-to-player and GM-to-player) but crossing certain categories have additional penalties.

Fatigue Threshold

As stated, the more damage you take, the harder the fight becomes. When this happens is noted by your Fatigue Threshold

Fatigue Threshold is matched to a wound category: hitting that wound category means that you start rolling your Defense Roll and all Skill and Confrontation Checks on 1 lower die.

When completely unencumbered, your Fatigue Threshold is at the maximum level, Critical, meaning you only become fatigued once you are near death.

Wearing armor, shields, and carrying lots of stuff may lower your Fatigue Threshold to a less injured category (e.g. Bloodied or Hurt), meaning you become fatigued more quickly. In a pinch, it may be wise to doff your shield or drop some gear to increase your fatigue threshold a notch.

Beyond Critical

A character whose total damage is beyond Critical is dead: roll up a new character.

The only exception is if your character is moving towards their goal.

In this case, they can continue to keep fighting until the end of the combat or until they reach double what they would need to reach Critical again.

However, this is a short lease: they’re still dead and no healing or medicine can bring them back.

Healing Damage

Whenever a character takes damage, they record another wound.

After a number of days equal to a wound’s current damage, you remove 1 damage from that wound.

This means that a 3-damage wound would take 3 days to become a 2-damage wound, which would take 2 days to become a 1-damage wound, and so on.

The upside is that all wounds heal simultaneously: if you have 10 3-damage wounds, it will only take you 6 days to completely heal.

There are other ways to speed up healing as well:

First Aid

By administering first aid within the first 10 minutes of a wound being inflicted, you lower the damage by 1.

You can only do this once per wound, however.

Medicine

By administering the proper medication, it can greatly speed up healing, removing 1 day from each wound for each point of damage, although there’s a minimum of 1 day.

So, for that 3-damage wound, it would only take 2 days to get to 2 damage, and 1 day to get to 1 damage.

Resting & Nursing

You can also take time off and not put a lot of stress and pressure on yourself: allowing your body to focus on healing.

In this case, if you have no Stress, every point of Relaxation gained that day is taken off the days to heal instead, however, you can only heal 1 damage a day and it only applies to 1 wound.

Fallen Comrades

Watching your friends die around you or abandon you is extremely Stressful, even if you’re winning.

Each time a side loses a quarter of their original number (either through dying, going unconscious, or running away), those remaining gain d10! Stress.

Taking a Breather

Pausing and resting for 1 heals you half the damage and Stress (rounded down) that you've take since the last time you've Taken a Breather.

If the rest is broken for any reason, you lose all benefit from it.

Honor

Honor is a measure how not only how a person measures their own integrity but also how other people see them. Honor isn’t about doing the commonsense thing; it’s about doing the heroic thing. People who run from a fight, even when outnumbered, lose Honor. People who let others talk bad about them and don’t get even lose Honor.

The question of Honor isn’t a question of good and evil; many evil men have been honorable. It’s more about living up to the ideals of your culture and being true to your inner self.

Categories & Bonuses

Honor has its benefits. Confidence allows you to push yourself a little bit more than you otherwise would. During the game, you can spend your Honor points to influence anything, rolls or static metrics, whenever you want on a 1 to 1 basis.

Doing so lowers your Honor but might save your character’s life. So, if you roll a 13 and your opponent rolls a 14, you could spend 3 Honor to beat their total. Likewise, if an enemy deals 8 damage to you and you have 6 Honor, you could spend all of it and only take 2 damage.

Additionally, each category has an Honor Dice, which will be talked about in the next sesseion.

Honor cannot go above 25 or below 0. The size of the Honor Die is also determined at the beginning of session and doesn’t change during play even if the category does.

Dishonor

0 - 5 Honor, No Honor Die

The lowest category, this person is unsure that they have what it takes to be a hero and so every decision is laced with hesitation and uncertainty.

Low Honor

6 - 10 Honor, d4!

This character hasn’t really proved anything, one way or another. Others and herself are waiting for her to make her mark on the world.

Average Honor

11 - 15 Honor, d6!

Having reached this category, the character has shown that they have potential and some follow through.

High Honor

16 - 20 Honor, d8!

She has now separated herself from the common rabble and can stand a head above the crowd.

Great Honor

21 - 25 Honor, d10!

This rare sliver represents those who can perfectly balance being ahead of others but not so much so that they invoke the ire of those below them as well as being confident while not being cocky.

Honor Dice

Fundementally, Honor Dice can be used in the same way as Honor, where it can be spent like Honor, only, instead of spending it on a 1-to-1 modifier, when you spend an Honor Die, you roll the corresponding die and add or subtract it.

Unless otherwise stated, uses of your Honor Dice are cross session so there's a place on your character sheet to record how many you have banked. As a new character, you'll start with 1 but as you go up Honor Categories, you'll gain more.

GMs are also free to reward Honor Die for particularly heroic behavior.

Honor Through Accountability

In the heat of the moment, it’s hard to keep track of all the bonuses and penalties that apply to situations, especially the penalties and especially Stress penalties, which change during the fight.

Because of this, it isn’t required that you apply Stress penalties. However, if you do, you gain 1 Honor, and the other side also applies their Stress penalty without that extra Honor.

If the GM reminds you that you need to apply your Stress, you don’t get anything but the penalty.

Chapter 1Basic Mechanics

Introduction

What is a Confrontation?

A Confrontation is any extended battle of wills.

This is mostly of two people in disagreement, who’re trying to convince the other to bend but it can also be the battle in a man’s soul as he tries to decide what to do.

You won’t break out this framework every time someone wants to haggle but this should be a tool in your belt to diversify the types of stories you tell and where you can get conflict.

When to Start a Confrontation

There are a lot of reasons why a Confrontation will take place, some better than others.

Can & Can’t Do

The first way to initiative a Confrontation is if both these criteria are met:

  1. The person (or people) can do what you want
  2. They’re on the fence

If the person can do what you want but they’re not on the fence about it, no Confrontation happens, so you can’t convince the holy knight to murder a baby: he can do it but he’s probably not on the fence about baby-killings.

If the person can’t do what you want but they’re not on the fence, in your favor, they’ll help where they can but, ultimately, it’s just not in their power.

So, a peasant you saved would be totally willing to help you take down the evil overlord but he’s unable to do so, so no Confrontation.

If a person can do what you want but isn’t on the fence but this time against you, no Confrontation. You’re not going to be able to convince your blood enemy to help you out by healing your wounds and giving you a hot meal.

Characteristics

All that being said, that’s not the end all be all; you can initiate a Confrontation if you can present it in such a way that it appeals to one of their Goals, Traits, Devotions, Racial Temperament, or Flaws.

Goals

If a person can be convinced that doing what you want them to do would forward one of their Goals, that can put them on the fence, allowing a Confrontation.

So, if you know that your archenemy is looking to expand his power, you can approach them; if you team up, you can both take down the bigger threat.

They might still say ‘no’ but showing them that your Goals align might help pave over a rocky relationship.

Traits

If a person has a Trait (or Racial Temperament) that applies, it might put them on the fence or convince them to attempt the action anyway.

For instance, if the knight had the Trait “Those above me know what is best” and is then tasked with killing a baby by his superior officer, that might initiate a Confrontation as he wrestles with his decision.

Devotions

Invoking Devotions area also a way to get people to consider things they normally wouldn’t.

So, if that peasant had a Devotion to the party, even though he can’t win against the evil overlord, he might still attempt it simply due to his love for the party.

Flaws

Finally, Flaws can cause people do things they normally wouldn’t.

For instance, a normally good knight who has an anger issue, driven mad by what the orc tribe has done to his hometown could enter a Confrontation to resist going on a murderous rampage and kill all the orcs, every single one of them. And not just the men, but the women and the children, too. They’re like animals, and he’ll slaughter them like animals.

Playing it Out

Okay, so what does a Confrontation look like?

Essentially, both sides will go back and forth presenting arguments to the other, which either counters it with their own arguments or concedes the point.

This very much resembles a normal conversation punctuated with Checks.

The end goal is to get more points than the opposition.

Time Scale

Because they’re a battle of wills, Confrontations have an extremely elastic time scale.

Sometimes, an entire Confrontation can happen within a second (in the terms of making a snap-decision), while other times, it could take weeks (like a trial or diplomatic summit).

And sometimes, it’ll vary.

Say you’re trying to convince an expert to join your crew.

You meet with him in his office where you do your initial pitch, but you quickly realize that there’s a lot you don’t know.

So, you don’t push the point, tell him to have a good day and then do two weeks of research, after which, you approach him again.

Getting Points

So, during a Confrontation, there are 3 ways to get points: the first is through presentation of Evidence, the second is to make an emotional Appeal, and the third is to have the opposition Concede the Point.

Evidence

Evidence is facts: things that you or the opposition believe to be true, even if it isn’t true in actuality.

When using them to convince the opposition, they should be things the opposition believes. If you’re using it to counter an argument, it should be something you believe.

Presenting or countering with Evidence gives you 2 points, regardless of what the other person does.

Appeals

Appeals use a person’s emotions to try and convince them. It is using the power of your belief to sway the other person.

In this case, you make a Check using your character’s Traits and adding the Check Mod that reflect their approach (default is Pre but you could add Str’s if, for instance, you were physically threating someone).

The opposition can counter with either an Appeal of their own or Evidence.

If defending against Evidence with an Appeal, you get 2 points, but you gain d4! Stress.

If both sides Appeal, the winner of the Check gets 1 point, the loser gets nothing, and both get d4! Stress.

Conceding the Point

If a person can’t counter an argument with their own, either through an emotional Appeal or through Evidence, they have to concede the point. This is most often signaled by them changing the subject.

In this case, the other side gets 3 points.

Multiple People in a Confrontation

If there are multiple people in a Confrontation, all making arguments, the opposition can choose which argument to respond to.

This doesn’t “waste” the unaddressed arguments – they can be tried again at a later time – but it doesn’t give points for using them at that moment

Getting Points

Okay, so a Confrontation will look very much like a normal conversation and there will be a lot more going on than just people presenting Evidence, making Appeals, or Conceding the Point.

Sometimes a side will just ask a series of questions to learn more about the other side or the whole Confrontation will just look like a tea party.

If there’s any confusion: ask what the intent is. If the intent is to try and convince the person, it might be time to add points.

If the point is anything else (information gathering, for example), then just keep things rolling.

Ending a Confrontation

In the end, one side will need to decide either way, which will end the Confrontation.

This can either be one side dropping it and walking away or it could be them pushing the point to get the other to make a decision.

Pushing the Point

When one side feels they may have the upper hand and that the other has run out of arguments, they can try to push the point, or end the Confrontation. He simply tries to push the other side to make a decision.

If it turns out that the other person does still have more to say, the Confrontation continues, gaining 1 point.

If, however, it turns out that the pushed person does, in fact, have no more to say, than the Confrontation ends and it’s time to compare point totals.

Victory or Defeat

At the end of the Confrontation, both sides reveal their points. The one with the most points gets their way.

Chapter 2Confrontations

Introduction

What is combat?

Combat is what happens when one side wants to kill the other and are trying to do it right now, on a second-by-second basis.

I don’t think I really need to define combat because, as a naturally violence species, combat often comes second nature to us but I thought it would be an interesting exercise.

Starting a Combat

The Clock

The Clock begins at 1, and then progresses to 2, 3, 4 etc, ad infinitum. When the Clock matches a character’s Initiative, they go. Each of these numbers represent 1 second of time passing. What second a character's next action is referred to as their initiative (or "where they are on the Clock")

This can be a difficult when you first start so we recommend that you start with only running two or three enemies until you get the hang of it.

Starting Initiative & Surprise

Each side rolls an initiative die. The lower the die, the more prepared they are for the attack, though typically the instigator goes on second 1. Those that know the battle is about to start (like being warned by a predetermined sign) use a d4, and the completely surprised receive a d20, while everyone else typically falls on a die between that.

Those whose initiative hasn’t come up are surprised and continue to do what they were doing as if nothing is wrong. They can take no other actions: they’re completely unaware of what’s going on.

Should someone attack them during this time, they are completely helpless and so their TN for defense is 1.

If the surprised person is hit, the pain quickly brings them into the reality of the situation, and they may act 2 seconds later (or before that if their initiative was the next second).

Combat Basics

Taking Actions

Every action takes a certain amount of time. On a character’s Initiative, they declare their action and are told how long that action will take. After that time, the action is complete.

So, if a character wants to walk across a room, which will take 8 seconds, and his Initiative is 5, once the Clock reaches 5, he starts walking and once it reaches 14, he’s gotten the other side of the room.

At any point, you may choose to forfeit your action and change your course. If, when halfway across the room (at second 9), the character sees that his buddy is in trouble, he can immediately decide that, no, he’d rather be there, his character stops walking across the room and instead heads over to help his friend.

Also, moving from a Run to Sprint, or vice versa, doesn’t reset that count, so don’t think you can skip taking damage by moving from a Sprint to a Run, you cheeky devil.

Zones of Engagement

There are 3 positions that you can have relative to your enemy. These positions aren’t reciprocal by nature: you can be threatened by someone and not have them be threatened by you.

Not Engaged

This is basically the default. You are too far away for them to reasonable do anything.

Threatened

This means that you’re within 5 feet + your opponent’s weapon’s measure. From here, if you move faster than a Jog and if they’re within half their weapon Recovery, they can attack you.

If you go from Engaged to Threatened, your weapon initiative doesn't reset, so if you become Engaged again for any reason before your weapon's Recovery, you may not attack.

Engaged

You’ve closed the distance and can actually make an attack. The person with the highest weapon measure attacks first. If both weapons are exactly the same measure, the attacks happen simultaneously.

Melee Combat

Melee Attack & Defense

Basic melee attacks always roll a base d20! + any attack modifiers, while defenders roll a d20! + any defense modifiers

Perfect Attacks & Defenses

A Perfect Attack or Defense is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll, while a Perfect Defense is an unmodified 20 on your Defense roll, but only if your final totals are still greater than you opponent’s. If not, the attack still hits for Perfect Attacks and you still dodge the blow for Perfect Defenses but you don’t gain the auxiliary benefits.

If both those qualifiers are met, you gain the following benefits on top of the normal ones:

Perfect Attack

You gain a free automatically successful Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Advanced Training in Unarmed Combat, you may roll for the additional benefit, but even on a failure, you gain the move’s base benefit.

You may also Shift with no penalty and change your Tactic.

Perfect Defense

You gain a free automatically successful Unarmed Combat attack. If you have Advanced Training in Unarmed Combat, you may roll for the additional benefit, but even on a failure, you gain the move’s benefit.

You may also Shift with no penalty and change your Tactic.

Additionally, you gain a chance to riposte, gaining a free full attack the next second if you’re wielding a melee weapon. This attack doesn’t affect your current count.

Near Perfect Attacks & Defenses

An unmodified roll of 19 on either Attack or Defense results in a Near Perfect Attack or Defense, where you may Shift for no penalty, change your Tactic, or, for attacks, gain an extra d4! to damage, or, for defenses gained a free riposte with your off-hand weapon if it’s smaller than your size.

Dealing Damage

Once both sides have rolled and added all modifiers, compare the totals against each other.

If the defender’s is equal to or greater than the attacker, the attack misses.

Parrying

If a melee attack roll is within the Parry Threshold of the defender’s total, the defender may choose to deflect some of the incoming attack.

The attacker rolls their damage and subtracts the shield or weapon’s DR (2/d DR for weapons), which subjects them to a chance of breakage, and then their armor’s DR. Any leftover is still done to the defender. For piercing weapons, the /d DR of the shield or weapon is tripled.

So, a defender with a Parry Threshold of 4, and a total of 10 for their defense roll vs an attack roll of 14, could choose to parry the blow, taking less damage.

However, if it’s not, the attack lands and the attacker rolls his weapon’s damage die and adds any appropriate damage modifiers. The defender than subtracts their Damage Reduction and the remaining total is subtracted from their Vitality and added to their list of wounds.

Damage Reduction

Armor allows you to reduce the amount of damage taken. This is often times shorted to DR. There are two types: Static, noted by just a number, and Per Die, noted by a number /D.

Static simply subtracts that number from the damage and is done.

/D subtracts that number multiplied by the number of dice the attack is using. So, if someone is attacking you with a slashing weapon (2d6!), and you have armor that gives you 4/D, you’d subtract 8 from the damage.

Either way, if the attacker chooses to remain in engaged, he then adds his weapon’s Recovery to the current second on the Clock. Once the Clock reaches that new number, he may attack again.

Knock-Backs

If the pre-DR damage is higher than your enemy’s Vitality Size Modifier + 1 for every M item you're carrying, the attack also moves them back 5 feet. If it’s twice the damage needed, they get knocked back 10 feet, and so on.

Ranged Combat

Ranged Attack & Defense

Ranged attacks and defenses function identical to melee attacks and defenses (including Flanking), only get a few additional bonuses and penalties.

The first is that range (of course) and the second is movement.

When defending against a ranged attack, you get a +2 for each Movement Category above standing still. So, if you're getting shot at, get the lead out.

Perfect Attacks

A Perfect Attack is one that puts you in a better position to win. A Perfect Attack occurs on an unmodified roll of 20 on your Attack roll.

If you’re firing a ranged weapon, you managed to hit a sensitive area, lowering their max movement speed by one category, minimum Crawl speed.

Near-Perfect Attacks

An unmodified roll of 19 on your Attack results in a Near Perfect Attack, giving you an extra d4! to damage.

Cover

Cover is anything that gets between you and the person trying to shoot at you.

For solid objects, such as walls, where it is easy to calculate how much of the person is covered, a flat bonus is appropriate. For every 10% covered, you get a +2, so 25% would be a +5, 50% would be a +10 and so on.

For people, tree, a moving object, or really anything that’s harder to calculate, you’ll use a dice backed on the difference in size, starting at a d20. Each difference in size shrinks or increases that cover die by 1 size.

Cover dice for most objects works the same as shields - if you’re behind cover, you add a roll of that dice to your defense. If you’re actively crouching behind it, you add the max it would give you to your defense plus another roll of the dice.

So, two medium size people standing one in front of the other would give the guy in the back d20 cover, same as two small sized people. If the person in the front is small sized and the guy in the back is still medium sized, the guy in the back only gains a d12 cover.

If the person is actively moving or trying not to be your cover, you can’t gain the bonus for actively crouching behind them.

If you don't move, however, cover becomes more and more useless as your shooter sights in. For each shot against you where you haven't moved, you either lower your effective cover percentage by 10 or shrink your cover dice by 1 size, depending on whether the cover is static or not.

Note this also applies to shields as well, so if you don't move while using a shield, it becomes less

Firing Into Melee

If there’s any one within 5 ft of a direct line between the origin of the missile and the target, they’ll have to roll the appropriate defense die vs the original attack roll to see if the missile hits them instead, starting with the person closest to the archer or gunner and ending with the intended target.

All the normal rolls apply so if the missile only misses because of cover, the missile has lodged itself in the cover and goes no further.

Dealing Damage

Dealing damage with a ranged weapon works almost exactly like dealing damage with a melee weapon, however, you’re unable to Parry a ranged attack (that’s what Cover is for)

Ranged Misses

On a miss with a ranged weapon, the missile doesn’t magically disappear into oblivion. There’s a good chance it might hit someone else, especially if your target is locked in melee.

In the case of a miss, roll a d20! and a d4. The d4 determines to which side the missile strays - an odd result means it goes left, an even result means it goes right.

The d20! determines how many feet it strays from the target. Draw an imaginary line between the archer and that point. Everyone within 5ft of that line needs to roll a defense vs the original attack roll, starting with the person closest to the archer and ending with the furthest possible target.

Inanimate objects may provide enough cover to be considered targets in both Firing into Combat and Ranged Misses, such as a large boulder or tree. In which case, medium sized objects roll a d12!, with each size lower decreasing the dice size by 2, and each size larger increasing the dice size by 2.

Misc. Combat Rules

Facing & Fighting Multiple People

The likelihood is that at some point you’ll want to be flanking or someone with want to flank you, to try and gain an advantage.

Defending right in front of you is pretty easy and so brooks no penalties, however, every 45 degrees off that the defender lowers their Defense dice by 1 size, with those being attacked from directly behind defend on a d8! since they take advantage of a huge blind spot.

This penalty can be mitigated through the use of shields. Small shields allow you to defend against an additional assailant, depriving them of that bonus, while Medium and Large ones allow you to defend against an extra 2 and 3, respectively.

A maximum of 8 people at a time can effectively attack person at once, assuming that they’re out in the open. After that, every assailant’s attack die is lowered by 1 size per extra person.

Trauma

If someone does more damage after DR than your Trauma Threshold, that triggers a Con Check vs d20! + the damage dealt.

Failure means that your character has been overcome with pain to the point that he’s unable to act, leaving him completely defenseless for a number of seconds equal to how much he failed by times 3.

Double Trauma

A character that gets dealt their full Vitality in damage in a single blow has suffered a grievous wound - the scar of which, if he survives, will be with him until the day he dies.

When this happens, the trauma is too much, and they simply die where they stand if they fail their check. Even if they succeed, they’re still suffer Trauma for the next 30 seconds but gain a wicked looking scar.

Battle scars are marks of pride and honor. Characters who become scared gain a 3d4 Trait “Battle Scars”. If they would get this Trait again, increase all dice by 1 size.

Coup De Grace

A helpless enemy is one completely at your mercy.

If a character is completely helpless, either from failing a Trauma check, being bound, or deep asleep, a character can kill the target instantly.

This move takes 10 seconds to complete as it takes a bit of positioning to pull off. The character’s whose throat is being slit gives out a cry of pain as they expire.

Strategies

These rules add another layer of strategy to combat and should be implemented as soon as you have the basics down.

A combatant utilizing these moves can only commit to one at a time, unless otherwise stated and can only switch on their attack unless it says otherwise.

Active Dodging

This move relays on actively focusing on dodging against incoming attacks.

A defender commits to how much he wants to focus on dodging incoming attacks, both melee and ranged, choosing from a bonus to his defense in the range of +1 to +5, but netting an equal penalty to his attacks (-1 to -5).

Additionally, this lowers the range for Near Perfect Defenses by 1, so you score them on a base natural 18 - 19. This tactic needs to be committed to for at least your full weapon Recovery. After that you may continue using it and change tactics at any point.

But focusing on defense does slow you down, since you're zigzagging and trying to juke out your enemy, so your movement is considered 1 Category lower for how far you move. So, if you're Medium sized and Active Dodging while at a Run, you would move at 10 ft / sec, instead of 15. You would still be subject to the damage, however, since you're still technically running.

Active Dodging can be combined with Retreat and Step Back, adding only the attack penalties but gaining all auxiliary benefits from each (so no, the defenses don't stack).

Careful Aiming

By taking a moment or two, you can sight in with any ranged weapon, giving you a better chance to hit your target, however, there are diminishing returns after a bit.

For the first 3 seconds, you gain an increasing bonus of +1 to hit. So, the first second you’re aiming, you get a +1, the next a +2 (for a total of +3), and the third you get a +3 (for a total of +6).

After that, that bonus shrinks by 1 each second. So, the fourth second you get a +2 (for a total of +8) and the fifth second you get a +1 (for a total of +9).

Careful Aiming can only be performed with a ranged weapon and can only be performed when the fighter is standing still. On top of this, the fighter takes Stress equal to the bonus they receive when they finally make the shot.

Further delay gives you no bonuses.

Choking Up on Your Weapon

When you’ve choked up on your weapon, your weapon’s Measure is halved, and you add that number, rounded down to its Recovery. Your halved Measure is considered your new Measure for determining who attacks first and what Tactics can be employed on you as well.

However, if an opponent is In Your Guard and you choke up on your weapon, you defend with a standard 1d20! and deal full weapon damage.

You’re unable to employ this tactic twice if someone again gets within your guard.

Feint

When performing this move, you (hopefully) get your enemy to commit his guard to a different location than you plan on hitting.

Before you roll, if you declare a Feint, you receive a +5 to that Attack but receive a -3 to Defense until your next attack.

Fend Off

If your weapon's measure is at least 1 foot greater than your opponent's, you can try and fend them off. This means that you may attack them freely, while they can't do the same to you.

You may only use this Tactic as someone is going from Threatened to Engaged, in which case they're automatically considered to be Fended Off. To full becoming engaged with you, they'll have to knock your weapon aside with a regular attack that deals no damage. Should they hit, they become fully engaged and can attack freely again.

Fend off can be combined with Jab.

In Their Guard

If you're engaged with a person and have a weapon that is at least 1 foot shorter than your opponent, you may attempt to get in their guard. On a successful attack that deals no damage, the shorter weapon fighter has successfully knocked his foe's weapon aside and is no fighting at extreme close range, cause them huge penalties.

The longer weapon may only defend with a d12!. All damage dice is also reduced by one size (3d4! → 3d3!). They may, in turn, employ the Choke Up on Your Weapon Tactic.

Jab

A strike that is meant to purposely to open your opponent’s guard and get them off their balance by making them shift their defense.

The Jab attack happens at half weapon Recovery (rounded up) for Piercing and Slashing weapons.

Instead of a d20!, the defender uses a d12! but on a successful hit, no damage is done, and the defender nets a -1 to their next full defense against a non-jab attack.

This penalty doubles with each jab, however, it only lasts until the defender’s next full defense.

Crushing weapons may use this tactic but it’s not any faster than their regular Recovery.

Jab can be combined with Fend Off.

Power Attack

This is the fighting equivalent of baseball batting your enemy, swinging with all your might.

With this attack, you receive a -3 to your Attack and next defense but your damage increases

Piercing weapons increase 1 die by 1 size. Slashing weapons increase all dice by 1 size and crushing weapons increase its highest die by 2 sizes.

Retreat

This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back at jog or at a 45 degrees angle from your opponent’s blows at a walk.

After your defense roll, if you declare a Retreat, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll but also a -5 to your next Attack and must continue to move back until your next attack, either at a jog, if moving directly back, or at a walk, if started to move at a 45 degrees angle.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Feint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage

Shift

You move at your walking speed to either side of your opponent but suffer a -1 to your next Attack and Defense for every 5 ft you travel before your next Attack or Defense.

However, if you Shift with another tactic, you don’t receive this penalty and gain a +2 that can be either used to increase a bonus or decrease a penalty from that tactic. You may do this freely with any Tactic except Retreat and Step Back.

Shifting is also used to change Facings so if you’re engaged with an enemy and you Shift. And don’t forget that you get those tasty bonuses from flanking your enemy if they don’t also Shift to face you.

Step Back

This is a defensive tactic that involves moving directly back from your opponent’s blows at your walking speed.

Before your defense roll, if you declare a Step Back, you receive a +5 to your next Defense roll but on your next attack roll shrink the damage of your dice based on its type and must continue to move back at a Walking speed until your next attack.

Piercing weapons lower 1 of their dice by 1 size. Slashing lowers all dice by 1 size and crushing lowers their highest die by 2 sizes.

If your opponent is using the Flurry of Blows, Faint, or the Power Attack tactic, if they do hit, you only receive half damage.

Retreat v. Step Back

While they may seem similar on the surface, these two different tactics have some key differences I thought I’d point out so that you can utilize each better.

  1. Step Back must be declared before you roll your defense while Retreat can be declared after, meaning that if you see you rolled low before employing them.
  2. Step Back moves your character straight back, while Retreat allows you to move up to 45° to the left and right, meaning that if you’re going to be backed against a wall, it’s an option to try and skirt your opponent.
  3. Retreat may be done at a jogging speed, while Step Back is always at a walk.

Ending Combat

Combat ends when one side is incapacitated, reached a truce, or have fled the field. At that point the Clock stops, and play moves back to narrative time.

If you engaged in a Morale Confrontation, everyone on the side that won the combat gains the difference in points to their Honor, regardless of whether or not their side has less. So, if the players have 3 points and the goblins had 7, every player still gains 4 Honor.

The losing side subtracts that with the same caveats.

Chapter 3Combat

Introduction

Challenges cover a lot of ground: in fact, it’s easier to say that pChallenges are anything that’s not covered by Confrontations or Combats.

I know, that doesn’t help, so here’s a more practical definition:

A Challenge is when the characters have a thing they want to accomplish (an Objective) but one or more things that would result in meaningful failure stand in their way (Obstacles).

Challenges are a framework that you can use as a catch-all for all the other types of situations that might come up in play.

Objectives

What do the players want? What thing, material or immaterial do they want? Where do the players want to be?

This is the Objective and is often determined by the Goals of the characters involved.

Setting up the endpoint allows you to get the scale of a Challenge down, which can help you get a scale and number of the Obstacles the characters are going to face.

Obstacles

Obstacles are places along the path to the Objective where meaningful failure could occur.

And we’re defining “meaningful” here as “things that will either effect the likelihood and shape of the Objective or the next Obstacle”.

So, for instance, if you’re breaking into house, climbing the wall could result in meaningful failure as a fall could damage the character, making it more difficult for them to perform the next task.

Or, if you’re researching a spell, an Obstacle could be taking notes: poor notes could mean that either you take longer to get the spell or you miss an important part of the shaping, and your spell now has a side effect.

The Obstacles should make sense: don’t feel like you need to force them or hit an arbitrary number, although you might find that how characters deal with Obstacles could result in new Obstacles or another Challenge.

For instance, fixing the serious side effects from shaping that new spell could result in another Challenge where the characters now have to figure out a way to undo a negative enchantment.

Or it could just result in another Obstacle. It all depends on the focus of the game.

Difficulty

Difficulty is a dice that is added to the Skill Check based on things that might be going on at the time to either help or hinder your success. This difficulty is noted just as a single dice (+d6!, for example) with you’ll add to the d20!.

Complications & Failures

Failing a Check doesn’t automatically mean that you’re instantly screwed: Failing a single Move Silently Check doesn’t result in the guards catching you. In Bonfire, there are Complications and hard failures.

Every Obstacle will have a number of Complications it can gain before it hard fails. This is called its Failure Threshold. You gain Complications by failing Skill Checks: every 5 points below what you needed to get gives the Obstacle 1 Complication, rounded up (so a failure by 1 - 5 is still considered a Complication).

Complications represent complications, such as the guard hearing something, or you leaving a clue behind that might give someone a heads up that you're there. An Obstacle gets +2 for every Complication is has on it.

When an Obstacle gets more Complications than its Failure Threshold, it hard fails. Hard failures are when something bad actually happens. In this case, it does result in you getting caught by the guards, or it results in your falling off the wall and taking damage. Even after a hard failure, you can sometimes try again, like in the case of falling off the wall.

Failure doesn’t diminish during the Skill Challenge, but it does reset for a new one.

Complications in the Narrative

Complications don’t mean that the Skill Challenge grinds to a halt while the players keep reattempting that Skill: instead, it should represent the players coming closer to danger.

For a quick example, let’s say that there’s an adventurer, we’ll call him New York Smith, York for short, who is attempting to jump a chasm, which has a Failure Threshold of 1.

He rolls his Check but fails by 7, giving the Obstacle 1 Complication. In this case, he obviously jumped but also didn’t make it. What does that mean? Well, York didn’t quite make it but managed to grab the ledge as he fell, so he didn’t fall to his death, but he isn’t safe either.

York is grabbing hold of the ledge so he can't make another Jumping Check, but he does reason that he can use Climbing. Makes sense to the GM so they roll again. If York fails this time, he falls, but if he succeeds, he manages to scramble up the cliff, safe on the other side, but he is working at a disadvantage: the GM gets +2 to her roll this time around so he better hope he put Ranks in Climbing.

Success & Boons

All that is required to overcome an Obstacle is 1 successful Check, although clever players who can think their way around a problem can, of course, bypass an Obstacle in a different way.

GMs might find it useful to break Obstacles up into Skill Challenges based on the level of granularity they're looking forward in their game. For instance, if the players treking over a wilderness, if the focus of play isn't on the travel, they might consider that one Obstacle, while a game that is about travel might break it up into multiple Obstacles the players need to overcome before they're considered to have successfully crossed.

Boons

These are the opposite of Complications: they give you a small benefit or boost for accomplishing a test extra well. You get 1 Boon for every 5 full points above what you needed.

Fallout

It is assumed that, during a Challenge, a character is working their hardest to win, therefore, every Check, whether successful or not, causes d4! Stress or 2d4! Stress if using a Weirdcraft Advanced Skill.

However, a Boon can be spent to not gain this Stress.

Time Scale

Like Confrontations, Challenges don’t have a set time scale they work off of, however, unlike Confrontations, once their time scale is set, there’s very little variation.

So, for instance, breaking into a house could have a time scale of a couple of hours, while tracking a bandit could have a scale of days, weeks, or months.

Sometimes an Obstacle will be on a longer or shorter time scale but that should be handled on a case-by-case basis.

For instance, while tracking Corwin, the characters find themselves lost in the old mines. This is an Obstacle that will take a few days to overcome.

Later, they come across a chasm that used to have a utility bridge, but it was destroyed after Corwin crossed it, so they need to find another way to get across.

Another Obstacle within the same Challenge but this time it only takes hours to resolve.

Strategies

Assets & Contacts

These represent static pools of knowledge or information that you can pull from if you have time. So, for instance, a book on the subject, or a library are examples of an Asset but also an old friend who used to be a ranger and who could give you a few pointers could also be considered one.

Anything you can think of that might apply might be an Asset you can draw on.

Assets have two stats: their Knowledge and their Organization. Basically, how much they know and how easy that knowledge is to get.

Each of these is given a qualifier of either Poor, Average, or Good.

For Knowledge, Poor represents 1 reroll, Average 2 rerolls, and Good 3 rerolls while for Organization Poor is d4! Days, Average is d4! Hours, and Good is d4! Minutes.

Spending an Asset's Organization gives you its rerolls based on its Knowledge.

That bonus can then be redeemed on a Skill Check later on. This can also be applied to Weapon, Armor, and Shield Trainings through manuals and sparring.

You don’t automatically know the Knowledge or the Time Investment of an Asset.

Another benefit of Assets is that it is often multifaceted and can be used to get different bonuses. That old ranger friend can help you with navigation but also probably knows a thing or two about botany.

Assets can also be increased and expanded upon as well, increasing its qualifiers. A good relationship with a contact might give you information quicker, donating to the local library may increase its selection, or hiring excavators to dig up a ruin may unearth more artifacts that help you decipher that ancient script.

There may be some special cases where an Asset will take care of itself, but you may have to check back on it, such as Snaring in the forest. You set your trap and then a day or two later, you can go back and check it to see if you caught anything - in the meantime, you can get other work done.

Redeeming Contacts

Characters get a number of starting contacts based on their Presence, with the expectation they'll gain more as they adventure, but these starting contacts can prove useful as they can be redeemed at any time and defined on the spot.

So, if the players need, for example, access to a monastery’s library, one of the players can remember that one of his buddies is actually a monk there and might be able to help, redeeming one of his contacts.

After that, of course, contacts can be used time and time again: they're part of the world.

How a contact helps is largely determined by the circumstances of why they were redeemed but the GM should, for the most part, consider them Assets starting with Poor Knowledge and Organization. A player can increase that when they redeem a contact by sacrificing additional contacts, raising one of their stats on a one-for-one basis.

So, a character with 3 unredeemed contacts can bring one in as a smuggler buddy who knows a lot about the local underworld, which would normally only cost him 1 of his contacts, but, instead, he sacrifices his 2 remaining ones to increase his smuggler buddy's Knowledge to Good. He can't redeem any more contacts but at least his smuggler buddy knows what he's talking about.

Roleplaying Out a Scene vs Using Assets

Assets are not meant to replace specific roleplaying areas where you need specific pieces of information: they’re meant to reward players for remembering to do otherwise boring and mundane things that might still help their character. You can also combine the two.

For instance, if the players are hunting a griffon, they might consider talking to a local monster hunter, who, though a scene, tells them specifics of how the griffon hunts and fights. During this time, the players learn that griffons tend to nest in mountains, so, after the talk is finished, one of the players hits up an old friend who knows a lot about climbing and spends a few hours training and getting points with him.

The first scene is roleplayed in detail while the second is handwaved because there’s nothing specific that the player will learn that will help them. Additionally, the first scene could also give the players a bonus to their Monstercraft roll for remembering additional things about the beast.

Using Other Players as Assets

I think it's completely reasonable that players would want to use each other's characters as Assets and why shouldn't they? So, in this case the player’s Knowledge is instead based around the Rank they have in the applicable Skill with their Organization being based on their Characteristics.

If they have 3 or fewer Characteristics related to teaching or communication, they’re considered Poor; 4 to 6 they’re considered Average; greater than 6, Good.

GMs should also feel free to reward players who sit there and talk things out and plan by awarding the Asset bonus as well, even if they don't spend a full hour+ planning.

Equipment

Your kit can really pull you through in a pinch and these are catchall rules for the use of equipment that doesn’t already come with auxiliary benefits, such as weapons (damage) and armor (DR).

Players are assumed to be using all equipment that would help them with a Check if they have time so they shouldn’t feel the need to go through their list of climbing gear every time they scale a wall, although, if time is short and they need to rush, that may be appropriate.

On a Skill Check, the first piece of equipment you use gives you a free reroll, taking the higher of the two results. After that, each additional piece gives you a +1, although if this additional gear has Traits that apply, you can use that instead of the +1. More on equipment Traits is found in the Deluxe version of Chapter 6:Equipment.

Redeeming this bonus causes the tool degrades, giving it (and any other equipment you use) 1 Wear, whether you succeed or not. More on Wear can be found in Chapter 6:Equipment.

Asset- & Equipment-Based Skilled

Some skills are only going to be useful if you have the proper equipment or the right Asset. For instance, if you’re not out in the wilderness, you can’t really use Snaring or if you don’t have the proper tools or a woodshop, Maintenance won’t do you any good. In cases, being able to make the Check in the first place is considered an auxiliary benefit and so this bonus doesn't apply. This will need to be handled on a case-to-case basis.

Helper Skills

Let’s say that you’re tracking someone in the wilderness. You have Hunting at Rank 4 and Endurance at Rank 3. Seems like being able to move quickly over a longer period of time, should help you catch the guys, and it does.

If you have another Advanced Skill that you think might help you, you can add it as a bonus, however doing so costs you d4! Stress for every Skill you apply. This can't be reduced by Not Sweating It.

This may seem like it is nearly identical to Preparation, and they are very close. The difference is that Preparation is a general thing that applies to a Skill Check you’re going to make in the future, while using a helper Skill is in the moment and is specific to the scenario. You can use both at the same time, since the help Skill gives a bonus and Preparation gives a reroll.

Skill Mobbing

For GMs, note that Helper Skills are how you handle Skill Mobbing, where everyone just overpowers a roll by all rolling against it. Instead, only one person rolls and only those with an applicable Advanced Skill and willing to take that Stress add their Rank to help out.

Multi-Tasking

Let’s be realistic here: you’re probably going to end up trying to do two things at once, like climbing a wall and doing so quietly.

If that’s the case, you’re going to have to “split” your d20! into smaller dice and assign those to each of the Checks you’re trying to make, because your attention is divided. The total max value of the dice can never exceed 20 but it can be lower than it. The other side will still be rolling full d20!s against you.

So, if you’re climbing a wall and trying to do so quietly, you’d have to make a Climbing and a Move Silently Check. You could split it like so: d12! for Climbing and d8! for Move Silently, or d10! for Climbing and d10! for Move Silently, or d12!+d4! for Move Silently and d4! for Climbing.

Or you could just say “screw being quiet” and roll a d20! for Climbing and take a 0 for Move Silently. In this case, the GM would just roll against your Skill Rank. So, if you choose to ignore Move Silently but have a Rank 4 in it, the GM would roll their dice, and you can hope they don’t roll higher than 4.

Either way, besides this, it works like a normal Check, except that, for the Stress gain, you're going to roll 1d4! for each Skill they're attempting but only use the highest if applicable. They need to succeed all Checks by 5 to completely remove the Stress gain.

Not Paying Attention

Multi-Tasking is also used when someone isn't paying attention, such as a guard who's on watch but he's been there for several hours at this point so he's not doing it so closely. In this case, start at a d10!, lowering it further as needs be.

Helping Others

It feels good to help people and Bonfire is all about group cooperation. The fighter helps the skill monkey survive combat and the skill monkey helps the fighter not look like an idiot while doing detective work.

And how you help other characters will really depend on the situation. Perhaps your Preparation can be used to help another person hide or perhaps you have a bit of knowledge that they don’t.

Really, this will be done to what feels right in the moment, but this note is here to draw attention to someone helping you as an Asset and someone adding their Rank to yours with a Helper Skill, and how they’re different.

And the difference is time. If you have plenty of time to coach someone while they’re climbing a cliff face, you’d be an Asset and you wouldn't take Stress.

But if you’re being chased by the guards and it’s a matter of seconds or minutes, you’d add a Helper Skill, and you would need to take the Stress.

Note that if the skill monkey is also trying to climb the cliff face and coach as they go, that would be considered a d10! worth of effort for Multi-Tasking, though you don’t have to actually roll to see if you help.

Not Sweating It

While the default is to assume that characters actually care about the outcome of the Check, that's not always the case since not everything is equally important. In the cases of lesser importance, the character can simply not sweat it.

In this case, they take no Stress from the Check but take a -4 penalty.

Preparation

Preparation is anything your character does to help a specific Skill Check that will be made in the future (even if that future is 2 seconds from now). Note that this doesn't have to be a Check you make: it can also apply to an ally's Check (if it makes sense).

Usually, these only take a couple of seconds but can vary widely depending on the circumstances. Throwing a rock is a matter of moments while constructing a gilly suit will take a lot longer.

As long as there is time, there is no chance of failure or, if the players have plenty of time, Preparation just happens. Mark what you did for later in the Skill Challenge.

However, if there is a time crunch or a chance of failure, Preparation works like a normal Skill Check with success means the preparation is actually useful.

Either way, you only get one Prep Check per Advanced Skill that applies for each upcoming Check, unless you have Assets but remember that they do take time.

That means that if you know you're going to have to make a jump, you can use Acrobatics to stretch, Endurance to do a few practice sprints to the edge, and Sports (long jumping) to remember techniques all to prep for the same Jump check.

When you redeem Preparation that you’ve readied, you can reroll Obstacle's d20, taking the worse of the two results.

Planning & Action Phases

While it isn’t entirely black and white, it’s helpful to think of Challenges as composing two phases: planning and action; the laying out the plan and then the heist.

Planning is gathering intel, getting your team and equipment together, and planning your route of attack. This is where you’ll use Preparation and Assets mostly.

Action phase is when you’ll be doing a lot of the Checks and Stress, as well as equipment and companions - using what you learned in the Prep Phase to help you get through the sticky parts.

But don’t get too hung up on separating these ideas though. Even during the heist, you might find it worth your time to sit on your heels and take your time while doing some Preparation as there will almost certainly be problems that crop up that you need to adjust to.

Rushing It

If you’re in a hurry to get the job down, you can just rush it. This lowers the time a Check actually makes but gives you a -5.

The bonus for this is that Complications you would get from the Check don't get added to Universal or Linked Obstacles in the Challenge, though Hard Failures do add 2 Complications to them.

Favor

Favor is a measure of your standing with a given god. It’s not an absolute measure, however, but more a measure of the chance that a Divine will answer your prayer. How far your Favor goes ranges widely based on how devoted you are to a god and how Honor you are.

Invoking Miracles

Now that you’ve got a nice stash of Favor, you probably want to spend it. That’s pretty simple: pray out loud for the Miracle in question. Silent prayers don’t work because no Divine is going to answer a prayer, they’re not going to get credit for.

For every Favor you spend, you get another use of your Honor Dice but comes with a few differences: the first, and most major, is that you can spend it on ANYTHING - you want to run faster or jump higher? You want to heal your wounds? You want to attack slightly faster? You can with Favor, however, the effects are only temporary, of course.

The second is that you can spend to change the world around you. Every Favor you spend can change a 5 ft square area with the following restriction: it can’t directly contradict things that have already been said.

For instance, you cannot wish to have an avalanche suddenly on the steppe or an inn to appear before the party that wasn’t there before, but you could wish for a flash flood or for an inn to be over the next rise, which was just out of view when you prayed.

Restrictions

Miracles are bound by Covenant: you can’t pray for something your Divine doesn’t have power over, so a Divine of the city can’t really help you in the mountains, for example.

But this is where being in a pantheon does help out: you can pray to another Divine in your pantheon for their blessing instead, but it requires twice as much Favor, and you don’t gain the benefit of your Cosmic Link.

The other restriction is that the Miracles need to be subtle: no angels coming down from the skies to keep you from falling to your death but perhaps there’s water or a passing hay cart that catches you just in time to soften your landing. There needs to be room for doubt.

Miraculous Healing

Servants can invoke miracles to heal their companions if their Covenant is specifically healing related.

However, the number that it rolled is subtracted from the days a wound has left to heal, not from the damage of the wound.

Acquiring Favor

Servants turn their eyes from the earth below to higher worlds, both literally and metaphorically. In this way, they’re the keeper of the times and seasons, as well as the calendar to note the occurrence of Holy Days.

As such, players who play Servants will need to keep track of the passage of time. Doing so gives them special bonuses according to the schedule laid out below.

You’ll note that it follows the Gregorian Calendar layout with holy days happening according to the Judeo-Christian traditions. The assumption that the characters will be following an in-game calendar with random Holy Days specific to their cult but we’re “translating” into the Gregorian calendar, in the same way that we assume the characters are speaking in their native fantasy tongues and not in English.

Doing this makes it easier for players to keep track because the calendar is a familiar shape. They won’t have to track out a whole year and remember that Beltane is on the 5th of Seed’s Row.

Rules for using your own calendar and keeping all Servants relatively balanced against each other is going to be the Deluxe Rules.

Favor is unique to the Divine you’re petitioning. Gaining Favor with another Divine, means that you lose an equal amount of Favor with all others because gods are bad at sharing.

Every Day

These are small, daily sacrifices. These tend to cost about 1 or 2 sc, 5 to 6 points of Stress, or 1 or 2 Vitality, depending on your Divine's preferences. They can be the sacrifice of a small, sacred animal, the burning of a portion of your meal, or the lighting of incense during morning prayers.

Servants

Anointed

Unanointed

Sabbatic Holy Days

Every 7 Days

This is a day of rest and contemplation, where the faithful are instructed to put aside the things of the world and focus on the Divine with activities related to their Covenant.

There is an emphasis on ritual over practicality to emphasize this divide, so while a Divine of Battle encourages his followers to fight every day, on his sabbath, he only wants them to focus on ritualistic combat and controlled bloodshed, as opposed to actual combat.

Again, this is to remind his followers of the holiness of the action and by forcing his followers to stop and think about their actions, he forces them to contemplate the deeper realities of combat.

The sabbath takes up 1 to 3 hours and the rest of the day can be spent at the faithful wants.

Servants

Special

This die is equal to your Honor dice if you’re anointed to the Divine you’re petitioning.

The Unanointed must also promise to become Anointed in addition to the cash and goods, in which case they gain a d6! worth of Favor. This Favor can put you over your max but must be spent on a Miracle immediately.

The Divines are extremely litigious. If you don't fulfill your oath in 1 month + 1 for every Honor Category higher than Average Honor you're in, they will seek vengeance.

You may no longer gain any Favor with any Divine that shares a pantheon with the one you're in and what you do have will decay, causing you to lose 1 Favor per day.

Priests of the Divine will also condemn you as an oath breaker, a very serious accusation, causing you to drop 1 Honor Category per month and preventing you from increasing your Honor.

Chapter 5The Divine

What is Weird?

Seems like a reasonable place to start. It is called a lot of things by different cultures: mana, magic, juju, but essentially, it’s the metaphysical power that allows people to reshape reality, which is why working with it is often called weirdshaping, or simply shaping, if you don’t want to sound pretentious.

Weird lies beneath the surface of all things, a hidden power that imbues life with those indescribable feelings. It is the reason you feel attracted to someone at first sight, are speechless when you see a beautiful view, or the reason why planets have healing properties. It is not that some things are inherently ‘magical’ or ‘non-magical’ - all things have weird that is waiting to be shaped.

Weird Traditions

Ways of shaping the Weird are split up into different “Traditions”, which with their own bent on ways the Weird can manifest. These are:

Animism

This Tradition focuses on the manipulation of the body, forcing it to endure beyond its normal limits and making it into a weapon.

Calling

This Tradition focuses on the Realms beyond the Middle one we’re so fond of. It involves the summoning of demons, elementals, and spirits.

Charm

This focuses on the manifestation of the Weird in its raw and primal form. This allows the Weird to interact with our world in a more physical way.

Glamour

This Tradition focuses on the manipulation of the senses, tricking the brain into seeing what the weird-adept wants you to see.

Investiture

This focuses on inanimate objects and either imbuing them with power or manifesting the latent potential that is already in them.

Occultism

This Tradition focuses on the hidden world and opening of the third mind: the divination of knowledge and the unseen.

Sortilege

This Tradition focuses on fate, chance, and probability: more specifically, it focuses on messing with it so that things go in your favor.

Anatomy of a Spell

When you shape the weird, it manifests in the Middle Realm as a spell, which is broken up into different parts or “Rudiments”, which are split into types. More details on the specifics are found under the Acquiring Rudiments section below but here’s a brief overview:

Shape

This is the shape that the spell shows up in. This can be a circle, cone, or wall, for example.

Range

This depends on the Shape of the spell but generally it’s the reach of the spell: how long is the cone or how far can you launch a salvo shaped spell.

Origin

This is where the spell comes into being: this can start as the weird-adept and emanate out, it can imminent from his touch, or it can manifest yards away from him.

Effect

This is where the spell usually gets its name and it’s what it actually does. A spell can cause Stress, remove friction, change appearances, and so on. Effects will also list the Interval, which will talk about next, and the base Range for that spell.

Effects are also special because you can add multiple Effects (or even the same effect multiple times, thus making it more powerful) to the same spell but increasing the Drain in the same amount. If it isn’t clear how an Effect would work if Stacked with itself, there’s a Stacking section for each Effect that should clear things up.

Shaping a Spell

Each of a spell’s Rudiments will have a Drain value, which added together, is how much that spell costs to cast. A spell must have at least 1 of each Rudiment type.

When you first shape a spell (on the second you receive your SP - although, if you don't have the spell Rudiments set, the GM is free to move the count along), you subtract its Drain from the number of SPs you have at that moment and the spell lasts for an amount of time equal to its Interval.

Interval

However, if you have SP left over at the end of that Interval, you can keep the spell up. Again subtracting the Drain from the number of SP you have.

If you have at least 1 SP when the Interval comes up but don't have enough to cover the entire Drain cost, you can continue to keep the spell up for 1 more Interval if you take the amount of SP you're missing in Stress. This is called "Weirdburn". You can only do it once.

If you choose to stop the spell at the Interval, any left over SP are lost.

You cannot stop a spell that has been shaped until it reaches its Interval.

Shape Rudiment Descrptions

Note that all shapes glow slightly with strange luminesce, alerting people where the spell is.

Circle

Drain 2

The Circle is the most basic Shape. It extends from the Origin with a Radius equal to the Base Range of the Effect. Anyone within the Circle is affected by the spell. If it deals damage or inflicts Stress, this occurs on the Interval or if a person enters/re-enters the shape. If not cast far enough away, this could also include the weird-adept.

Chain

Drain 0

This is the most primal Shape a spell can come in. The weird-adept casts the spell and it chooses 1 viable, random target within the Range of the Spell. After half the spell’s Interval, it then leaps to the next. Anyone hit by the spell suffers its Effects for the entire time the spell is cast.

People may be hit multiple times, which simply counts as the spell Stacking on them, but the weird-adept may try and redirect the spell, causing it to skip over a person, but they take Stress equal to the number of times they’ve done so.

Cone

Drain 3

The Cone starts with its tip at the Origin and then extends in a 45-degree arch from it up to the spell’s Range. Anyone within the Cone is affected by the spell. If it deals damage or inflicts Stress, this occurs on the Interval or if a person enters/re-enters the shape.

The Cone can also be moved and shifted, rotating around its Origin, moving a facing every 3 seconds.

Salvo

Drain 5

The Salvo is the spell chucked up to 3 * its base Range from the spell’s Origin. It requires a ranged attack to hit (though ignoring Cover). On a miss, it follows the normal ranged miss rules. Anyone that is hit, suffers the full effects of the spell. If it deals damage or inflicts Stress, this occurs on the Interval.

From where the Salvo's touch location is, that is where the Shape of the spell is placed. Those within the Base Range of the Effect of that Shape (even on a total miss) suffer the effects of the spell for half its Interval.

Sphere

Drain 5

The spell takes on the shape of a 2 ft diameter sphere that flies around, coming into being at the spell’s Origin. While the weird-adept has complete control over the direction with only minimal effort (so it doesn’t affect what else he’s doing), it still has some limitations. The first is that it only goes 10 ft / second, no faster, no slower, and the weird-adept may only turn it 45 degrees every 3 seconds.

Anyone touched by the sphere suffers the Effects of the spell.

Ray

Drain 2

The ray is a thin line of magic that extends from the spell’s Origin up the 10 times the spell’s Range. It manifests in a single instant, requiring a roll to hit, but those that it hits, suffer the full Effect of the spell, though he can only try once per second, and this does eat into the Interval.

If no one was hit and the spell is still being cast, the weird-adept can manifest another ray and try again until he hits or he decasts the spell, though he can only try once per second and this does eat into the Interval.

Wall

Drain 2

The wall is a line that starts at the spell’s Origin and extends up to its Range, but also into the air and below the ground up to 50 ft. It also doesn’t have to be a straight line, weaving, twisting and turning, however, no part of it can get within 5 ft of another part. It must also be anchored to the ground. The wall cannot be moved once placed unless it has an Aura Origin.

Anyone passing through the wall is affected by the Spell. Those that are in the wall’s way when it is cast, get a defense roll vs a ranged attack to get out of the way.

Dice Size Hierarchy

Bonfire plays around a lot with dice sizes, so it’s important to know what dice to grab in those cases.

Dice sizes go as follows:

d2 → d3 → d4 → d6 → d8 → d10 → d12 → d20

If a d4 is exploding and drops to a d3 or lower, it stops exploding.

If the dice size increases past a d20, you’ll add an extra dice starting at a d4. Further dice sizes will increase this smaller dice. This can continue ad nauseum, but no matter how many dice there are, they’re always considered to be rolled on 1 dice.

So, 3d20! + d10! is still considered 1 dice.

Illumination

For sake of simplicity, there’s five categorizations of how much light there is in an area:

Blinding

This is like staring into the sun. It's very painful and not very pleasant.

Assuming that you cover your eyes, you'll be acting as if you're blind. Even then, a moment's exposure even temporarily results in a d10! second delay as you try to recover. If you continue to expose yourself to this level of illumination, you'll be unable to act at all and will do blind in a number of seconds equal to your Con.

Bright

This is the level of light of starting into a fire or just having your eyes near it. It sucks but it probably won't do permanent damage.

This doesn't really affect your aim or anything like that, but it does make it difficult to focus on things, delaying any action by 1d4! seconds as you try and squint and see what you're doing.

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a d12! and take longer.

Normal

This is normal daylight and occurs no operating penalties.

Dim

This the equivalent of a dark room or nighttime. There’s some visibility; the character can make out shapes and shadows but no fine details.

In melee combat, those in dim-lighting defend and attack with a 1d12! and using ranged weapons is even more of challenge. However, if you insist, you’ll be attacking with a 1d6!.

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a d12!.

Additionally, Sprinting is impossible without acquiring a Stress per second moved.

Dark

This is pitch black with the character not even being able to see their hands in front of their faces. This is a dungeon after the torch goes out.

Combat becomes difficult as anyone in darkness defends and attacks with a 1d6! And ranged combat is right out, but, if you insist, you’ll be attacking with a 1d4.

Skills Checks that require sight are also rolled on a d6!.

And moving above Strolling speed is extremely nerve racking, netting a Stress per Speed Category per second. So, moving at a Run for 3 seconds gives you 9 Stress (3 for the Run for 3 seconds).

Your character isn’t immortal, or at least, they didn’t start out that way, and so still needs to fulfil their basic needs of food, drink, and sleep. Without those basic requirements, he may find himself a bit more lethargic or, worse case scenario, dead.

There are three things your character needs to take care of, as mentioned above: Food, Water, and Sleep, and three levels associated with each, Normal, Rationing, and Going Without, which related to how much you have of each.

If a person sleeps the normal amount (assumed to be 8 hours), eats a healthy amount of food, and drinks a good amount of water, they take no damage or Stress.

However, if they cut back on any of three (called rationing) or don't get any sleep, food, or water (called going without), they take the associate damage and/or Stress as noted by the table.

This is on 1 Wound per need but does stack: having no food and water gives you 2d6! damage and d6! Stress.

Additionally, a Wound doesn't heal until the associated need is returned to normal.

Stat Score Damage

Stat Score damage occurs in several places and it’s fairly straightforward. Then why have this section, you ask? As a quick aside to keep the game moving.

The sudden change in ability is incredibly unnerving, meaning that until the person can stop, take a breath and re-adjust to their new reality (meaning the player can adjust the stats on his character sheet without breaking off Combat, a Confrontation, a Challenge or any narrative tension the GM has built up), he gets a penalty of 1 for each point lost to anything that Stat Score influences.

So, a -3 to Dexterity would net a temporary -3 to Attack, and Defense, +3 to Initiative, all while reducing the Confrontation Die by 3 sizes, if applicable.

If the amount drained is more than the score, the person becomes appropriately incapacitated, but not dead, until the Stat Score damage is healed.
