Welcome to Bonfire Character Creation! You'll notice that there's a lot of steps and, if you're familiar with other TTRPGs, that it's a bit longer and a bit more complicated than other games. Why is that?

The first is that it gives you, the player, more choice. Do you want to build a cunning fighter than actually using their brains instead of their brawns to defeat others? Do you want to play a sly diplomat that crushes her opponents' morale without even lifting a finger? Or perhaps you want to play a power wizard that concocts his spells on the fly, building the perfect one for each scenario?

More importantly, do you want those different characters to actually feel different when you play them? So that convincing some actually feels different than just beating them to death?

This is the first thing that Bonfire offers you.

The second thing is that it allows you to be surprised during creation: through your choices and rolls, you'll get a more fully formed character than in other systems. You'll get what you want with extra twists that help add interest and depth to your character that you wouldn't get in other systems.

And not only that, but you'll also have those choices, twists, and depth mean something in play. Is your character brash? You'll be rewarded for playing that way in the game.

So, what are you waiting for? Let's get started!

Step 1: Roll Stats

Your character has 6 attributes that define your character physically and mentally. These are Strength (Str), Dexterity (Dex), Constitution (Con), Intellect (Int), Willpower (Will), and Pre (Presence).

Roll 3d6 for each of these Abilities to generate that score.

Strength

Strength (Str) represents your character’s pure brawn. How much can she lift and carry? How strong are her punches?

It governs the base damage you do, how much you can carry, and strength-based skills.

Dexterity

Dexterity (Dex) is a measure of how agile your character is. Can she dodge arrows, or can she execute a graceful pirouette? Dexterity is crucial for avoiding blows in combat or traps in a hallway.

It covers how accurate you are, how well you can dodge, how quickly you get up in moving in situations, and dexterity-based skills.

Constitution

Constitution (Con) represents your character’s overall health and toughness. Can he shrug off the Dancing Death or does he succumb to a head cold? Can she take a solid blow in combat and not even blink? These are the things Constitution oversees.

It controls your base Vitality, the amount of minimum Vitality at every other level up, and how much damage you can suck down without going comatose, and constitution-based skills.

Intellect

Intellect (Int) measures your character’s raw knowledge: how much data can he remember? Can she do mathematical calculations or remember who the Elector of Stanitz is? Intellect doesn’t help in applying that knowledge, but it does give you knowledge to draw on.

It governs how accurate you are, how many CrP you get on level up, and intellect-based skills (which are a lot of them).

Willpower

Willpower (Will) is applied knowledge. Does she process information quicker and learn from other’s mistakes? Does he understand life is more than just a set of numbers of probabilities? Can she see other’s viewpoints? How well can she overcome adversity or keep her ground?

It controls your starting Stress Threshold, how likely you are to get hit, how quickly you get up and moving in situations, and willpower-based skills.

Presence

Presence (Pre) represents force of personality. Does he bend others to his will? Is she the focus of any room she enters? Do people look up to them or what to be him? Presence is not looks or even likeability: it represents this animal magnetism that people follow.

Presence covers how much bonus Favor you get when worshipping the Divines, your starting Honor, how strong your Traits are, how many contacts you start the game with, how many followers you can have, and presence-based skills.

A Note About Low Stats

Bonfire is specifically balanced for you to roll 3d6 in order, with 10 being the average result but don't worry about feeling like you have low scores or they're not in the order you want: you'll have a chance to rearrange and modify them later.

But even if you have objectively lower stats than the rest of the company, you'll still be able to contribute in a meaningful way in your own area of expertise because Bonfire is built as a team game, so nobody is going to be good at everything.

In Bonfire, you'll almost always be weaker than your opponent: what makes a hero is not supernatural abilities, but the simple fact that he fights longer and harder for what he believes in than other men.

Helpful Tips for New Players

Bonfire is a little different than most RPGs so it's important to keep things in mind:

1) You will never be good at everything: you'll have to rely on teammates.

2) Utilizing different skill sets to help each other is the best strategy to overcome any obstacle. If something seems hard or tedious, it’s because you need to adapt and use a new strategy to overcome it.

Each additional skill set you can incorporate into any challenge increases your chance of surviving exponentially.

3) Don't try to read and understand every rule. Yes, read through the rules generally, but start with a specific character concept and focus on how your character interacts with the world.

Think outside the box: Bonfire is built to reward that kind of thinking.

4) You are going to die. Don't get married to your first few characters.

The pace of the game is intended to be slower and more thoughtful: the more you slowdown and think, the more likely you are to succeed (whatever that means to you) but remember to keep moving forward.

5) Monster body parts are worth a lot.

Step 2: Receive CrP

Alright, you have your scores. Now it’s time to determine how many Creation Points (or CrP, for short) you’ll start with. These CrP will be spent on modifying your character, buying them their skills and increasing their Scores. They may also be spent on rerolls at certain steps.

Even still, you may want to switch your stats around. This may be because you have almost the right stats, but you have a specific character concept in mind. For instance, using the example stats in Step 1, your party may need a diplomatic character more than a strong character. In this case, it would really help to swap Strength and Presence.

You can freely switch around your stats as need but, in that case, you only receive 25 CrPs.

If you switch only 4 of your stats, you receive 50 CrPs.

If you switch 2, you receive 75 CrPs.

And if you keep your stats the way they are, with no changes, you gain 100 CrPs to work with.

Fractional CrP

During Character Creation, you'll have times when things give you discounts, sometimes in percentages. In these cases, track the fractional CrPs.

Step 4: Select Archetypes

Your character has an area of expertise represented by two Archetypes.

You choose a Primary and a Secondary Archetype and pay the price in CrP for both based on your race’s Archetype Cost section. Descriptions of the different Archetypes are found in Chapter 3:Archetypes along with more details on how to combine them.

If you’ve chosen to swap around all your stats and have no CrPs to work with, congratulations, you’re now in CrP debt and need to make it up by the end of Character Creation.

You must select two Archetypes before going further.

Starting Stress Threshold

Your starting Threshold is your race's Stress Threshold Modifier + your Will score + a roll of your Primary Archetype Category's Stress Dice.

Experts receive +d8, Socialites, Servants, & Weird-Adepts a +d6, and Fighters a +d4.

Divide that by 4: that’s the amount of the Stress in each Category.

For fractions on Stress Categories, round up on Unsure but down on the rest.

So, if you’re a Human Socalites (d6) and you roll a 2 with a Will of 8, you have a 25 Stress Threshold. Your Unsure is 1 - 6, Bloodied between 7 - 12, Wounded between 13 - 18, and Critical between 19 - 25.

Record your Stress Threshold on your character sheet.

Example

Being an elf gives me a 20 Stress Threshold Modifier and being an Expert gives me a +d8, with which I rolled a 3, giving me a total of 36 for my Stress Threshold ( 20 + 3 + 13 (Will) ).

Not too shabby.

The way to quickly calculate Stress Categories is to divide in half and then divide in half again. Unsure is 1 to halved half, Nervous is halved half + 1 to half, Shaken is half + 1 to half + half halved, Breaking is half + half halved + 1 to your full Stress Threshold.

Confusing enough?

So, for a 36 Stress Threshold, half is 18 and half of 18 is 9.

Unsure is 1 to 9.

Nervous is 10 (9 + 1) to 18.

Shaken is 19 (18 + 1) to 27 (18 + 9).

Breaking is 28 (18 + 9 + 1) to 36.

Starting Vitality

To calculate your base Vitality, add a roll of your Primary Archetype’s Vitality Die to your Constitution + their Vitality Size Modifier.

Divide that by 4: that’s the amount of the Vitality in each Category.

Your Trauma Threshold is twice that (rounded up). Trauma is talked about more in Chapter 3:Combat.

For fractions on Wound Categories, round up on Hurt but down on the rest.

So, if you’re a Human with and your Vitality Die is a d10 and you roll a 7 with a Con of 12, you have 34 Vitality. Your Hurt is 1 - 9, Bloodied between 10 - 17, Wounded between 18 - 25, and Critical between 26 - 34.

Record your Vitality Die and your roll on your character sheet.

Example

Since I have Thief for my Primary Archetype, my Vitality Die is a d4, which replaces the Expert’s d8 Vitality and I roll a 1.

The way to quickly calculate Wound Categories is the same as calulating Stress Categories but swapping the names.

So, for 14 Vitality, half is 7and half of 7 is 3.5.

Hurt is 1 to 4 (because we round up).

Bloodied is 5 (4 + 1) to 7.

Wounded is 8 (7 + 1) to 10 (7 + 3 (rounded down this time)).

Critical is 11 (7 + 3 + 1) to 14.

Levels of Induction & Max Favor

There are 3 levels of commitment to a cult, that determines how often you get Favor and how much Favor you can have:

Unanointed

These are the lowest level: those outside the cult and its mysteries. They gain no benefits of any kind.

Anointed

These are those that are the lay members of the faith. They gain small bonuses to their max Favor for the deity they’re devoted to. You can only be anointed to one Covenant at a time.

If you have a positive Devotion to a deity, one of their cults, or one of their Covenants, you can choose to begin the game as an anointed to that deity. Otherwise, you can become Anointed during gameplay once you have that Devotion.

You can only be anointed to one deity at a time.

Servants

These are the highest rung of the commitment ladder and serve as the officers of the cult of the god. They’re priests, abbots, hierophants, pontiffs, nazarites, hermits, etc. These are men and women that have taken vows to devote their life to the cult.

In game terms, this means that these are those who have a Servant Archetype as either their Primary or Secondary Archetype.

Step 8: Skills

Skills are additional areas of expertise that your character has obtained above his or her race and class.

Both cost CrP and are found in Chapter 5:Skills but, before you rush over there, you need to determine the starting Ranks for your Skill Suites.

Keep in mind, once you do go rushing over to buy Skills, that you don't add any of your free purchases from your Archetype at this step.

Calculating Starting Ranks

There are 7 non-Combat Skill Suites:

  • Athletics
  • Lore
  • Streetwise
  • Survival
  • Strategy
  • Trades
  • Weirdcraft

There are 5 Combat Skill Suites:

  • Armor
  • Melee Weapons
  • Ranged Weapons
  • Shields
  • Unarmed

To determine your starting Rank in each, follow the guide below. Note that, if you get Ranks in a Skill Suite here, you automatically gain Training in that Suite.

  • Your Primary Generalist's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 3.
    • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 3, the other starts at Rank 2, otherwise pick one to start at 3.
    • When you Specialize, you gain Rank 2 in its Main Skill Suite
      • If you get two (noted with an "and", not "or"), one starts at Rank 2, the other starts at Rank 1, otherwise pick one to start at 2.
      • If your Primary and Secondary Archetype both reference your Secondary Archetype's Main Skill Suite, use your Primary's Generalist Archetype' Main Skill Suite.
  • Your Secondary Generalist's Main Skill Suite starts at Rank 2.
    • If you would normally get two, you only get one of them.
    • When you Specialize, you gain Rank 1 in its Main Skill Suite.
      • If you would normally get two, you only get one of them.
  • Pick 1 Skill Suite to start at Rank 2.
  • If, for any reason, a Skill Suite doubles up, you only get a +1 to its Rank.
    • This is true each time this happens (so tripling up gives you an additional +1; quadrupling up gives another +1, etc.)
  • Finally, select 4 Skill Suites: these start at Rank 0.
    • If you're a Fighter, 3 of these Skill Suites need to be Combat Skill Suites and correspond to your 3 Martial Focuses.
    • You can, instead, choose Skill Suites you already have Ranks in, but it only increases its Rank by 1.
    • All other Skill Suites start as Untrained (marked with a U).

Note that you get the Advanced Language Skill for free in your native language with its Rank starting at your Int.

Step 9: Flesh Out Personality

Your character also has a personality (shocker) that sets them apart.

What do they want? What do they believe? Who do they like? What do people think of them?

These questions are answered using Goals, Traits, Racial Temperament, Devotions, and Reputation, which we’ll fill out now.

Goals

Goals serve two purposes.

First, they help your GM figure out what you want to do with the character: where you want to take them and what they’re trying to accomplish. At this stage, if you haven't already, you should bring your GM to talk about the shape of the campaign and what Goals will work the best.

Second, they help you get CrPs that you can then turn around and use to make your character stronger.

A character can have 3 Goals at any one time, although it’s fine if you only start with 1.

You should discuss with your GM what Goals are going to work for the campaign to get on the same page. The GM has the final say on what Goals are appropriate to take.

Have a Goal that you can continue to work for but also have a Goal(s) that are actionable as you get bonus CrP for completing them. They should take more than 1 session to complete, at the very least.

Example

This would be the time to talk to the GM about what time of game they’re running so the character’s Goal can align with that idea and the other players’.

Traits

Traits are a lot of things.

They can be things that stick out about your character. Things that people tend to notice about them or that the character defines his identity by. These can be something specific (“I have blonde hair”) or something general (“I have an intimidating demeanor”) or somewhere in between (“I’m an orc”).

They don’t have to be physical in nature. It could be how your character views himself (“I’m confident” or “I’m good at math”). They can also be something about your character that he just can’t explain (“I’ve always been good with dogs” or “I can read people easily”).

Conflicting Traits

Because human beings are wonderful, chaotic being, they're capable of believing two entirely opposing things at the same time and this is represented in Bonfire by having conflicting Traits.

I would actually recommend you start with a conflicting Trait (or Devotion). Chapter 1Step-by-Step Overview

Which Race Should I Choose?

Your race effects character creation in 3 ways: Stat Score Modifiers, Gameplay Rewards, and Archetype Costs. If you're new to RPGs in generally, go for a race that interests you based on the flavor.

Stat Score Modifiers

These modify the base stats that you roll. You can use it to balance out your character's weak spots or you can use it to maximize what your character is already good it. We recommend the latter as Bonfire is a game built around teamwork: you'll have friends who will be good at things your character isn't.

Note that these adjustments can't bring score above 20 or below 1.

Gameplay Rewards

Each race is meant to encourage a certain type of play. For instance, orcs gain bonuses for starting things, like combats or Confrontations, so they reward players who are aggressive, while elves get rewarded for being careful with how they engage with others. These are noted under the Racial Temperament section in their summary.

Archetype Costs

Some races can spend less time mastering things than others, freeing up time (CrP) to focus on other pursuits or becoming true masters of their vocation. This is reflected in the Archetype Costs.

Introduction

Archetypes represent your character’s background training and starting skills and there are two types of Archetypes: Generalist and Specialist.

Generalist Archetypes

These Archetypes represent the overall theme.

For instance, Fighter Archetypes tend to be good at things related to combat. So, if you’re looking to make a combat focused character, you can look there. They are Expert, Weird-Adept, Servant, Socialite, and Fighter. You must pick two starting out.

Generalists will have a wide variety of discounts within their theme that are good for just getting straight bonuses.

Purchasing Archetypes

First choose a Generalist Primary and a Generalist Secondary Archetype and pay the price in CrP for both based on your race.

You use the Vitality Die, and the Primary Drawback sections from the Primary, and combine both the free Advanced Skills (listed under the Main Skill Suite) and the Archetype Abilities sections, though you completely remove 1 ability marked with * from your Secondary Archetype, unless you Double Down.

All Generalists, except for the Servant, can be selected as both your Primary and Secondary Archetype, called Double Down. So, you can be a Fighter/Fighter but not a Servant/Servant.

The benefits for double downing will be noted at the end of the Archetype but they only remain while both Archetypes are not Specialized, but note that these bonuses are lieu of receiving your Archetype Abilities twice.

If two characters have the same ability, unless it says otherwise, those abilities stack.

Specialist Archetypes

These represent a more focused version of the Generalist Archetypes.

They’re more expensive but come with additional powers. When you purchase a Specialist Archetype, it replaces the Generalist Archetype of the same type except with GM permission. So, for instance, if you’re an Expert, you can replace it with the Ranger or the Thief.

Purchasing Specialist Archetypes

At 1st level and at every level up, you can purchase a Specialist Archetype which replaces your Generalist Archetypes. If at 1st level, see the Specialist Archetypes at 1st Level sidebar below.

You can replace one or both at each level, in either order, barring double-down Weird-Adepts who have to specialize both at the same time into the same Specialist Archetype.

If you do choose to specialize just one of your Generalist Archetypes when you're Double-Downed, you lose the Double-Down benefits.

You can also choose to replace neither if it doesn’t fit your character because once you purchase a Specialist Archetype, you are stuck with it.

Specialist Archetypes completely replaces everything from the Generalist Archetype they’re replacing though it doesn’t retroactively remove things you’ve already rolled for or free purchases.

So, a Fighter, with a d10 Vitality Die decides, at level 4 to become a Soldier with a Vitality Die of a d8. He doesn’t then reroll all his Vitality Die - from that point forward, he simply uses the d8 instead.

They also gain a free purchase of that Specialist's Main Skill Suite as noted in Step 8 of Chapter 1:Step-by-Step Overview.

You’ll pay an additional 20 CrP for each one you’re replacing regardless of race.

Your GM may choose to reject an Archetype combination based on the fact that the two Archetypes don’t fit together thematically. So, while a Thief/Priest (Beast Covenant) might be really powerful because of the Beast Covenant’s Shape Changing ability, if there’s not a Patron of Thieves and Animals in the campaign world, you’re out of luck.

Remember that if you’re replacing your Secondary Archetype, you’ll still need to select a leveling ability from your new Archetype to remove.

Specialist Archetypes at 1st Level

If you’re more familiar with the game, your playstyle, and which Archetypes suit you, you might consider purchasing your Specialization(s) at 1st level. That’s perfectly fine, but you’ll still need to pay the CrP for both the Generalist and the Specialist.

You’re considered to have purchased your Generalist Archetype first AND THEN purchased your Specialist, which means that you still get any free stuff from abilities that say "at first level" from your Generalist, so Experts get some free skills and Weird-Adepts get their starting Rudiments.

Your Primary Specialist’s Vitality Die and Primary Drawback also replaces your Primary Generalist’s.

Anatomy of the Divine

Covenants

Divines have spheres of power that represent their aspects. These are codified into Covenants, or promises they make with their servants in exchange for a portion of the Divine’s power.

Cults of that Divine typically pick one, maybe two Covenants, of their chosen Divine to focus on.

For instance, Wülder, a Gothic god, watches over Raiding, Industry and Trade. There could be 3 different priests to Wülder, one focusing on his role as the Bridge Between Men, one seeing him as primarily a watcher over people going on cattle raids, and one worshiping him as the greater crafter and source of inspiration.

While they all worship the same Divine, the Miracles they can invoke in his name are different.

This means that priests of different cults may not get along with each other and there can even be intra-faith wars to purge heretics (those that focus on different Covenant from them).

The main bonus of Covenants is that they make Miracles within them easier to invoke. The specifics are elaborated on in the Invoking Miracles in Chapter 5:The Divine.

Cosmic Links

The Higher Planes are those that exist above the crude and primitive world and so, naturally, the Divines are linked to the events and behaviors of the celestial bodies that make their homes in the heavens.

A Cosmic Link is the specific celestial body that the Divine is associated with, and it usually gives an insight to the personality of the god. The Cosmic Link doesn’t necessarily mean your Patron is primarily the Divine of that Cosmic thing. So, a Divine associated with the moon might not be primarily a moon god, in the same way that while Venus was associated with the Morning Star, she was primarily viewed as a love goddess by the Romans. Still, your holy symbols may incorporate the moon motif commonly to represent her.

For clarification’s sake: when something says “decrease” in this section’s explanations, you go down the chart by that many steps. If it says “increase” go up, so increase will always be a bonus, and decrease will always be bad.

Comet

This is associated with fervent but fleeting powers. Whenever you invoke a Miracle, you gain two Honor Dice with a +3 but lose an additional Favor.

Evening Star

The evening star is a source of strength and beauty: it's the first star to rise in the evening. When you invoke a Miracle, if you roll below the average, you gain the average instead. So, if you roll d8 and get a 3 or less, you get 4 instead.

Moon

The moon is a steady force, its effects are subtle but constant. When you invoke a Miracle, if you roll two Honor Dice, keeping the highest.

Morning Star

The morning star is the last star in the heavens before day and is a lone watcher. When you invoke a Miracle and roll the minimum amount, you don’t lose any Favor.

Sun

The sun is the lord of heaven, strong and unbending, its power unquestioned. When you invoke a Miracle and roll the max amount, you don’t lose any Favor.

Cyclical

Often hailing from older, more primitive times, the Cyclical deity represents earthly inevitabilities in a dichotomy, sometimes with themselves and sometimes with an opposing deity. This can be Spring and Summer, life and death, creation and destruction. They may be weak now, but they will wax unstoppable. When you invoke a Miracle, and roll under its average, your next Miracle gains a +1. This is cumulative.

If you roll above its average, this bonus is then removed, and you get a -1 to your next Miracle. Again, this is cumulative and resets when you roll below.

So, if you have a d6! Honor Dice, and you roll a 2, the next Miracle you invoke gets a +1. That next Miracle you roll, you get a 3 for a total of 4 (because of the +1), giving you a +2 the next roll. On that roll, you roll a 5 for a total of 7 ( 5 + 2 ). The next Miracle you then invoke gets a -1

Skill Suites Vs Advanced Skills

You’ll notice that there are two types of Skills in Bonfire: Skill Suites and Advanced Skills.

So, what’s the difference?

Skill Suites

These skills represent broad swathes of knowledge that cover a variety of subjects. Suites are hard to increase because there’s so much ground to cover but can be used if you don’t have the specific Advanced Skill.

An example would of this would be using the Survival Suite if you didn’t have the Signaling Advanced Skill.

Advanced Skills

These skills are more tightly focused, covering one intellectual discipline. They’re easier to master but may not be applicable in every case.

An example of this would be First Aid, Fire Building, and Scavenging are all Skills in the Survival Skill Suite, while Law, History, and Medicine are all Skills in the Lore Skill Suite.

Acquiring Skills

Throughout your character’s career, she’ll acquire and improve her skills. Both types of skills are purchased with CrP, that is spent either at Character Creation or when leveling up.

All skills have a unique cost that represents how easy or difficult they are to master. You pay the price as noted in the section below, you improve/purchase the skill.

Rank

If you haven't already calculated your starting Rank for your Skill Suites, you need to go back to Step 8 of Chapter 1: Step-by-Step Overview and do that before purchasing any additional Ranks in your Suites or Advanced Skills.

If you haven’t purchased an Advanced Skill before, its current Rank is equal to the Rank of the mother Skill Suite and increased by 1 from there.

When you buy a Skill Suite that you're already trained in, if you've already bought Ranks in its children Advanced Skill, you increase those Ranks by 1 as well. This includes Skills you can buy multiple times with different subsets, like the Craft Advanced Skill.

If there is more than more than 1 Stat listed, use the highest.

Combat Check Mods

Combat Skills behave identically to normal Skills except that their Check Mods are calculated a bit differently.

Instead of taking the highest stat, you’re going to refer to the following formulas and table below.

An (A) or (S) means that these abilities will be affected by your Armor and/or Shield if you're wearing them.

Attack (Atk): Dex Mod + Int Mod

Defense (Def): Dex Mod + Will Mod + A + S

Initiative (Init): Dex Mod + Will Mod + A

Damage (Dam): Str Mod

You'll also have Parry and Recovery, which are both based on the weapon you used but Parry may be replaced by your shield's Parry while your Recovery will be affected by your Armor.

Climbing

Base Cost 7

Stats Str

This Skill covers climbing and repelling up and down walls of various heights and textures.

Difficulty should be based on height, with about every 40 ft increasing the Difficulty by 1 dice size.

Textures should also play a major factor in not just how tough the Check is but also how many Checks need to be made for a given wall.

So, if there’s a wall, where the base is stone at a 45% slant for 20 ft, then it moves to close knit stones for another 40 ft, and then finally to a wood overhang for just 5 ft, and then to a wooden wall for 10 ft, there would be 4 Climbing Checks in this order: +d4!, +d10! (d8! for base but because it’s 40 ft, it gets bumped up 1), d10!, and +0.

Base Cost 30

Stats Int

This suite covers general knowledge, which is mostly academic in nature. Most of it is pretty heavy stuff but the basics are second nature or stuff that’s been picked up here and there.

Administration

Base Cost 7

Stats Int

While they’ll never inspire the awe or respect that other leaders do, people who oversee the day to day functions of an organization keep the lights on so that others can make the speeches.

Administrators can make a Check at +0 to gain a clue about an item the party might need on their adventure, or one to retroactively remember that they did buy an item back at the market for just such an occasion.

Each of these Checks they make increases the Difficulty of the next one by 1 step.

History

Base Cost 6

Stats Int

Everyone knows certain large events, but few can tie those events into an overarching narrative and understand their effects on later events.

The History Skill allows a character to not just recall events but also to link them to the present day, to certain traditions and rituals of a culture, or the behavior of certain members of a culture, giving insight on how to proceed.

Language (specific)

Base Cost 5

Stats Int/Will

A character receives this Skill for free in their native tongue with a Rank equal to their Int and it allows them to speak and be understood by others with Skill in that language. Like all Languages, this isn't increased with the Lore Skill.

Purchasing other languages can be done, however, if it a language completely unrelated to a language you already known, the Language's Rank starts at 0: otherwise use of that that Language's Rank as a starting Rank.

Checks are only made when timing is critical, such as communication in a battle, or when understanding is key, such as when debating foreign policy.

Additionally, when two speakers are in a Confrontation, the one with the higher Language Rank gains a +d4! to their roll (see Applicable Skills in Chapter 2: Confrontations). In case of tie, nothing happens.

The Lore Skill Suite can’t be used in place of a Language Check for tongues you don’t know unless you have the Language Skill in a related Language. Using a real-world example, someone with the Language (Swedish) Advanced Skill couldn’t use the Lore Skill Suite to understand a person with Language (French) but could use it when talking to someone with Language (Norwegian) or Language (Danish).

Law (culture)

Base Cost 5

Stats Int

This Skill covers the intricacies of the legal system, including landmark rulings and common punishments for common crimes. A character can make their case or find loopholes to exploit the legal system.

A character’s Lore Skill can only be used to substitute for their native culture but not others’. If the character does want to branch out, his mastery of Law for that new culture starts at 0, as he must learn everything from the ground up. Diving more into his own culture’s laws (buying this Advanced Skill for his culture) uses the Lore Skill as base, as per usual.

Literacy (alphabet)

Base Cost 7

Stats Int

This Skill allows a character to read and write in a language’s alphabet, giving them the power to pen books and poems, or passive aggressive notes. A character who's Trained in the Lore Skill Suite is considered to be literate in their native language's alphabet.

This Skill can be taken multiple times, each instance representing mastery of a new writing system. The scribe can then use that alphabet to pen any language they know, in the same way that we could use the Latin Alphabet to pen Arabic or the Korean Alphabet to write English.

To decipher a text, the person needs to know both the alphabet it's written in and the language it's using, which most of the time won't matter but can be used as a cheap way to do a cypher in a pinch.

Checks are only made when timing is critical, such as quickly scanning a letter, or when understanding is key, such as reading an extremely techinical work, or where basic competency is unsure, such as reading an early version of the language.

Mathematics

Base Cost 9

Stats Int/Will

While this may seem like just a Skill that allows you to be able to juggle figures and add totals and calculate the area of a circle like a huge nerd, mathematics has a more practical use: correctly guessing distances.

While you can try to eyeball how far away something is, a Mathematics Check vs +0 allows you to do rough calculations, giving you the ability to know exactly what penalty you’re going to get for using a ranged weapon or who's going to be affected by the Shape of a spell you’re going to cast.

Medicine

Base Cost 8

Stats Int

With proper treatment and nursing, a wound that would lay you out for weeks, may only keep you down for a couple of days. This Skill involves the knowledge of long-term care, when bandages should be changed, which poultices will have the greatest effect, and what minor surgeries need to be performed.

A character with this Skill can make a Check for a wounded person, adding the Severity of his largest Wound to the opposing roll.

So, if a person has a 5, 3, and 8 Severity Wound, he can make a Medicine Check vs d20+8.

Success means that the person can take 1 day off the healing time for every Wound every time it drops a Severity.

So, a success against a 5 Severity Wound means that it was treated properly and takes 10 days to completely heal ( 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 0 ) instead of the normal 15.

You may also increase the Difficulty modifier by half again as much - if you succeed, you take 2 days off per Severity. So, if you're against a d20!+9, you can make it a d20!+14 ( 9 + ( 9 / 2 ) ) for that bonus.

Additionally, when resisting Poison, Toxin, or Diseases, you can add your Medicine Rank, rounded up, to each round of the Confrontation.

Monster Craft

Base Cost 8

Stats Int

This Advanced Skill is under both the Lore and Survival Skill Suite but still counts as 1 Skill, so you don’t have to buy it twice - they share their Rank. It simply represents the different ways that each Skill Suite approaches the craft.

When you first purchase this Skill, you need to choose which Skill Suite to base if off of: only purchases of that Skill Suite increase it. You cannot switch it.

Monster Craft gives you insight into the creatures that roam the Middle Realm. Simply put, each Boon gives you a (truthful) answer to a question.

Difficulty is based on how common the monster is. Common is +0, uncommon +d6!, rare is d12!, and legendary is +d20!.

Base Cost 11

Stats Dex/Will

Mostly commonly used for creating fake currency, forgery is a Skill that allows a character to reproduce something effectively and fool others. This Skill acquaints the character with common forging techniques and knowledge with what possible agents will be looking for.

The forger must also have the appropriate corresponding Craft or Trade Skill, so he can’t forge coins if he doesn’t have the Metalworking Skill, though that’s within reason: a character forging an official passport with one at hand doesn’t need to have Literacy as he can just copy the letters, though he may also need to have Artistry (wood cutting) to create the heraldry on it, but someone trying to forge an official declaration from scratch does need to have the Literacy Skill in that language plus Artistry (wood cutting).

Intuition

Base Cost 5

Stats Will

The ability to understand people and get where they're coming from can help you figure out what will and won't work with them better.

It has 3 uses: to get a feel on how likely a Confrontation is, to confirm an NPC's Characteristic, and to discover an NPC's Characteristics.

To get a feel about the likelihood of a Confrontation, a Check is required with the Difficulty based on how well the person knows their target.

Boons can be used to determine what additional things might be dealbreakers or insights into why this tactic might work while Complications can result in a misread of the situation.

They can also use it to confirm their theories about what Characteristics a character has. The player needs to make a reasonable guess about the Characteristic first - so players who’re simply throwing out guesses automatically fail.

The Check is based on how much the person and the target have interacted with each other but typically start at Impossible and are lowered by 2 for each previous interaction, although the GM can further lower the Difficulty if the interaction was particularly meaningful or telling.

Finally, the GM will subtract double that Characteristic's Rank from their roll so that more powerful it is, the easier it'll be to confirm, although they may add their Deception Rank for free as well.

Boons can be spent for additional insight into that Characteristic (like learning it's general Rank) while Complications might result in the Characteristic being slightly off or leaving out a key detail.

Players don't need to exactly correct in their guesses, as long as they get the spirit. If they succeed, it's a good opportunity to just tell them what the Characteristic is directly.

A character can also use their Intuition Skill to 'read' someone: learn a Characteristic without making a guess at it.

The Check is based on how much the person and the target have interacted with each other but typically start at Impossible and are lowered by 1 for each previous interaction, although the GM can further lower the Difficulty if the interaction was particularly meaningful or telling.

Finally, the GM will subtract that Characteristic's Rank from their roll so that more powerful it is, the easier it'll be to guess, although they may add their Deception Rank for free as well.

Boons can be spent for additional insight into that Characteristic (like learning it's general Rank) while Complications might result in the Characteristic being slightly off or leaving out a key detail or being completely wrong.

First Aid

Base Cost 8

Stats Dex/Int

This gives the character a crude understanding of battlefield medicine: making splints and staunching wounds.

A character with this Skill can make a Check for a person wounded in that last 30 minutes, adding the Severity of any Wounds that he's treating to the opposing roll.

So, if a person has a 5, 3, and 8 Severity Wound, he can make a First Aid Check vs d20+16 or he can choose not to treat the 8 Severity Wound and make a vs d20!+8. Success means that the person can take 1 level of Severity off those Wounds. So, a 5 Severity Wound drops immediately to a 4 Severity Wound. You cannot go back and do a Check for a Wound that you skipped.

You have 30 minutes to perform First Aid Checks for a person, with each Wound taking 3 minutes, if applicable.

You may also increase the Difficulty modifier by half again as much - if you succeed, you hit 2 points of damage. So, if you're against a d20!+9, you can make it a d20!+14 ( 9 + ( 9 / 2 ) ) to heal 2 points of damage.

Handle Animal (specific)

Base Cost 6

Stats Pre

This Skill deals with the handling and care of a specific animal, from birth to the slaughterhouse. It also deals with training and common illnesses to make sure the stock is healthy and virile.

While most Checks are handled like normal knowledge Checks or on a case-by-case basis (such as calming down your animal during a storm), using a question-and-answer style, this Skill is also used for training, with the base number of tricks you can teach an animal equal to their Int (note that carrying things and handling a bridle are two different tricks).

The animal needs to have both the basic temperament and physical ability to learn a trick (no sheep becoming pack animals and no dogs learning to pick locks). The GM may increase or decrease this for particularly smart/dumb and obedient/obstinate animals.

After a month of training, a roll is made vs +0. If successfully, the animal learned the trick. If not, another Check can be made in a month, although every failure increases the Difficulty to learn that trick by 1 dice size.

The base price of this Skill is calculated towards domesticated animals. If the player wants to learn how to deal with wild animals, this Skill costs thrice as much to purchase.

Hunting

Base Cost 7

Stats Will

Most of the time, you’re not going to down your query in one go, and so the hunt is on as your watch for subtle signs of their passing. While this Skill was originally used to hunt wildlife, it has been repurposed by wardens and bounty hunters trying to capture the most dangerous game.

The Hunting Skill can be used to track your prey, get a sense of their movements, and predict where they’re going. It can also give insight into common and uncommon natural creatures.

If you’re using it for food, hunting is a boom or bust strategy: at the end of each full day you spend hunting, roll a Check.

For each Boon you gain 2 days’ worth of food, but either way, you gain 1 damage from exhaustion and possibly more if you suffer a Complication.

Hunting is also used to harvest Reagents from dead animals and monsters which can be sold or used to increase your spell power.

Combat isn’t just about raw physical power, but more about how it’s directed and used. This Skill Suite deals with logistics and making the most out of what you have.

Combat Style (style)

Base Cost 9

Stats Con/Int

By studying the best tactics and how to fight in certain circumstances, a warrior can take advantage of his surroundings and combat styles emerge. Combat Styles can be anything but involved in parts: a situation and a metric.

This situation is when the Combat Style is most effective. For instance, it can be “in the forest”, “fighting on rooftops”, “being outnumbered”, “being in a duel”, “fighting while prone”, etc. Really anything (as long as your GM approves).

The second part of the Combat Style is the metric applied to. This can be anything from the following list: Fatigue, Initiative, Recovery, Attack, Damage, and Parry.

Both the situation and metric are selected at the time of purchase and cannot be changed.

If the situation is applicable, the character receives a +1 to that metric for every 2 Ranks of that Skill he’s obtained.

So, a character with the Combat Skill (forest fighting, attack) at 5 who gets into a fight in a forest gets a +2 to attack.

Having a high Rank in Strategy doesn't automatically give you Combat Style bonuses under all circumstances and buying this Skill for the first time starts it off at Rank 0, instead of the Tactic Suite's Rank.

Wearing Items

Normally what is written under the Carry column for each item is its Size. The only exception is clothes, armor, and personal containers. If worn on your person, not carried or stuffed in a backpack, these become less encumbering since their weight is distributed across basically the entire body.

In this case, shrink them 1 size for Carry purposes. Small items become negligible. Wearing boots, a Small item, then makes them effectively weightless, while any Heavy Armor makes them only M.

Personal Containers

Personal Containers, such as backpacks or sacks, go one step further. Since it is assumed that your Carry weight is how much you can hold effectively, it does take into account that some things are too awkward to effectively carry despite being fairly light.

These containers increase your Carry by the noted amount.

The exceptions, sort of, to this are coin purses, which make coins negligible (otherwise they’re 10 = 1S), quivers which do the same thing but with arrows, and scabbards (for Large and Medium weapons) and sheaths (for Small weapons) allow you to wear weapons - shrinking them 1 size.

Going Over Carry

You can go over your Carry, of course, but it will slow you down. Going over your Carry prevents you from Sprinting.

Beyond that, each M of weight over your limit removes the next quickest Movement Category (so, removing Running, Jogging, Walking, and then Crawling) until your character is unable to move.

Unless proven otherwise, the GM should assume the characters are carrying their max Carry if they pick up something new while out adventuring.

Drag

Instead of trying to carry something, a character can always drag it behind them, which is much easier.

In this case, a person can drag 3 times what they can Carry, but this is a straight multiplication.

So, a person who can carry 1L can drag 3L; a person who can carry 2M can drag 6M.

However, regardless of how much they're dragging, they cannot move above a Walk and going over their drag limit once, dropped them to a max of Stroll. Going over twice prevents them from moving at all.

Currency

The denomination of coinage is fairly simple and is done in divisions of 10.

10 cc = 1 silver coin (sc)

10 sc = 1 gold coin (gc)

10 gc = 1 platinum coin (pc)

So, 100 cc is equal to 1 gc.

The silver coin is the base of the economy and is what most day-to-day interactions use. If a denomination isn’t noted, it’s assumed to be sc.

Note that 100 coins is 1S

Superior Equipment

Not all equipment is built the same and, if you have some extra coin, it might benefit you to invest in some good quality gear.

Superior gear is always unique and even two +1 longswords can be different. This is done through Traits and they're like the Traits that your character has: they're used to describe the gear.

During a Confrontation, Combat, or Challenge, if that Trait applies, you can gain an extra bonus equal to its Rank to what is going on. This is in addition to the normal +2 for gear you get in a Confrontation and the reroll that you would normally get in a Skill Challenge.

So, for instance, let's say that you get some better armor. You ask to make it extra padded and that becomes its Trait: Extra Padded. A little while later, you find yourself in combat and you take a particularly nasty blow and decide that you don't want to die, and the Trait applies here so you add a temporary +1 to your armor's DR.

This, however, comes at a cost: when used this way in Combat or a Skill Challenge, your gear becomes a little more worn, gaining 1 Wear.

You can have additional Traits on a single item, increase the Rank of Traits you already have and/or add new ones (as long as they make sense and start at Rank 1).

Every time you do so adds an extra 50% the cost of the item. This is compounded and is calculated using the total Ranks of all Traits, so getting a +2 costs the same as 2 +1s.

So getting a Finely Tuned (+2) Mandolin would cost 135 sc ( ( 60 * 1.5 ) * 1.5) while getting one that is Finely Tuned (+1) and Inlaid (+1) would cost the same.

Wear

Wear represents how close your weapon is to breaking.

Simply put: if, on a Skill Check, Attack roll (for weapons), or Defense roll (for shields and armor), you roll equal to or below the Wear of that item on the unmodified d20, that item breaks and becomes functionality unusable until it’s repaired.

There are some specific cases that should be noted:

  1. You still maintain any penalties (Fatigue, Defense Modifiers from armor and shields, for example) that item gives you unless you discard it.
  2. In the case of weapons, you can still use the broken weapon - you just half all the damage it does.
  3. And finally, instead of having the item break, if it’s a Superior quality item, you can instead reduce one of its Traits by 1 as long as you have at least a Rank 1 in that Trait.

Repairing

Since the Maintenance Skill will only get you so far, you might need to remove Wear from your gear through blacksmithing. This is done through personally having the applicable Skill (Leather- or Metalworking, for example) or through contract work.

Hiring It Out

If you just hand off your gear to a blacksmith, the price for repairs is 10% the base Source price for the item (not factoring in the price increase for it being Superior if that's the case) per point of Wear and takes 1 day for every 2 sc the repairs cost.

Doing It Yourself

If personally done through the appropriate Skill, the cost for raw materials is 5% the base Source price for the item per point of Wear with the base time being 1 day for every 4 sc, however, two things to note:

The first is that you need to roll a Check vs d20! + the amount of Wear rounded to the next largest dice size (so, 4 Wear becomes a d6!) as the end of that time. Each Complication results in that amount of leftover Wear that will require twice the time and raw materials to fix (even if you then go to blacksmith) at which point you'll need to make another Check.

So, if you're repairing a glaive (base cost 49 sc) with 8 Wear on it. The cost per Wear is 2sc 4cc so the total for all 8 would be 19sc 2cc, which will take just about 5 days.

At the end of those 5 days you'd roll a Check vs d20!+d10!.

If you fail by 10 (2 Complications), that means that you were only able to repair 6 Wear, leaving 2 left over. To fix that it would be an additional 9sc 6cc (since it's 4sc 8cc this time per Wear) and take another 4 days or so.

Note that your total comes to 28sc 8cc where going to a blacksmith would have cost you 39sc 2cc.

The last thing to consider is that if you don't have a forge for metalworking, you'll have to rent one, which can range from 1cc to 1sc per day depending on how busy the forge (good luck trying to rent one at a military camp).

End Of Session Rewards

A person who sets off on an adventure doesn’t return the same person as they were when they left. Trials and hardships not only hone their skill, but also who they are and what they stand for.

In Bonfire, this change is measured by CrP and Honor, which are counted and rewarded at the end of every session.

Crp

The only way to gain CrP after character creation is to move towards your Goals or help others move towards theirs.

You don’t receive CrP for random things, such as winning a Confrontation, Combats, or Skill Challenges. Those are more about gaining Honor.

You gain CrP for challenging who your character is. If they gain CrP from a Combat, it is not from just defeating a random foe or just being in Combat. It could be from your character facing their fears or rival, killing a person for the first time, or learning to be a leader in a stressful situation.

Those things are a means to an end, not an end in and of themselves.

At the end of each session, if you've moved towards a Goal, whether your own or another player's, whether your character knew about it or not, and whether you did it "off-screen" as player or in-character or not, you gain 5 CrP. If you moved towards additional Goals, you get an extra 2 CrP for each one you helped with.

However, they only get the bonus CrP for helping another person if their Goals aren't identical to yours.

So, let's say that you moved towards one of your Goals and helped another person with theirs. You would get 7 CrP (5 + 2).

Or let's say that there wasn't enough time to do anything on your Goal, but you managed to help two other players; you'd still get 7 CrP at the end of the session (5 for the first, 2 for the second).

Or, or, if you were a real wunderkind, and moved towards your Goal AND helped the two other players, you'd get 9 CrP (5 + 2 + 2).

There is a bonus of another 5 CrP for each member if everyone in the party has moved towards their Goals that session.

Completing Goals

On top of this, if a party member actually completes one of their Goals, they (and anyone else who helps them) gains an additional 10% of their current total (spent and unspent CrP) as well as 5 Honor (though this only applies to the person who completed it). If multiple Goals were completed, you get an additional 5% bonus but no additional Honor.

Once a Goal is completed, it can't be taken again, with GMs having the final say on what a Goal can be.

The GM may award additional CrP for completing plot important points in the story.

Follower Experience

Followers don't gain CrP like normal characters: instead, getting half the CrP of their boss. They get no other CrP unless they're Proteges.

In this case, they’re treated like a normal PC in terms of gaining CrP with the expectation that the player only needs to move towards his or one of his protege’s Goals to count towards whether or not everyone has moved towards their Goals.

Additionally, a character can give up to 50% of their earned CrP from a session to their protege in forms of advice, training, and general guidance.

They do come at a cost and with a requirement. The requirement is that the candidate for protege must have a Devotion to the character. The cost is that they count as 2 followers towards your limit.

Once a follower has been declared a protege, they cannot go back to being a regular follower: they must either be killed, dismissed, or promoted to a full character.

Honor

At the end of each session, the players will elect each other’s characters for things they think that character deserved Honor for. It is generally poor form to elect yourself but is also reasonable if you pulled off something amazing and the rest of the group didn't seem to notice.

These elections need to fit into one of these three categories:

Strength

This character demonstrated strength based on how their character’s culture defines strength, which could be physical prowess but could also be cunning or grifting.

Mastery

This is given when a character solves a tricky problem or came up with a creative solution. Note, this shouldn't be given for good rolls unless the players want to lose Honor for bad rolls; this is about out of the box thinking.

Courage

When the chips were down, did your character give up the fight or did they really show what it means to be a hero and stare down death?

Roleplaying

Did the player really role play his character? Where his decisions about what he thought gave him the best chance to win or was it about what his character thought would be a good idea? Note, that since players are electing each other, jackasses who use “that’s just what my character would do” to dick over the party won’t get rewarded for this much. This should be specifically linked to a character's Confrontation Characteristics.

For each of these categories, the GM can also dock Honor from a character. Did they run away, or did they conveniently forget any Characteristics that would have screwed them over?

Gaining Honor Dice

Whenever you go up an Honor Category, you gain a free use of your Honor Dice. Also, when you reach 25 Honor, you get one as well, even though you didn't technically go up a category. Once used, they go away but they stay with your character as long as they live.

Certain Archetype abilities may also give you them temporarily, in which case, the Archetype will note for how long you have them.

Post Mortem Rewards

GMs can award the Ex and Honor that would have gone to a dead character that session (including bonuses for fullfilling their Goals based on the dead character's Ex) to the player's new character.

If you buy off a Flaw for an amount of CrPs equal to its original bonus and doing so gives you a Rank 10 Trait “Overcame X” with X being the Flaw. You can only buy off mental or behavioral Flaws unless there are unusual circumstances.

Background Flaws that have resolved through play can also give you this Trait according to the GM's discretion.

Training Time

Spending CrP requires 1 day per 10 CrP being spent (rounded up).

This self-reflection and training takes a large amount of the day. If the character is interrupted by any strenuous activity (gaining Stress and/or damage), they’ve lost the day and must start over.

They can also heal and gain Relaxation while doing this training as normal.

Skipping Training Time

You might be in a position where you’re unable to spend the required time to train.

If that’s the case, you can skip the time requirement by forfeiting 5 unspent CrP for each day of training you want to skip.

CrP spent this way is completely lost and doesn’t go towards your next level.

Healing & Relaxing

You heal and relax as normal during your training time.

Leveling Up

Step 1: Increase Stats

At 3rd total level and every 5 total levels after that (8, 13, 18, etc.), you may increase 1 of your Stats by 1

And at 5th total level and every 5 total levels after that (10, 15, 20, etc.), you increase a random Stat by 1. This is rolled on a d6, with 1 being Strength and 6 being Presence. You can't influence this with your Honor.

These can’t bring a stat above 23. If it does, you don't receive the bonus that level.

Step 2: Assess Characteristics

Traits

As a character grows, things that used to be important to them no longer are and things they never used to identify with become a part of them.

A character can switch around their Traits, refunding a number of temporary “Trait Points” equal to their Int and they can then use to increase other Traits.

These Trait Points go away at the end of this day of training: any leftovers are lost so spend them.

They may also swap out Trait at Rank 0 for an entirely new one.

After your 3rd total level, they may only do this swap once per level, however.

Before then, you may swap your Traits around freely: after all, you may still be getting on your feet in terms of the type of character that would best fit in the group and campaign.

Goals

During this time, the character carefully turns over their aims in life and can ask themselves if this is really the path they want to travel down.

Goals may be switched out, dropped or added freely at this stage but can't total more than 3.

Reputation

The GM will also adjust your Reputation and complete your Reputation sentences as she deems appropriate. She gets to add 1 point at this point, but she can also move as many existing points around as she wants. She may also change the ending to any Reputation sentences she wants.

She may also ‘free’ them up to distribute later: so, if your character moves far away where people haven’t heard of them, you could lose your Reputation, which might be good or bad.

Note to GMs: while you can complete the sentences at any time, giving the player a heads up as to where their Reputation is headed, it isn’t advised that you start putting points into the other two Reputations until the character reaches their 5th total level.

Step 4: Increase/replace Archetype Abilities

Check your selected Archetypes to see if they have any of their Abilities that either grow stronger (i.e., the Thug’s Precision Strike), gain additional free Skills (i.e., any of the Expert Archetype’s Skill Focuses), or gain something else for free (i.e., the Weird-Adept Archetypes gaining new Rudiments).

After that, if you’ve spent CrP to specialize into a new Archetype, you do so now, following the normal rules set forth in Chapter 3:Archetypes.

Step 6: Adjust Honor & Followers

Honor

Now the other players will have a chance to adjust your Honor by silently judging the character, rating him 1 to 10 in 3 categories: Flaws, Teamwork, Respect.

Flaws How well has this player played their Flaws? Do they only seem to be brought up when it’s convenient but then forgotten they need to get away with something?

Does she use her Flaws to further the story and character development, or does she use them to annoy others?

A character that leans more to the former, rather than the latter, deserves a higher score.

Teamwork Does this player work well with others? Do they pull their load and put themselves on the line or do they pick fights that derail the session? Are they on top of things and help set up or are they late and spend all their time on the phone?

Again, a character that leans more to the former, rather than the latter, deserves a higher score.

Respect How do you think this character is viewed by those he interacts with, by both the party’s character and NPCs? Has he kept his word (or at least, not gotten caught) or does he have a habit of backstabbing others?

Calculating the Bonus/Penalty The GM should then take the scores and average them across each category and then subtract 5 from each of them. Those three modified averages are then totaled: this is how much Honor they gain or lose.

For example, Claire receives scores of 5, 4, and 6 for her Flaws, 3, 5, 1 for her Teamwork, and 10, 8, 10 for Respect, giving her an average of 5 (0 after the -5) for Flaws, 3 (-2) for Teamwork, and 10 (5) for Respect.

Totaling the modified average, she gains 3 Honor

The individual scores and who gave them should be kept a secret, though the player can know how much they scored in each category to help them improve.

Followers

If you’ve reached your 10th total level, your GM should also roll to see if you’ve attracted a follower.

Learning Non-standard Abilities

Players who go out of their way to roleplay their character learning a new ability can get that ability irrespective of leveling, Archetype combination, and/or CrP spending, but only if they’ve taken some penalty for doing so (suffered for it) and only at the discretion of the GM.

Example: the fighter is realizing that he’s finding his faith as the adventure progresses. He’s actively doing things to gain Favor but beyond that, while the rest of the characters are relaxing and healing in town, he’s off at the local temple, learning about his patron Divine.

Because of this, while the other players get Relaxation, he’s forfeiting that so the next couple of adventures see him with a higher average Stress (and thus closer to having a Mental Break) than the rest of the party.

Additionally, he sought out priests of his cult and paid them to teach him, thus sacrificing his own coin on their lessons rather than spending it on repairs, fine foods, or new gear.

After a few sessions and adventures, he’s more than earned the Favored of the Divines ability that’s usually reserved for the Priest Archetype.

The rule of thumb is that a player must have gotten in at least 2d4!+2 situations where penalties they accrued in pursuit of their new ability have come into play. So, the fighter needs to go out on 5 adventures with his higher Stress for him to gain that ability.

You can adjust this as a GM: suppose the fighter got into a combat where his weapon broke due to him not repairing it and he nearly died because of it. In that case, you can count that as more than one.

The primary purpose of this is to dissuade players who are just trying to game the system and dipping here and there for specific abilities. This should be a reward for players who roleplay their character well and not for min-maxers who abuse the system so feel free to adjust these guidelines as you go for each individual player.

Who Tracks This?

The GM has a lot on her plate and a lot to track so it is on the players to track and advocate for getting this bonuses if they're actively seeking them out.

If you're looking for a particular ability, give your GM the heads-up before starting and discuss what you might do to get it. This will make life much easier for both of you.

Rewards Beyond Wondrous Items

Non-standard abilities can also be used as a reward in lieu of Wondrous Items. So, if you would give out a Relic or Occult Item, give out an ability instead, either through a divine boon or because of some talent a character has discovered they have.

Leveling Abilities

You can also give out leveling abilities but, once gained, the character will need to continue to sacrifice and train to increase them: they don’t level up automatically as they don’t come naturally to them, although this can be for a lesser cost.

If you don’t want to keep track of that (and I don’t blame you), you can have these abilities increase at half the rate as they normally would but still increase automatically at level up.

Made-up Abilities

Maybe you can’t find an Archetype ability that matches what your player is going for. A common example that I’m sure will come up for you is ambidexterity.

In this case, use the same rules but give them that ability.

So if a character is constantly using his off-hand in combat, giving him a heavy penalty, after 2d4!+2 combats, he’s gotten the hang of it and gains ambidexterity.

CrP Discounts For Skills

Beyond abilities, you can also use this to reward players by either discounting Skills or just giving them to them. So, a character who uses a weapon in a Combat Skill Suite they're not Trained in, can get that Skill Suite at a discount or for free at the next level up (or even during play).

Followers

While a character may use the Recruiting skill to find hirelings at any time, after a character reaches total level 10, they start having people seek them out. At that point, and at every level (or 2 for those with a Weird-Adept Archetype) level above that, the GM rolls on the Follower Attracted table found below but to give context to it, let's talk about the different types you can attract.

Follower Types

These come in three varieties:

Groupies

These are those that wish to swim in the wake of the big fish, primarily profiting off the other followers of the party and are often washerwoman, prostitutes, or errand boys. They don’t ask anything of the party but are happy to be hired for specialty jobs as long as they pay well.

They’re also the least loyal and they’ll never put their lives on the line for the party.

Hirelings

Hirelings make up the paid help. They often seek out the party directly and have a basic skill or two they can offer. The idea is that the party will help them further their craft and connections for a bit of pay. They’ll make themselves as helpful as possible - such as repairing armor and weapons for the cost of room and board.

Hirelings won’t break contracts unless the captain of the party falls into Low Honor and will be more loyal than groupies, however, they’ll only risk their lives if it’s in their contract to do so.

Retainers

These are those that could be full adventurers on their own but has hitched their wagon to your train in hopes of getting more. They’re there out of loyalty but they do demand a greater share of the loot due to the fact that they bring more to the table.

These are, essentially, lower-level characters that you can run.

Sidekicks

Sidekicks are something special. A Sidekick is a person who is completely loyal to a character. They would lay down their lives for a character. A character has a limited number of Sidekicks that he can call upon over the course of his whole lifetime.

Sidekicks are childhood friends, people who deify the character, or other such unquestionably loyal followers so you only get 1 in their entire life.

A Sidekicks is essentially a second Player Character that the player gets to run.

Any new follower can be declared by the player to be a Sidekick, having sought out the party, he is recognized as an old friend or a new friend who swears undying loyalty to the character. Additionally, a follower can be elevated to the status of a Sidekick if he or the character saves the other’s life.

Even before total level 10, a character can search out his Sidekick, however, they must be old friends from his past in this case.

Only severe abuse can break the bond between the hero and his Sidekick, but should this happen, the Sidekick becomes an enemy of equivalent zeal.
